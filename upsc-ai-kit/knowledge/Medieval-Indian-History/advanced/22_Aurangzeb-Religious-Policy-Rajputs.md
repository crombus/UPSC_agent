## 1. Snapshot & core idea

**Deeper — Chandra's balanced frame: orthodoxy plus statecraft failure.**

✅ Satish Chandra explicitly treats Aurangzeb's character as **complex**: an orthodox ruler who wanted to remain within Islamic law, but also a Timurid sovereign who could use secular decrees (*zawabit*) and choose between legal opinions when raison d'etat required it.
- ✅ His puritanical measures — bans on intoxicants, closer association with ulama, reimposition of **jizyah**, and restrictions affecting temples — failed to rally Muslims or create deeper loyalty to an Islamic-law state.
- ✅ Chandra highlights a contradiction: Aurangzeb formally accepted the *sharai* principle protecting old temples of *zimmis*, yet permitted destruction of many old-standing temples before 1687. Neither blanket exoneration nor a claim of uniform empire-wide destruction fits the evidence.
- ✅ Chandra notes corruption and grasping behaviour among some ulama/qazis appointed in the jizyah apparatus, weakening the political utility of this policy.
- ✅ The reimposition of jizyah alienated sections of Hindus and strengthened groups already opposed to the empire, but Chandra also says religion by itself was not the point at issue in the empire's decline.

**Deeper — Marwar-Mewar as a succession crisis mishandled by empire.**

✅ Part II uses newly available sources such as **Waqa-i-Ajmer** and **Jodhpur Hukumat-ri-Bahi** to revise older explanations. Taking Marwar into **khalisa** after Jaswant Singh's death had precedents when succession was disputed and debts were owed.
- ✅ The crisis escalated because Mughal officials behaved as conquerors, because Aurangzeb awarded the tika to **Inder Singh**, and because the Rathor sardars rejected the proposed division/settlement involving Ajit Singh.
- ✅ Rana Raj Singh of Mewar was alienated by Mughal interference in Rajput internal affairs and the military occupation of Marwar.
- ✅ Chandra's conclusion is sharp: Aurangzeb's Marwar-Mewar policy was **clumsy and blundering**, yielded no advantage, damaged prestige, and created doubts about Mughal support to old allies.

## 4. Must-Know Facts (Prelims)

- ✅ Aurangzeb reimposed **jizyah in 1679**; it was abolished again soon after his death.
- ✅ **Jaswant Singh** died in 1678; **Ajit Singh** was his posthumous claimant; **Durgadas Rathor** led resistance.
- ✅ **Inder Singh** was initially favoured by Aurangzeb for the Jodhpur tika.
- ✅ Mewar's Rana Raj Singh objected to Mughal interference and occupation of Marwar.
- ✅ Prince **Akbar** rebelled in 1681 with Rathor support but failed.
- ✅ Chandra rejects both a pure strategic-annexation view and a pure religious-conspiracy view of Marwar.

## 5. UPSC Traps

> 🔑 Trap: Do not confuse **religious orthodoxy** with a monocausal explanation of all rebellions and decline.

- ❌ Aurangzeb simply abandoned all Timurid/secular statecraft. → Correct: he still used political discretion and *zawabit*.
- ❌ Taking Marwar into khalisa automatically meant final annexation. → Correct: khalisa control had precedents in disputed successions.
- ❌ Jizyah successfully rallied Muslims behind the empire. → Correct: Chandra says it neither rallied Muslims effectively nor checked decline.
- ❌ Rajput rupture caused immediate military collapse. → Correct: military effect was limited after 1681, but prestige and trust suffered deeply.
