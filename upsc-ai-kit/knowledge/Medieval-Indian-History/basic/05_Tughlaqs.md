## 1. Snapshot & core idea

**Foundation — Muhammad bin Tughlaq: ambitious state-building, weak execution.**

✅ Muhammad bin Tughlaq is presented by Satish Chandra as a remarkable ruler: learned in religion, philosophy and sciences, open to Muslim mystics, Hindu yogis and Jain saints, and willing to appoint officials by merit rather than only noble birth.
- ✅ His weakness was haste and impatience: many experiments were bold in design but failed in implementation, earning him the label of an "ill-starred idealist."
- ✅ **Daulatabad:** Deogir was renamed Daulatabad and used as a second capital to improve control over south India; officials, leading men and some Sufis were pressured to move, but Delhi was not emptied.
- ✅ **Token currency:** copper and brass coins were assigned the value of precious-metal currency; the scheme failed because unrestricted private imitation and weak verification destroyed confidence.
- ✅ **Agrarian policy:** in the doab, over-assessment, artificial prices and famine produced distress; later the **diwan-i-amir-i-Kohi** tried to extend cultivation through loans, but officials misused funds.

**Foundation — Firuz Shah: benevolence plus long-term weakness.**

✅ Firuz Shah Tughlaq tried to prevent further break-up by appeasing nobles, soldiers and theologians, while avoiding hard-to-control regions such as the Deccan.
- ✅ He failed to recover Bengal but retained a large north Indian Sultanate; his reign was remembered for peace and quiet development rather than military brilliance.
- ✅ He made offices and **iqtas** more hereditary, stopped harsh audit-torture of nobles, and extended heredity to soldiers — measures that bought peace but weakened central control.
- ✅ He built canals, founded towns such as Hissar-Firuzah and Firuzabad, set up hospitals, gave dowries to poor girls, and created a public works department.
- ✅ He collected about **1,80,000 slaves**, using some in karkhanas and some as a personal bodyguard; after his death the slave corps became a political problem.

## 4. Must-Know Facts (Prelims)

- ✅ Muhammad bin Tughlaq ruled **1325–51**; Firuz Shah ruled **1351–88**.
- ✅ Daulatabad was **Deogir**, not a new city; it was intended as a second capital for southern control.
- ✅ Delhi was **not completely emptied** during the Daulatabad move.
- ✅ Token currency failed mainly because forged coins flooded the market.
- ✅ **Diwan-i-amir-i-Kohi** was the agricultural development department.
- ✅ Firuz dug canals, founded towns, built hospitals and promoted public works.
- ✅ Firuz made offices and iqtas hereditary, weakening the Sultanate's central machinery.
- ✅ Timur's invasion came after Firuz's death, when the Sultanate was already weakened.

## 5. UPSC Traps

> 🔑 Trap: Muhammad bin Tughlaq was not simply "mad"; Satish Chandra treats him as intelligent and innovative, but administratively impatient.

- ❌ Daulatabad was made the only capital. → It functioned as a **second capital**; Delhi remained populated.
- ❌ Token currency failed because paper money was unknown in the world. → Token currency existed elsewhere; failure in India came from **forgery and weak enforcement**.
- ❌ Firuz was strong because his reign was peaceful. → Peace came partly by **appeasing elites**, which weakened the centre.
- ❌ Firuz's welfare measures made the state modern. → They were benevolent but socially limited and tied to medieval privilege.

## 7. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Muhammad bin Tughlaq shows the difference between **administrative imagination** and **state capacity**.
- ⚠️ Firuz Shah's reign can be assessed as a welfare-oriented but politically decentralising phase.
- ⚠️ The Tughlaqs reveal the core Sultanate dilemma: expansion beyond administrative reach produced regional breakaways.
