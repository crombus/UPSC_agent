**Core proposition:** ⚠️ Communalism functions as a form of social-political mobilisation
around religious identity, not merely a religious phenomenon, and communal violence follows
a traceable sociological sequence — rumour, polarisation, physical segregation, then
violence — that institutional impartiality and rumour-control mechanisms can interrupt if
applied early and consistently.

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Communalism** | The use of religious community identity as a basis for political and social mobilisation, often asserting that community's interests are separate from, or opposed to, other communities' interests. |
| ⚠️ **Majority communalism** | Mobilisation by a numerically or politically dominant religious group asserting primacy or special claims over public space, symbols or policy. |
| ⚠️ **Minority communalism** | Defensive or assertive mobilisation by a minority religious group around perceived threats to its identity, security or religious practice. |
| ⚠️ **Separatist communalism** | Communal mobilisation escalating into demands for separate political or territorial status, historically the most extreme form. |
| ⚠️ **Riot sociology (rumour-polarisation-segregation sequence)** | The observed sequence by which localised incidents, amplified by rumour, produce inter-community polarisation and physical/residential segregation, which in turn raises the likelihood of organised or spontaneous violence. |

## 3. How communalism functions socially

1. **Competitive mobilisation:** ✅ political and social actors can mobilise religious
   identity to build a voting bloc, assert symbolic dominance over public space, or resist
   perceived marginalisation, converting religious identity into a politically salient
   category.
2. **Historical grievance as fuel:** ⚠️ real or perceived historical grievances (contested
   religious sites, past violence, unequal development) provide durable material for
   communal mobilisation to draw upon repeatedly.
3. **Rumour as the trigger mechanism:** ✅ localised incidents (a personal dispute, a minor
   altercation) can be rapidly reframed through rumour into a community-versus-community
   narrative, especially where formal information channels are slow or distrusted.
4. **Polarisation and segregation as escalation:** ⚠️ once rumour-driven polarisation sets
   in, residential and social segregation along religious lines tends to increase, reducing
   routine inter-community contact and further weakening mutual trust.
5. **Violence as the escalation endpoint:** ⚠️ where polarisation and segregation are
   sufficiently advanced, organised or spontaneous violence becomes more likely, particularly
   where local institutions are perceived as partisan rather than neutral.
6. **Institutional impartiality as trust repair:** ⚠️ visible, consistent institutional
   neutrality — even-handed policing, prompt and fair investigation, transparent
   administration — is the primary sociological lever for rebuilding inter-community trust
   after a communal incident; perceived institutional bias instead entrenches segregation
   and future mobilisation potential.

## 6. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ Communalism is a social-political mobilisation phenomenon organised around religious
  identity, not a description of religious belief itself.
- ✅ Majority, minority and separatist communalism are the three standard typological forms.
- ✅ The Places of Worship (Special Provisions) Act, 1991 freezes the religious character of
  places of worship as of 15 August 1947, with the Ayodhya dispute specifically excluded
  from its scope.
- 📰 The Act's status remains sub judice as of 17 July 2026, with ongoing Supreme Court
  litigation; no final verdict has been delivered.
- ⚠️ Rumour-polarisation-segregation is the standard sociological sequence explaining
  escalation from localised incident to communal violence.

## 7. UPSC traps

- ❌ Communalism is simply intense religious belief or piety. -> It is a political-social
  mobilisation phenomenon using religious identity as an organising axis, analytically
  distinct from personal religious devotion.
- ❌ Majority and minority communalism are morally or sociologically identical phenomena. ->
  They differ in their social position and typical motivation (assertion of primacy versus
  defensive/assertive response to perceived threat), though both are communalism in the
  technical sense.
- ❌ The Places of Worship Act, 1991 has been struck down or fully upheld with finality. -> As
  of 17 July 2026, litigation remains pending before the Supreme Court; no final verdict has
  been delivered, and the Act remains in force.
- ❌ Communal riots are spontaneous, unpredictable events with no traceable social pattern.
  -> Riot sociology identifies a traceable rumour-polarisation-segregation-violence
  sequence that can, in principle, be interrupted at an early stage.
