**Core proposition:** ✅ VisionIAS, citing UNDRR, defines a disaster as "a
serious disruption to the functioning of a community, which causes
human, material, economic and environmental losses beyond a community's
ability to cope," resulting from "the combination of hazards, conditions
of vulnerability and insufficient capacity or measures to reduce the
potential negative consequences of risk" (PDF p. 4).

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Hazard** | "A dangerous condition or event, that threat or have the potential for causing injury to life or damage to property or the environment" — natural (geophysical, hydrological, climatological, meteorological, biological) or anthropogenic (PDF p. 4). |
| ✅ **Vulnerability** | "Conditions determined by physical, social, economic, and environmental factors or processes, which increase the susceptibility of a community to the impact of hazards" — economic, physical, social, environmental and attitudinal forms (PDF pp. 4-5). |
| ✅ **Risk** | "A measure of the expected losses due to a hazard event occurring in a given area over a specific time period"; working relation "Risk = Probability of Hazard x Degree of Vulnerability" (PDF p. 5). |
| ✅ **Risk acceptance/avoidance/reduction/transfer** | Four distinct ways of dealing with risk — informed acceptance of consequences; avoiding risk-generating activity; applying techniques to reduce likelihood/consequence; and shifting the burden (e.g. insurance) to another party (PDF p. 5). |
| ✅ **Rapid-onset vs. slow-onset disaster** | Rapid onset (earthquake, cyclone, flood, tsunami) has sudden, acute impact; slow onset — "Creeping Emergencies" (climate change, desertification, soil degradation, drought) — unfolds over months or years and must be reflected in preparedness planning (PDF p. 6). |
| ⚠️ **Resilience** | Not source-defined in a single VisionIAS line but built from its cycle/DRR material as the capacity of a system to resist, absorb, accommodate to and recover from a hazard's effects in a timely and efficient manner — the concept directly asked in 2024 Q17. |

## 3. Risk/problem mechanism

1. **Hazard occurs** — natural (geophysical, hydrological, climatological,
   meteorological, biological) or anthropogenic (pollution, deforestation,
   chemical spillage) (PDF p. 4).
2. **Exposure and vulnerability determine impact** — ✅ VisionIAS
   distinguishes economic (income diversity, access to production means),
   physical (proximity, construction type), social (poor, pregnant/
   lactating women, disabled, children, elderly), environmental
   (ecosystem sensitivity) and attitudinal (community response to change)
   vulnerability (PDF pp. 4-5).
3. **Capacity determines whether hazard + vulnerability becomes a
   disaster** — insufficient capacity or risk-reduction measures convert
   a hazard event into "a serious disruption... beyond a community's
   ability to cope" (PDF p. 4).
4. **Classification routes the response** — ✅ by source (natural,
   socio-natural, anthropogenic) and by duration (rapid onset vs. slow
   onset) (PDF pp. 5-6); each classification is developed hazard-by-
   hazard in topics `05-13`.

## 4. Disaster-management cycle application

✅ VisionIAS structures the cycle into a pre-disaster **Risk Management
Phase** (prevention, mitigation, preparedness) and post-disaster **Crisis
Management Phase** (relief, response, rehabilitation, reconstruction,
recovery) (PDF p. 6), replacing the traditional "3 Rs" (Rescue, Relief,
Restoration) with "3 Ps" (Prevention, Preparedness, Proofing) (PDF p. 6).
✅ Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR) is "the concept and practice of reducing
disaster risks through reduced exposure to hazards, lessened
vulnerability of people and property, wise management of land and the
environment, and improved preparedness" and comprises a policy
framework, risk assessment, risk awareness, plan implementation, early
warning systems and use of knowledge (PDF p. 8). See `00_Master-
Framework.md` Section 2 for the full cycle table reused across every
hazard topic.

## 5. Institutions and policy tools

- ✅ **Ten-point PM agenda on DRR (AMCDRR, New Delhi, November 2016):**
  each point is explicitly aligned with one or more Sendai priorities of
  action (PDF p. 8) — the Indian policy bridge between the DM cycle and
  the global framework.
- ✅ **Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030:** adopted
  at the Third UN World Conference on DRR, Sendai, Japan, 2015; successor
  to the Hyogo Framework for Action (2005-2015); "first major agreement
  of the post-2015 development agenda," with **four priorities** and
  **seven global targets** (PDF p. 62).
- ✅ **National Disaster Management Plan, 2016** incorporates the four
  Sendai priorities into five Thematic Areas for Action — Understanding
  Risk, Inter-Agency Coordination, Investing in DRR (structural),
  Investing in DRR (non-structural), and Capacity Development (PDF p.
  14) — developed fully in topic `02`.

## 6. India applications and examples

- ✅ VisionIAS flags the NDMP 2016's own shortcomings: it is "too generic,"
  lacks a precise time frame, does not project fund requirements, and —
  unlike Sendai or the SDGs — "does not set any goals or targets" (PDF p.
  14, cross-referenced PDF p. 16). 📰 NDMP 2016 was India's first
  edition; the **revised NDMP, 2019** is the latest published National
  Disaster Management Plan and should be cited as the operative plan,
  not the 2016 edition. ⚠️ This is the direct evidentiary basis for a
  Mains answer contrasting India's domestic plan with Sendai's own
  quantified targets.
- ⚠️ **PYQ mapping:** 2024 Q17 directly asks "What is disaster
  resilience? How is it determined? Describe various elements of a
  resilience framework. Also mention the global targests of Sendai
  Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (2015-2030)" (`local paper text
  reproduced verbatim`, 250 words, 15 marks) — the single most directly
  relevant PYQ in this entire folder.

## 7. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ Risk = Probability of Hazard x Degree of Vulnerability (VisionIAS
  working relation, PDF p. 5).
- ✅ Four ways of dealing with risk: acceptance, avoidance, reduction,
  transfer (PDF p. 5).
- ✅ Sendai Framework (2015-2030) has four priorities and seven global
  targets; it succeeded the Hyogo Framework for Action (2005-2015),
  which had five priorities (PDF pp. 61-62).
- ✅ The PM's ten-point DRR agenda was announced at AMCDRR, New Delhi,
  November 2016 (PDF p. 8).
- ⚠️ Rapid-onset disasters (earthquake, cyclone, flood, tsunami) versus
  slow-onset/"creeping emergencies" (drought, desertification, climate
  change) is a repeatedly tested classification (PDF p. 6).

## 8. ❌ Traps and stale-claim cautions

- ❌ Sendai Framework is a binding international treaty. -> It is a
  voluntary, non-binding framework agreed at a UN World Conference; do
  not describe it as creating enforceable legal obligations.
- ❌ Resilience simply means "bouncing back" to the pre-disaster state.
  -> A complete resilience framework (asked directly in 2024 Q17)
  requires naming specific elements — risk knowledge, robustness,
  redundancy, resourcefulness, rapid recovery and the capacity to adapt/
  transform, not identical restoration of the prior (possibly
  risk-generating) state.
- ❌ The NDMP 2016 is still the latest or only National Disaster
  Management Plan. -> NDMP 2016 was only the first edition; the
  **revised NDMP, 2019** is the latest published National Disaster
  Management Plan and is the correct plan to cite as operative.
- ❌ "Risk reduction" and "risk transfer" are interchangeable. -> The
  source explicitly separates risk reduction (lowering likelihood/
  consequence) from risk transfer (shifting the burden, e.g. insurance)
  as distinct strategies (PDF p. 5) — developed fully in topic `16`.

## 9. 📰 Current official anchor

- 📰 The Sendai Framework's implementation status is authoritatively
  tracked by **UNDRR**, whose **Sendai Framework Midterm Review Political
  Declaration (2023)** is the correct dated anchor for any claim about
  global progress against the seven targets — do not cite VisionIAS's
  2015-era description of the targets as a current-progress statement.

## 11. Mains framework

1. Define the specific risk in hazard-exposure-vulnerability-capacity
   terms (Section 3), never as a generic "disasters are increasing"
   statement.
2. Route the answer through the disaster management cycle (Section 4),
   naming the specific phase engaged by the question.
3. Where the question invokes global commitments, name Sendai's four
   priorities precisely, not a vague "international cooperation."
4. Where "resilience" is asked, name specific framework elements
   (knowledge, robustness, redundancy, resourcefulness, rapid recovery),
   not a one-line definition alone.
5. Close by relating the specific hazard/answer back to India's own
   plan (NDMP) and its acknowledged gaps (Section 6), showing awareness
   of both the global standard and the domestic shortfall.
