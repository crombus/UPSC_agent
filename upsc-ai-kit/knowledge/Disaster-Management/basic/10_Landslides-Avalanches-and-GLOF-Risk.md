
**Core proposition:** ✅ VisionIAS defines a landslide as "the movement
of a mass of rock, debris, or earth down a slope" — a form of "mass
wasting" — affecting "at least 15 per cent of the land area" of India,
driven fundamentally by the Indian plate's continuous northward
movement (~5 cm/year) against the Eurasian plate rendering Himalayan
rock "friable, weak and prone to landslides and earthquakes" (PDF p.
31). ⚠️ An avalanche — the rapid down-slope movement of a mass of snow
and ice — is a mechanistically distinct Himalayan mass-movement hazard,
governed by snowpack, slope and weather conditions rather than rock/
debris movement, and is monitored/warned by the Defence Geoinformatics
Research Establishment (DGRE), not treated as a sub-type of landslide.

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Landslide** | Down-slope movement of a mass of rock, debris or earth under gravity — a form of mass wasting (PDF p. 31). |
| ✅ **Cloudburst** | Extremely fast, localised heavy rainfall — around 10 cm or more per hour over roughly 20-30 sq km — caused by saturated clouds unable to release rain due to strong upward warm-air currents, until raindrops become too heavy and fall together suddenly (PDF p. 33). |
| ✅ **Landslide vulnerability zones** | Very High (young Himalayan/Andaman-Nicobar mountains, high-rainfall steep-slope Western Ghats/Nilgiris/North-East, frequent-earthquake zones, intense human-activity areas); High (similar factors, lower intensity/frequency, excluding Assam plains); Moderate/Low (Trans-Himalayan Ladakh/Spiti, Aravali, rain-shadow Western/Eastern Ghats, Deccan plateau; also mining/subsidence-linked landslides in Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, MP, Maharashtra, AP, Karnataka, TN, Goa, Kerala); Other/safe areas (Rajasthan, Haryana, UP, Bihar, most of West Bengal/Assam, southern coastal regions) (PDF pp. 31-32). |
| ⚠️ **Avalanche** | A rapid down-slope movement of a mass of snow, ice and entrained debris, released when gravitational stress on an unstable snowpack exceeds its internal strength — triggered by a combination of snowpack structure (weak layers), slope angle (typically 30-45°), and weather (fresh snowfall load, temperature fluctuation, wind-loading); mechanistically distinct from a landslide (rock/debris, not primarily snow/ice) and from a GLOF (a lake-outburst flood, not a slope-failure mass movement), though all three are Himalayan mass-movement/cryosphere hazards. |
| ✅ **Glacial Lake Outburst Flood (GLOF)** | Sudden release of a large volume of water from a glacial lake, caused by the failure or overtopping of the natural dam impounding it — whether an ice dam, moraine dam, bedrock dam or other natural dam — releasing melt water, moraines and the lake's contents and causing catastrophic downstream flooding with major geomorphic/socio-economic impact (PDF pp. 26, 78). |

## 3. Risk/problem mechanism

1. **Landslide natural causes**: heavy rain (the main cause); earthquakes
   and volcanic explosions destabilising Himalayan rock (PDF p. 32).
2. **Landslide anthropogenic causes**: deforestation removing soil-
   binding vegetation; road construction dislodging rock structure and
   altering slope angles; shifting agriculture in the North-East; and
   house/hotel construction generating destabilising debris (PDF p. 32).
3. **Cloudburst mechanism and India-specific pattern**: hilly terrain
   promotes vertical warm-air currents that trigger cloudbursts most
   often when Bay-of-Bengal monsoon low-pressure clouds travel northward
   across the Ganga plains onto the Himalayas; a single disaster event
   (e.g. Uttarakhand 2013) can involve multiple cloudburst instances (PDF
   p. 33).
4. **Avalanche triggers**: ⚠️ a weak layer within an accumulated
   snowpack fails under added stress — fresh/wind-loaded snow,
   fluctuating temperature (freeze-thaw weakening bonds), rain-on-snow,
   or a steep slope (most avalanche-prone in the 30-45° range) — a
   distinct trigger-mechanism from both landslide (rock/soil/rain-driven)
   and GLOF (glacial-lake dam failure); concentrated in the high-
   Himalayan/Ladakh/Siachen snow-bound belt.
5. **GLOF triggers**: glacial retreat from global warming increasing the
   number/size of glacial lakes; rapid slope movement into the lake and
   ice-dam melting; anthropogenic radiative-balance alteration (mass
   tourism, roads, hydropower projects accelerating glacial melt); Black
   Carbon surface-heating impact; earthquakes; blocked subsurface
   outflow tunnels; and long-term dam degradation (PDF pp. 78-79).

## 4. Disaster-management cycle application

- ✅ **Landslide mitigation (NDMA guidelines)**: hazard-zone
  identification with specific-slide stabilisation, monitoring and
  early-warning systems at selected sites; area-specific measures;
  hazard mapping; restrictions on construction/roads/dams and large
  settlements in high-vulnerability zones; large-scale afforestation and
  water-flow-reducing bund construction; terrace farming replacing
  Jhumming in the North-East; retaining walls; excavation/construction/
  grading codes; landslide insurance/compensation arrangements (PDF pp.
  32-33).
- ✅ **Cloudburst mitigation**: NDMA has not issued cloudburst-specific
  guidelines — flood and landslide guidelines apply by extension;
  measures include settlement on hard rock/firm ground rather than
  valleys, vigilance for slope changes, banning indiscriminate
  construction in landslide-affected areas, surface/subsurface drainage
  planning, bioengineering (local vegetation plus engineering), and
  dense rain-gauge networks in vulnerable areas (PDF pp. 33-34).
- ⚠️ **Avalanche mitigation**: DGRE (Defence Geoinformatics Research
  Establishment, DRDO) issues avalanche warnings/forecasts for the
  Himalayan/Ladakh region using snowpack, slope and weather data;
  complementary measures include avalanche zoning of settlements/roads,
  defence/civilian route-closure protocols during high-risk periods, and
  protective structures (snow fences, deflection walls) — a distinct
  warning-and-mitigation chain from both landslide guidelines and GLOF
  monitoring.
- ✅ **GLOF mitigation**: early-warning access with continuous remote-
  sensing monitoring; dam remediation (artificial dams, tunnels, open
  cuts, concrete outflows, flood-protection walls); GLOF hazard mapping;
  identification of safe evacuation sites and vulnerable communities;
  community-based early-warning systems (PDF p. 79).

## 5. Institutions and policy tools

- ✅ **ISRO** — among the organisations engaged in monitoring glacial
  lakes and other water bodies in the Himalayan region's Indian river
  basins (PDF p. 79).
- ⚠️ **DGRE (Defence Geoinformatics Research Establishment, DRDO)** —
  India's dedicated avalanche-warning institution, issuing snow/
  avalanche forecasts and warnings for the Himalayan/Ladakh region based
  on snowpack, slope and weather monitoring; the correct institutional
  anchor for any avalanche-warning-specific claim, distinct from GSI's
  landslide-forecasting role and ISRO's glacial-lake monitoring role.
- ✅ **Copenhagen's cloudburst master plan** — cited as a useful
  international model, aiming to decouple 30-40% of excess stormwater
  from the combined sewer system through concretisation, canal
  creation and greening (PDF p. 34).

## 7. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ A cloudburst is classified at roughly 10 cm or more rainfall per
  hour over ~20-30 sq km.
- ✅ Landslides affect at least 15% of India's land area (document-period
  estimate).
- ✅ Very High vulnerability landslide zones include the Himalayas,
  Andaman and Nicobar, Western Ghats/Nilgiris and North-East India.
- ✅ Mining- and subsidence-linked landslides are concentrated in
  Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, MP, Maharashtra, AP, Karnataka, TN,
  Goa and Kerala.
- ✅ GLOFs result from the failure or overtopping of a glacial lake's
  natural dam — ice, moraine, bedrock or other natural dam — releasing
  melt water, moraines and the lake's contents.
- ⚠️ An avalanche is triggered by snowpack instability, slope angle
  (typically 30-45°) and weather (snow load, temperature, wind); DGRE
  (DRDO) is India's dedicated avalanche-warning institution for the
  Himalayan/Ladakh region.

## 8. ❌ Traps and stale-claim cautions

- ❌ Landslide, cloudburst, avalanche and GLOF are the same hazard. ->
  Each has a distinct mechanism: landslide (gravity-driven mass
  movement), cloudburst (extreme localised rainfall event), GLOF
  (sudden release from a glacial lake caused by failure/overtopping of
  its natural dam — ice, moraine, bedrock or other), avalanche (rapid
  down-slope movement of a snow/ice mass triggered by snowpack, slope
  and weather conditions — Section 2) — related but mechanistically
  distinct hazards.
- ❌ NDMA has issued dedicated national cloudburst guidelines. -> The
  source explicitly states NDMA "has not released guidelines
  specifically on Cloudbursts"; flood and landslide guidelines apply by
  extension (PDF p. 33).
- ❌ Cloudbursts can be reliably predicted with current technology. ->
  VisionIAS notes it is "very difficult to predict the cloud bursts due
  to its very small scale in space and time," even though IMD provides
  real-time rainfall situation/intensity and forecasts (PDF p. 34-35).
- ❌ The "15% of land area" and vulnerability-zone lists are current
  without verification. -> Treat as document-period estimates; verify
  against current GSI/NDMA sources.

## 11. Mains framework

1. **Name the specific hazard precisely** (landslide, cloudburst,
   avalanche, GLOF) rather than treating them as interchangeable
   (Section 8).
2. **Cite the specific vulnerability zone** relevant to the region asked
   about (Section 2), not a generic "the Himalayas are landslide-prone"
   statement.
3. **Distinguish natural from anthropogenic causes** (Section 3),
   naming specific anthropogenic drivers (mining, road construction,
   shifting agriculture) precisely.
4. **State honestly that cloudburst prediction remains very difficult**
   (Section 8) even with IMD's real-time monitoring capability.
5. **Close by linking landslide/GLOF risk to Himalayan development-risk
   tension** (topic `01`'s risk-creation loop) and multi-hazard
   cascading risk (topic `04`).
