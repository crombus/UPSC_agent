# GS-IV Ethics - Master Conceptual Framework

> **Purpose:** Connect every Ethics topic before studying the detailed files.
> **Core question:** How does a public servant convert personal values, institutional rules and
> constitutional morality into decisions that are lawful, impartial, transparent and in the
> public interest — especially when these pulls conflict?

---

## 1. The ethics stack: from person to system

```text
PERSON                    INSTITUTION                 STATE/SOCIETY
Values + Attitude            Rules, Codes,             Constitution, Law,
+ Aptitude + EQ    -->        Service Conduct   -->     Accountability and
(private character)          Norms, Probity            Anti-Corruption
                              (public conduct)          Architecture
        |                        |                            |
        v                        v                            v
   Ethical dilemma  ------>  Decision under      ------>  Outcome tested by
   (conflicting duties)      uncertainty/pressure         legality + probity +
                                                           public interest
```

**Core proposition:** ✅ ARC 1.5 — "values need to be sustained by institutions to be durable";
values alone or institutions alone are each insufficient. ⚠️ GS-IV therefore always tests the
*combination*: a value/attitude/thinker's idea (Papers such as 2024-2025 Mains show quotation-based
questions on Vivekananda, Kant, Thiruvalluvar, William James — see `02`, `07`) applied through an
institutional/legal lens (Nolan principles, codes, RTI, PC Act — see `09`, `15`, `16`, `19`).

## 3. The ARC's central diagnostic (dated 2007, still the exam's reasoning spine)

- ✅ ARC 1.4-1.5: two competing theories of corruption — "decline of values" vs "deviant minority
  responding to bad incentives." ARC's own position: **both values and institutions matter.**
- ✅ ARC 1.13: **coercive corruption** (citizen is an unwilling victim of extortion) vs
  **collusive corruption** (bribe-giver and bribe-taker jointly harm the public/state) — the
  single most tested distinction in the corruption-law space (see `19`).
- ✅ ARC 1.9: over-centralisation widens the "distance between authority and accountability."
- ✅ ARC 1.12: quality of politics is "perhaps the most important determinant" of societal
  integrity — corruption migrates to wherever competition/decentralisation has not reached.
- ⚠️ Exam application: almost every GS-IV theory question (why do good people act corruptly?
  why do systems fail honest officials?) can be answered by combining this values-vs-incentives
  diagnostic with a concrete Indian institutional example.

## 4. Recurring conceptual distinctions (exam traps live here)

| Distinction | Core difference | File |
|---|---|---|
| Code of Ethics vs Code of Conduct | Ethics = broad guiding principles; Conduct = precise list of dos/don'ts with sanctions | 09, 16 |
| Ethical dilemma vs legal violation | Dilemma = conflict between two legitimate duties/values; violation = breach of a settled rule/law | 09, 22 |
| Coercive vs collusive bribery | Coercive = citizen-victim under extortion; collusive = both parties gain, public/state loses | 19 |
| Accountability vs responsibility | Responsibility = duty owed going forward; accountability = answerability after the fact, to a specific forum | 11 |
| Corporate ethics vs CSR | Ethics = how business is done (means); CSR = discretionary giving-back (often ends-focused spend) | 12 |
| Probity vs integrity vs honesty | Honesty = truthfulness; integrity = consistency of values in action; probity = demonstrable, procedurally verifiable rectitude in public office | 14 |
| CVC/CBI/Lokpal jurisdiction | CVC advises/superintends; CBI investigates; Lokpal has overarching jurisdiction over Ministers, MPs and specified officials | 11, 20 |
| ARC recommendation vs enacted law | ARC 2007 recommended a Lokpal, whistleblower law, collusive-bribery offence; each has a distinct, separately dated legislative history | throughout |

## 5. Answer-writing spine used across every Mains-angle section

1. **Define/anchor** the core concept precisely (definition-first, not story-first).
2. **Locate the tension** — which two legitimate values, duties or institutions are pulling apart.
3. **Apply a named framework** — a thinker, moral theory or Nolan-type principle, explicitly.
4. **Ground in Indian administration** — a real, source-grounded example, case or provision.
5. **Resolve/recommend** — a decision or reform that is legal, proportionate, transparent and
   protects long-term public interest, not just the immediate optics.
6. **State the safeguard** — how misuse of your own recommendation would itself be checked.
