## 1. Architecture

```text
2005: RTI ACT                          2019: RTI (AMENDMENT) ACT
Fixed 5-year tenure (or age 65) for      Tenure, salary, service conditions of
CIC/ICs; salary/status equated to        CIC/ICs now prescribed by CENTRAL
Election Commissioners (statutory,       GOVERNMENT RULES, not fixed in the
Parliament-protected)                    Act itself (executive discretion)
        |                                        |
        v                                        v
   STRONG STATUTORY INDEPENDENCE    ->      CONTESTED INDEPENDENCE
   of the appellate/enforcement body         (critics: reduced insulation from
                                              executive influence; the 2019 Rules
                                              prescribe a 3-year term)
```

**Analytical claim:** The ethical strength of RTI's disclosure-by-default design depends critically
on the **independence of its enforcement body** (the Information Commissions) — the 2019 Amendment
is directly relevant to any advanced GS-IV answer because it shifts a previously statutory,
Parliament-fixed protection into executive rule-making discretion, a live institutional-independence
concern.

## 2. Concepts and distinctions

| Concept | Precise meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **RTI's disclosure architecture** | Section 4 mandates *proactive* suo motu disclosure by public authorities; Sections 6-8 govern citizen-initiated requests and specify exemptions (Section 8: national security, cabinet papers, commercial confidence, personal privacy, etc.). |
| ✅ **RTI (Amendment) Act, 2019 — precise change** | Removed the fixed five-year (or age-65) tenure and the statutory salary-equivalence (to Election Commissioners at the Centre, to the State Election Commissioner/Chief Secretary at the State level) for the CIC and Information Commissioners, replacing both with terms/conditions prescribed by Central Government rules. |
| ⚠️ **Institutional independence as an information-ethics variable** | Transparency law is only as strong as the independence of its adjudicating body; a datum-transparency mechanism (RTI) can be substantively weakened by process-level changes (appointment/tenure/salary control) without touching the citizen's formal "right" to request information at all. |
| ⚠️ **Information asymmetry and its ethical significance** | Bureaucratic discretion is partly a function of information asymmetry between official and citizen; RTI's ethical contribution is reducing this asymmetry, but its effectiveness is bounded by enforcement-body independence and citizen capacity (see `01`'s "asymmetry of power" framing). |

## 3. Detailed treatment

### The RTI (Amendment) Act, 2019 — a live institutional-independence case

- ✅ Pre-2019: the RTI Act fixed the CIC/Information Commissioners' tenure at five years (or age 65,
  whichever earlier) and equated their salary/status with the Election Commissioners (Centre) or
  State Election Commissioner/Chief Secretary (States) — both protections set directly in the
  Parliamentary statute.
- ✅ Post-2019: these protections were replaced by terms and conditions "as may be prescribed" by
  Central Government rules — meaning tenure, salary and allowances are now set by executive
  notification rather than fixed in the Act. 📰 Current rules (as independently verified) set a
  three-year term, and the change has drawn sustained criticism (from legal experts, former
  commissioners and transparency advocates) that it reduces the Information Commissions'
  institutional insulation from executive influence, including for State Information Commissions,
  whose service conditions are now also determined centrally rather than by the respective State
  Government.
- ⚠️ Advanced ethical reading: this is a textbook illustration of how **transparency law's
  substance can be hollowed out through process-level (appointment/tenure) changes** without
  amending the citizen-facing "right to information" provisions themselves — a caution for any
  Mains answer that treats RTI's continued existence as evidence of unchanged institutional strength.

### Proactive disclosure (Section 4) as the underused, most powerful transparency tool

- ✅ RTI Section 4 obligates public authorities to *proactively* publish specified categories of
  information (organisational structure, decision-making norms, budget, subsidy programmes) without
  waiting for a citizen request.
- ⚠️ Advanced point: Section 4 compliance has historically been uneven across departments; a strong
  reform recommendation is to make proactive disclosure the primary transparency channel (reducing
  citizen burden and RTI-application backlog) rather than relying predominantly on the slower,
  request-driven Section 6 mechanism.

### Transparency's limits — genuine exemptions vs default secrecy

- ✅ RTI Section 8 exemptions (national security, cabinet deliberation, commercial confidence,
  personal privacy, ongoing investigation) are legitimate and necessary — the ethical concern (per
  ARC's own Colloquium framing) is when exemptions are invoked as a *default* posture rather than a
  narrowly justified exception, effectively reintroducing an Official-Secrets-Act mindset through
  administrative practice even where RTI itself remains formally in force.
- ⚠️ A useful advanced test: does the withholding authority provide a specific, reasoned justification
  tied to a named Section 8 exemption, or a generic, unparticularised invocation of "public
  interest"? The former is legitimate exemption use; the latter risks default-secrecy practice.

### Transparency and probity — mutual reinforcement

- ⚠️ Transparency (this file) and probity (`14`) are mutually reinforcing but conceptually distinct:
  transparency is the *systemic condition* of information availability; probity is the *quality of
  conduct* that transparency makes verifiable — a fully transparent system can still reveal
  substantively improper conduct (transparency exposes, but does not by itself prevent, a probity
  failure); conversely, genuine probity is easier to sustain, and easier to prove, under conditions
  of transparency.

## 4. Institutional and reform architecture

- ✅ ARC's own recommendation (Colloquium annexure) that the Official Secrets Act, 1923 be repealed
  remains a dated 2007 recommendation; 📰 its current legislative status should be verified
  independently rather than assumed resolved.
- ⚠️ Restoring statutory (Parliament-fixed) tenure and salary protection for Information Commissioners
  — reversing the 2019 Amendment's executive-rule-making approach — is a frequently proposed reform
  to re-strengthen RTI's enforcement-body independence, directly relevant to "how to strengthen RTI"
  Mains answers.
- ⚠️ Expanding proactive (Section 4) disclosure obligations, backed by measurable compliance audits,
  would reduce reliance on the more litigious, backlog-prone individual-request mechanism.

## 5. Indian applications and boundary cases

- ⚠️ A department publishing its tender-evaluation criteria and scores proactively (Section 4-style)
  reduces both citizen RTI-filing burden and opportunities for post-hoc, selective justification of a
  contested award.
- ⚠️ Boundary case: an authority citing "commercial confidence" (Section 8(1)(d)) to withhold basic
  contract-value information from a completed, non-competitive-sensitive public works project
  illustrates exemption over-invocation — the ethical test above (specific, reasoned justification)
  would flag this as likely default-secrecy practice rather than legitimate exemption use.

## 6. Limitations and trade-offs

- ⚠️ Strengthening Information Commission independence (reversing 2019-style executive discretion)
  must be balanced against legitimate government interest in efficient, coordinated administrative
  appointments — the trade-off is institutional insulation versus administrative flexibility.
- ⚠️ Excessive expansion of proactive-disclosure obligations without corresponding administrative
  capacity can produce compliance-theatre (voluminous, low-value published data) rather than
  genuinely useful transparency — quality and usability of disclosed information matter as much as
  volume.
- ⚠️ RTI's citizen-capacity dependency (see basic file) means transparency reforms alone cannot fully
  substitute for civil-society capacity-building and legal-literacy investment.

## 7. Must-Know Facts for Advanced Prelims

- ✅ The RTI (Amendment) Act, 2019 removed the statutorily fixed tenure/salary protections for the
  CIC and Information Commissioners (Centre and States), replacing them with Central Government
  rule-making discretion.
- ✅ RTI Section 4 mandates proactive/suo motu disclosure; Sections 6-8 govern citizen-initiated
  requests and specify exemptions.
- 📰 Current rules under the 2019 Amendment set a three-year tenure for Information Commissioners
  (verify against the latest official notification before citing as fixed/permanent).

## 8. Advanced Prelims traps

- ❌ The RTI (Amendment) Act, 2019 abolished citizens' right to request information. -> It did not
  touch the citizen-facing right-to-request provisions; it changed the tenure/salary/service-
  condition protections of the Information Commissioners (the enforcement body), a distinct and more
  indirect institutional-independence concern.
- ❌ RTI exemptions under Section 8 are themselves controversial or new. -> The exemptions are a
  standard, necessary part of the original 2005 Act; the ethical concern is their *pattern of
  invocation* (narrow/justified vs default/generic), not their existence.
- ❌ Proactive (Section 4) disclosure has fully replaced citizen-request-based RTI in practice. ->
  Section 4 compliance remains uneven across departments; citizen-request-based RTI (Section 6)
  remains the dominant practical mechanism.

## 11. Mains-ready framework

**Central thesis:** Transparency's ethical power in Indian governance rests on RTI's disclosure-by-
default design, but that power is contingent on the continued institutional independence of its
enforcement body — a dependency the 2019 Amendment has directly tested — and on proactive disclosure
reducing reliance on citizen-capacity-dependent individual requests.

1. State RTI's default-reversal principle (disclosure-unless-justified) and its Section 4/6/8
   architecture.
2. Cite the 2019 Amendment's specific institutional change (tenure/salary via executive rules).
3. Assess the resulting independence concern and its implication for RTI's practical enforcement.
4. Recommend specific reforms (restore statutory tenure protection; expand proactive disclosure;
   invest in citizen legal-literacy/capacity).
5. Ground the discussion in one concrete case (MKSS/Parivartan, `23`) for evidentiary weight.
