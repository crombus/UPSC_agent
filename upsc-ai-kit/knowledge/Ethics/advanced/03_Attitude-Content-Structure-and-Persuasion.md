**Analytical claim:** In administrative ethics, the exam-relevant frontier is not "what is
attitude" but **where legitimate persuasion (public information campaigns, RTI-enabled advocacy)
ends and manipulation/propaganda begins**, and why a positive attitude does not reliably predict
ethical behaviour under institutional pressure.

## 2. Concepts and distinctions

| Concept | Precise meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Attitude-behaviour gap** (LaPiere's classic finding) | Stated attitudes and actual behaviour can diverge sharply under real situational pressure — a caution against assuming declared values predict conduct. |
| ✅ **Cognitive dissonance resolution paths** (Festinger) | (a) change attitude to match behaviour, (b) change future behaviour to match attitude, (c) add a justifying cognition (rationalisation) — the third path is the ethically risky one in administration. |
| ⚠️ **Persuasion vs manipulation vs propaganda** | Persuasion respects audience autonomy and uses truthful argument; manipulation exploits cognitive biases/asymmetric information; propaganda uses repetition/emotional framing at scale, often state-sponsored, to bypass critical evaluation. |
| ⚠️ **Attitude strength** | Attitudes held with high certainty, personal relevance and frequent rehearsal resist persuasion and predict behaviour better than weakly held attitudes — relevant to why some officials resist and others succumb to corrupt pressure. |

## 3. Detailed treatment

### The attitude-behaviour gap in administration

- ⚠️ An official may sincerely hold a pro-transparency attitude (cognitive + affective components
  intact) yet withhold information under superior pressure (behavioural component overridden) —
  this is a textbook attitude-behaviour gap, not hypocrisy per se.
- ⚠️ Institutional design (RTI's legal mandate, whistleblower protection — see `15`, `19`) exists
  precisely because persuasion/attitude alone cannot guarantee behaviour; a **binding external
  constraint** closes the gap that attitude alone leaves open.

### Cognitive dissonance as an ethical risk multiplier

- ✅ Festinger's third resolution path — adding a self-justifying cognition — explains why officials
  who begin with sound values can gradually rationalise small compromises ("everyone does it," "it's
  a victimless adjustment") into settled corrupt practice.
- ⚠️ Administrative counter-measure: regular ethics review, peer accountability and transparent
  audit trails interrupt the rationalisation pathway before it consolidates into habit.

### Persuasion at algorithmic scale — the 2025 social-media question

- ✅ 2025 GS-IV Q1(a) tested "key ethical dilemmas" of social media in "the present digital age."
- ⚠️ Advanced reading: social-media platforms operationalise the Hovland-Yale model at
  unprecedented scale and precision — the *audience* variable is now algorithmically segmented
  (micro-targeting), which can shade legitimate persuasion into manipulation by exploiting
  individual cognitive biases invisible to the audience itself.
- ⚠️ This raises a distinctive GS-IV dilemma: the same channel used for legitimate public
  information campaigns (vaccination uptake, tax compliance) can be used for disinformation and
  political manipulation — the ethical test is **transparency of intent and respect for audience
  autonomy**, not the technology itself.

## 4. Institutional and policy application

- ⚠️ Public information campaigns (financial literacy, sanitation behaviour change under Swachh
  Bharat, digital-payment adoption) are legitimate state-run persuasion exercises; their ethical
  legitimacy rests on truthful content and freedom from coercion, distinguishing them from
  propaganda.
- ⚠️ Civil-service training (Mission Karmayogi's behavioural-competency modules) targets attitude's
  cognitive and affective components through simulation/case-based learning, while institutional
  rules (codes of conduct, audit) close the residual attitude-behaviour gap.

## 5. Indian applications and boundary cases

- ⚠️ A district administration running a targeted, community-leader-led persuasion campaign to
  change attitudes toward girl-child education operates ethically because it is transparent, fact-
  based and non-coercive.
- ⚠️ Boundary case: a government social-media campaign that uses emotionally manipulative,
  fear-based messaging without disclosing sponsorship shifts from persuasion toward propaganda —
  an ethically contestable practice even if the underlying policy goal is legitimate.

## 6. Limitations and trade-offs

- ⚠️ Persuasion-based reform (attitude change) is slower and less certain than rule-based reform
  (binding institutional constraint), but more durable once internalised — a trade-off administrators
  must weigh when choosing between an awareness campaign and a mandatory regulation.
- ⚠️ Over-reliance on institutional constraint without attitude change risks purely compliance-based
  ("box-ticking") conduct that reverts once monitoring relaxes — echoing the code-of-ethics vs
  code-of-conduct distinction in `16`.

## 7. Must-Know Facts for Advanced Prelims

- ✅ LaPiere's classic 1930s study demonstrated a stark divergence between stated attitude and
  actual behaviour, founding the "attitude-behaviour gap" literature.
- ✅ Festinger's cognitive dissonance theory (1957) identifies three resolution paths: change
  attitude, change behaviour, or add a justifying cognition.
- ✅ The Hovland-Yale model's four persuasion variables are source, message, channel, and audience.

## 8. Advanced Prelims traps

- ❌ A strongly held attitude always predicts behaviour reliably. -> Attitude strength improves but
  does not guarantee prediction; situational and institutional pressures can still override it.
- ❌ Cognitive dissonance is always resolved by changing behaviour to match the ethical attitude. ->
  It is equally, and often more easily, resolved by rationalising the unethical behaviour instead —
  the ethically risky path administrators must guard against.
- ❌ Persuasion and manipulation are the same ethically. -> Persuasion preserves audience autonomy
  and relies on truthful argument; manipulation exploits bias or conceals intent.

## 11. Mains-ready framework

**Central thesis:** Attitude change through persuasion is necessary but insufficient for ethical
administration; durable conduct requires closing the attitude-behaviour gap through institutional
constraint, while distinguishing legitimate persuasion from manipulation/propaganda at scale.

1. Define attitude (ABC model) and its distinction from value/belief.
2. Diagnose which component (cognitive, affective, behavioural) the question's scenario targets.
3. Apply the Hovland-Yale persuasion variables to the specific channel/audience in question.
4. Flag the attitude-behaviour gap and the institutional constraint needed to close it.
5. Where relevant (social media, campaigns), draw the persuasion/manipulation/propaganda line
   explicitly using transparency-of-intent and audience-autonomy criteria.
