## 1. Architecture

```text
DEONTOLOGY (Kant)        CONSEQUENTIALISM (Mill)       VIRTUE ETHICS (Aristotle)
duty/rule-based           outcome/welfare-based          character/habituation-based
"was the ACT right          "was the OUTCOME               "would a PERSON OF GOOD
 in itself?"                 good on the whole?"             CHARACTER act this way?"
        \                          |                              /
         \                         |                             /
          v                        v                            v
                    RAWLS: justice as fairness —
           tests whether the INSTITUTION/RULE itself is fair,
           applied prior to any individual act evaluation
```

**Analytical claim:** GS-IV rewards candidates who recognise that these are not competing trivia
facts but **three genuinely different decision procedures** that can yield different answers to
the *same* dilemma — the sophisticated move is showing where they converge (reinforcing a
conclusion) or diverge (requiring a reasoned tie-break), and then adding Rawls to test whether the
underlying *rule/institution*, not just the isolated act, is just.

## 2. Concepts and distinctions

| Concept | Precise meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Kant's Categorical Imperative — two formulations** | (1) Universalisability: act only on a maxim you could will as universal law; (2) Humanity formula: treat persons always also as ends, never merely as means. |
| ✅ **Rule vs act utilitarianism** (Mill/Bentham lineage) | Act utilitarianism judges each act by its individual consequences; rule utilitarianism judges the *rule* an act follows by the consequences of everyone following that rule — the latter is closer to how public administration actually reasons (policy = a rule applied repeatedly). |
| ✅ **Aristotle's practical wisdom (phronesis)** | The capacity to judge *which* mean is appropriate in a *particular* situation — virtue ethics is therefore not a fixed formula but a trained, context-sensitive judgment, distinguishing it from both Kant's universal rule and Mill's outcome calculus. |
| ✅ **Rawls's two principles of justice** | (1) Equal basic liberties for all; (2) social/economic inequalities permitted only if attached to positions open to all (fair equality of opportunity) and to the benefit of the least advantaged (difference principle). |
| ⚠️ **Reflective equilibrium** (Rawls's method) | Moving back and forth between general principles and particular judgments until they cohere — a defensible method for a candidate reconciling a "gut reaction" to a case study with a stated ethical theory. |

## 3. Detailed comparative treatment

### Kant's quotation (2024 GS-IV Q3(c)) — deeper reading

- ✅ "In law, a man is guilty when he violates the rights of others. In ethics, he is guilty if he
  only thinks of doing so."
- ⚠️ Advanced reading: this operationalises the **legal-ethical guilt gap** central to `09`'s
  distinction between ethical dilemma and legal violation — a public servant who merely *intends*
  to favour a contractor (without completing an illegal act) has committed no crime, but has failed
  an ethical (Kantian) test of pure will/duty.
- ⚠️ Institutional implication: codes of ethics (aspirational, covering intention/disposition, see
  `16`) are needed precisely because codes of conduct (rule-based, covering completed acts) cannot
  reach the Kantian intention-level failure.

### Mill's liberty principle as a built-in constraint on utilitarian policy reasoning

- ✅ Mill's *On Liberty* (1859) argues state/social power over an individual is justified only to
  prevent harm to others, not merely to maximise aggregate welfare or enforce majority preference.
- ⚠️ Advanced application: a purely aggregate-welfare justification for coercive public-health or
  surveillance measures must additionally clear Mill's harm-principle test — utilitarian
  justification alone is necessary but not sufficient in a liberal-democratic administrative ethic.
- ⚠️ Rule utilitarianism resolves the classic "utilitarianism justifies punishing an innocent person
  to placate a mob" objection: judging the *rule* "punish only the guilty" by its long-run
  consequences (deterrence, trust in justice) yields a better aggregate outcome than judging the
  single act in isolation — a defensible answer to the standard anti-utilitarian objection.

### Aristotle's phronesis vs Kantian universalism — the context-sensitivity debate

- ⚠️ Kant's universalisability test can struggle with genuinely conflicting duties (truth-telling
  vs protecting an innocent person from harm); Aristotle's phronesis instead asks what a practically
  wise, virtuous administrator would judge *in this particular case*, admitting that mechanical
  rule-following sometimes fails.
- ⚠️ This tension is precisely why GS-IV case studies (see `22`) cannot be solved by a single
  formula — the case-study method's "framework evaluation" step explicitly borrows from all three
  traditions before reaching a judgment.

### Rawls — testing institutions, not just acts

- ✅ The veil of ignorance is a **design-stage** tool: applied *before* knowing one's position, to
  test whether a proposed rule/policy is fair to the worst-off participant.
- ⚠️ Administrative application: before finalising a reservation, subsidy or eligibility rule, ask
  "would this rule survive scrutiny by someone who did not know whether they would be born into the
  advantaged or disadvantaged group?" — this reframes distributive-justice debates (see `12`, `18`)
  away from majority-preference utilitarianism toward the difference principle's protection of the
  least advantaged.

## 4. Institutional and reform architecture

- ⚠️ Codes of ethics vs codes of conduct (`16`) map onto the Kantian intention/duty layer versus
  the completed-act/rule layer respectively — a structurally Kantian distinction embedded in Indian
  administrative-reform recommendations (ARC 2.2.6).
- ⚠️ Public-policy cost-benefit analysis is structurally rule-utilitarian; constitutional
  fundamental-rights review of that same policy is structurally Rawlsian/Kantian (rights as side-
  constraints, not simply weighed against aggregate benefit) — a useful frame for any GS-IV/GS-II
  overlap question on judicial review of welfare legislation.

## 5. Indian applications and boundary cases

- ⚠️ A cost-benefit-justified infrastructure project displacing a small tribal community tests all
  three theories differently: utilitarian calculus may favour the project (net aggregate benefit);
  Kantian/rights-based reasoning would require the displaced community's consent/adequate
  rehabilitation as a non-negotiable side-constraint, not merely a compensating cost; Rawlsian
  reasoning asks whether the project design itself benefits the least advantaged (the displaced
  community) or only the beneficiaries elsewhere.
- ⚠️ Boundary case: rule utilitarianism and Rawlsian fairness often converge in Indian welfare
  policy design (predictable rules protecting the worst-off tend to maximise long-run aggregate
  trust and welfare too) — a strong Mains answer can note this convergence rather than treating the
  theories as perpetually opposed.

## 6. Limitations and trade-offs

- ⚠️ Kantian universalism can generate rigid, situation-blind conclusions (e.g., an absolute
  truth-telling duty regardless of context) that practical administration sometimes cannot sustain
  without causing disproportionate harm.
- ⚠️ Utilitarian calculus is vulnerable to measurement problems (whose welfare counts, and how is it
  compared) and to justifying rights violations if the numbers are framed favourably — Mill's
  liberty principle is a partial, not complete, safeguard.
- ⚠️ Aristotelian virtue ethics, without codified rules, risks inconsistency across different
  officials' individual judgments of "what a virtuous person would do" — hence its pairing with
  codes of conduct (`16`) for consistency.
- ⚠️ Rawls's veil of ignorance is a powerful heuristic but assumes rational, self-interested
  choosers behind the veil — a simplifying assumption that itself requires justification in
  non-Western, communitarian contexts (a common critique applicable to Indian policy debates).

## 7. Must-Know Facts for Advanced Prelims

- ✅ Kant's two formulations of the Categorical Imperative are the universalisability formula and
  the humanity (means/ends) formula.
- ✅ Rule utilitarianism judges rules by their general consequences; act utilitarianism judges
  individual acts by their specific consequences — a key internal utilitarian distinction.
- ✅ Rawls's difference principle permits inequality only if it benefits the least advantaged group.

## 8. Advanced Prelims traps

- ❌ Kant's ethics has no place for consequences at all. -> Kant rejects consequences as the
  *justifying basis* of duty, but does not claim consequences are irrelevant to practical planning —
  the key claim is that duty cannot be *overridden* by favourable consequences.
- ❌ Rule utilitarianism and rule-based deontology are the same thing. -> Rule utilitarianism still
  justifies the rule by its consequences; deontology justifies the rule by its own inherent
  rightness (duty), independent of consequences.
- ❌ Rawls's veil of ignorance requires actual, real-world anonymity in policymaking. -> It is a
  hypothetical reasoning device for testing fairness, not a literal procedural requirement.

## 11. Mains-ready framework

**Central thesis:** GS-IV dilemmas are best resolved by explicitly running a scenario through
duty (Kant), consequence (Mill) and virtue/practical-wisdom (Aristotle) reasoning, then testing the
underlying rule's fairness through Rawls's veil of ignorance — not by invoking a single theory in
isolation.

1. State the dilemma precisely; identify the competing duties/values.
2. Apply Kant's universalisability/humanity test.
3. Apply Mill's aggregate-welfare and liberty-principle test.
4. Apply Aristotle's practical-wisdom/mean test for the specific context.
5. Where the dilemma concerns institutional design rather than one act, apply Rawls's veil of
   ignorance to the underlying rule.
6. State where the theories converge (reinforcing your recommendation) or diverge (justify your tie-break).
