**Analytical claim:** The advanced skill is not reciting the three theories but **stress-testing**
them against genuinely hard cases where they diverge, and showing methodological self-awareness
about which theory is doing the justificatory work in a given recommendation.

## 2. Concepts and distinctions

| Concept | Precise meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Trolley-problem-type divergence** | Classic thought experiments (redirecting harm to save more people) show utilitarianism permitting an act that deontology's side-constraints would forbid — the canonical illustration of genuine theoretical divergence, directly transferable to triage/priority-list dilemmas in disaster or health administration. |
| ✅ **Agent-relative vs agent-neutral reasons** | Deontology often gives agent-relative reasons (I must not personally violate this duty, even if someone else would produce a worse outcome by not violating it); consequentialism gives agent-neutral reasons (what matters is the best outcome, regardless of who brings it about). |
| ⚠️ **Moral luck** | The observation that outcome-based judgment (consequentialism) can hold agents responsible for factors partly outside their control (a reasonable decision that had a bad outcome due to chance) — a caution against purely consequentialist evaluation of a public servant's *good-faith* decision. |
| ⚠️ **Role-based/relational virtue** | Aristotelian virtue ethics, extended by care ethics and Indian dharma-based thought (`06`), recognises that the "right" virtuous action can depend on one's specific *role* (a district collector's duty differs from a private citizen's) — directly relevant to public-office-specific ethics. |

## 3. Detailed treatment

### Where the three theories genuinely diverge — administrative trolley problems

- ⚠️ **Triage during scarcity** (ventilators, vaccines, disaster relief with limited resources):
  utilitarianism supports maximising lives/welfare saved (possibly deprioritising the very old or
  those with lower survival probability); a rights-based deontological view resists any formula
  that treats a person's life as instrumentally less "valuable," insisting on procedural fairness
  (e.g., first-come or lottery) instead; virtue ethics asks what a compassionate, just administrator
  would judge, likely blending both — proportional priority by clinical urgency, softened by
  procedural fairness safeguards.
- ⚠️ This is precisely the kind of dilemma tested in GS-IV case studies involving resource
  allocation, disaster management or public-health prioritisation (see `22`).

### Moral luck and evaluating officials' good-faith decisions

- ⚠️ A procurement officer who followed due process and reasonable judgment, but whose contractor
  choice later performs badly due to unforeseeable market conditions, should not be judged solely
  by the bad outcome (pure consequentialist blame) — this is precisely the ARC's own concern (see
  `21`) that vigilance machinery must distinguish a "genuine commercial decision going wrong" from
  actual misconduct.
- ⚠️ Advanced point: this is a **deontological/virtue-ethics corrective to consequentialist
  administrative evaluation** — good governance requires judging the *process and intention*
  (duty/virtue) of a decision, not only its eventual outcome, precisely to protect honest risk-taking.

### Rule utilitarianism as the practical compromise in public administration

- ⚠️ Most actual public-administration ethics operates as **rule utilitarianism**: policies (rules)
  are judged by their aggregate long-run consequences, but *individual acts* within that policy
  framework are expected to follow the rule even in a specific case where breaking it might produce
  a marginally better local outcome — this preserves predictability, trust and non-arbitrariness
  (values central to `09`, `14`), which pure act-utilitarian discretion would undermine.
- ⚠️ This explains why "the rule was followed even though a better outcome was conceivable by
  breaking it" is often the ethically *and* institutionally correct case-study answer.

### Virtue ethics and role-specific duty (dharma parallel)

- ⚠️ Aristotle's phronesis, applied to public office, becomes role-specific: the practical wisdom
  expected of a district collector, a police officer or a judge differs because their institutional
  role and public trust differ — directly parallel to the Indian dharma concept of *svadharma*
  (duty appropriate to one's role/station), reinforcing `06`'s realist-idealist synthesis with a
  role-based virtue lens.

## 4. Institutional and reform architecture

- ⚠️ Codes of conduct (rule-bound, see `16`) are institutionally rule-utilitarian/deontological in
  form; codes of ethics (aspirational, character-oriented) are institutionally virtue-ethics in
  form — India's dual code architecture (ARC 2.2.6) can be explicitly mapped onto this theoretical
  distinction for an advanced answer.
- ⚠️ Vigilance/disciplinary mechanisms that distinguish process-integrity from outcome-quality
  (ARC Ch.7, see `21`) are an institutional embodiment of the moral-luck correction described above.

## 5. Indian applications and boundary cases

- ⚠️ A bank's internal advisory committee assessing whether a "vigilance angle" exists in a failed
  commercial decision (ARC 7.2, see `21`) is institutionally operationalising the deontological/
  virtue-ethics distinction between process-integrity and bad-luck outcome.
- ⚠️ Boundary case: a purely rule-utilitarian defence ("the policy overall does more good") cannot
  excuse a specific act that violates a fundamental-rights-level deontological floor (e.g.,
  non-discrimination) — Indian constitutional jurisprudence itself often functions as this floor,
  overriding aggregate-welfare policy justifications (a useful GS-II/GS-IV overlap point).

## 6. Limitations and trade-offs

- ⚠️ Deontological rigidity can produce administratively paralysing outcomes in genuine emergencies
  where competing duties cannot both be honoured — practical wisdom (virtue ethics) is needed to
  adjudicate, not a mechanical rule-application.
- ⚠️ Consequentialist calculation is vulnerable to manipulation through selective framing of "whose
  welfare counts" and over what time horizon — a risk in cost-benefit-justified displacement or
  environmental-clearance decisions (see `13`).
- ⚠️ Virtue ethics' reliance on "what a person of good character would do" can be circular or
  inconsistent across evaluators without codified criteria — hence its institutional pairing with
  explicit codes (`16`) and case-study training (`22`).

## 7. Must-Know Facts for Advanced Prelims

- ✅ The trolley-problem class of thought experiments is the standard illustration of genuine
  divergence between consequentialist and deontological reasoning.
- ✅ "Moral luck" describes the phenomenon of an agent being judged, unfairly on strict deontological/
  virtue-ethics grounds, by factors outside their control that affected the outcome.
- ✅ Rule utilitarianism, unlike act utilitarianism, judges the general rule (not the individual
  act) by its aggregate consequences.

## 8. Advanced Prelims traps

- ❌ Virtue ethics provides a fixed formula for every situation like deontology's universal rule. ->
  It relies on context-sensitive practical wisdom (phronesis), explicitly resisting a single fixed
  formula.
- ❌ Rule utilitarianism always produces the same conclusion as strict deontology. -> They can
  converge in practice (both favour predictable, rule-following conduct) but are justified by
  different reasoning (aggregate consequences vs inherent duty) and can diverge in edge cases.
- ❌ Moral luck means outcomes are irrelevant to administrative evaluation. -> It means outcomes
  alone should not be the *sole* basis for evaluating good-faith decisions; process and intention
  must also be assessed.

## 10. PYQ-based analytical application

- ✅ 2024 GS-IV Q1(a) (AI in administrative decision-making) rewards an answer that explicitly
  triangulates consequentialist efficiency claims against deontological fairness/dignity limits and
  virtue-ethics judgment about appropriate human oversight.
- ⚠️ Case-study answers (2025 GS-IV Q7) score higher when the candidate implicitly shows awareness of
  moral luck — evaluating the DC's decisions by the soundness of the process under uncertainty, not
  merely by the disaster's eventual outcome.

## 11. Mains-ready framework

**Central thesis:** A mature GS-IV answer treats the three theories as complementary diagnostic
lenses — deontology sets non-negotiable floors, consequentialism optimises within them, virtue
ethics/practical wisdom adjudicates genuine conflicts — while explicitly guarding against moral-luck
bias in evaluating officials' good-faith decisions.

1. Identify the dilemma and the theory each stakeholder/position implicitly relies on.
2. Apply the deontological floor test (any non-negotiable duty/right at stake?).
3. Apply the consequentialist optimisation test within that floor.
4. Apply the virtue-ethics/practical-wisdom test for the specific context and the official's role.
5. Explicitly flag any moral-luck risk in judging the outcome versus the decision process.
6. State the final, theory-triangulated recommendation and its safeguard against misuse.
