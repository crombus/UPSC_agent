```text
        COGNITIVE           AFFECTIVE            BEHAVIOURAL
      (belief/thought)   -> (feeling/emotion) -> (action tendency)
            |                    |                     |
            +------ the "ABC" (Rosenberg-Hovland) structure of attitude -----+
                                    |
                                    v
                    PERSUASION (source, message, channel, audience)
                                    |
                                    v
                    ATTITUDE CHANGE  <-->  COGNITIVE DISSONANCE
              (William James: "alter life by altering attitudes")
```

**Core proposition:** ✅ Attitude has three components — cognitive, affective, behavioural (the ABC
model) — and is distinct from **value** (broad, enduring, cross-situational) and from **belief**
(a single cognitive claim); attitude is the evaluative stance toward a *specific* object.

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Attitude** | A relatively stable evaluative disposition (positive/negative) toward a specific object, person, event or idea, comprising cognitive, affective and behavioural components. |
| ✅ **Cognitive component** | Beliefs/knowledge/information the person holds about the attitude object. |
| ✅ **Affective component** | The emotional charge (like/dislike, comfort/discomfort) attached to the object. |
| ✅ **Behavioural (conative) component** | The predisposition/intention to act in a particular way toward the object. |
| ✅ **Cognitive dissonance** (Festinger, 1957) | Psychological discomfort from holding two inconsistent cognitions/attitudes, or from acting against one's own attitude — resolved by changing the attitude, the behaviour, or rationalising the gap. |
| ⚠️ **Attitude vs value vs belief** | Value = general, enduring, cross-situational standard (e.g., honesty); attitude = evaluative stance toward one specific object (e.g., "I dislike this particular policy"); belief = a single factual/causal claim a person holds true. |

## 3. How attitudes form and change (mechanism)

1. Attitudes form through **direct experience, social learning (family/peers/media), and
   reinforcement** — repeated positive/negative outcomes attach evaluative charge to an object.
2. **Persuasion** changes attitude via four classical levers (Hovland-Yale model): the
   *communicator's* credibility, the *message's* content/framing, the *channel* used, and the
   *audience's* predispositions.
3. **Cognitive dissonance** is a powerful internal driver of attitude change: when behaviour and
   attitude conflict (e.g., a civil servant who values transparency is asked to withhold routine
   information), the discomfort pushes either attitude change, behaviour change, or self-justifying
   rationalisation — the last being an ethical risk in administration.
4. Attitudes influence but do **not mechanically determine** behaviour — situational constraints,
   social pressure and institutional incentives can produce behaviour inconsistent with a held
   attitude (the attitude-behaviour gap).
5. ✅ William James's teaching (2025 GS-IV Q3(b), verbatim: "a human being can alter his life by
   altering his attitudes") captures the *malleability* of attitude — it is not fixed temperament
   but a chosen, revisable stance.

## 4. Indian administrative applications and examples

- ⚠️ A field officer's negative attitude toward a beneficiary community (formed through biased
  social conditioning) can distort service delivery even where rules are neutral — training must
  target the affective, not only the cognitive, component.
- ⚠️ Persuasion principles explain why *credible source* + *clear message* + *appropriate channel*
  (e.g., a respected local leader delivering a vaccination message via a trusted local-language
  channel) shifts public attitude more than a purely top-down circular.
- ✅ 2025 GS-IV Q1(a): social media's "ethical dilemmas" in the "present digital age" is fundamentally
  an attitude/persuasion-at-scale problem — algorithmic amplification changes which messages reach
  which audiences, reshaping public attitudes outside institutional control.

## 5. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ The ABC model of attitude (cognitive, affective, behavioural) is associated with Rosenberg and
  Hovland.
- ✅ Cognitive dissonance theory was proposed by Leon Festinger (1957).
- ✅ The Hovland-Yale persuasion model identifies source, message, channel and audience as the four
  key persuasion variables.
- ⚠️ Attitude is specific-object-directed; value is general and cross-situational — a frequently
  tested distinction.

## 6. UPSC traps

- ❌ Attitude and value mean the same thing. -> Value is a broad, enduring standard; attitude is a
  specific evaluative stance toward a particular object — they are related but distinct levels.
- ❌ A positive attitude guarantees consistent ethical behaviour. -> Situational pressures and
  institutional incentives can produce an attitude-behaviour gap; attitude is necessary but not
  sufficient for ethical conduct.
- ❌ Persuasion is inherently unethical manipulation. -> Persuasion is a neutral influence process;
  its ethical status depends on the truthfulness of the message and the respect for audience
  autonomy, not on the act of persuading itself.

## 8. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Structure attitude-change answers as: define attitude (ABC) -> identify which component is
  weakest/targeted -> apply a persuasion lever (credible source, message framing, channel choice) ->
  give an Indian governance example -> note the attitude-behaviour gap as a limitation.
- ⚠️ For social-media/technology questions, explicitly note that persuasion at algorithmic scale
  changes the *audience* variable of the Hovland-Yale model — reach and targeting are now automated.

> **Answer thesis:** Attitude is a specific, three-component evaluative stance that is changeable
> through targeted persuasion, but its translation into consistent ethical behaviour requires
> institutional reinforcement, not persuasion alone.
