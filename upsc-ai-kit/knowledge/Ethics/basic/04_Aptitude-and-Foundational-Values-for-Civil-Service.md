  Integrity          | consistency of principle in action, resists inducement
  Impartiality/       | decisions on merit, not personal/political/familial bias

  Dedication to        | service motivation beyond minimum compliance

  Tolerance            | respect for diversity of views, faiths, communities

**Core proposition:** ⚠️ Aptitude is the *disposition* to acquire and apply these values under
real administrative pressure; the syllabus lists the values as if independent, but Mains questions
usually test them in combination (e.g., impartiality *and* compassion in a welfare-scheme dilemma).

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ⚠️ **Aptitude** | A natural or trainable capacity/inclination for a task or role — here, the disposition to perform public duties competently and ethically. |
| ✅ **Integrity** | Consistency between stated values and actual conduct, especially under temptation or unobserved conditions — the Nolan Committee (cited in ARC 2.2.5) lists it as a distinct principle from honesty. |
| ✅ **Impartiality/Objectivity** (Nolan) | Decisions on public appointments, contracts or benefits made strictly on merit, without favour to family, friends or political allies. |
| ⚠️ **Non-partisanship** | A civil servant's duty to serve the government of the day loyally and professionally regardless of personal political preference, and without allowing party political considerations to shape official advice or action. |
| ⚠️ **Dedication to public service** | Motivation to serve the public interest that exceeds the minimum required by rules — visible in responsiveness, initiative and proactive problem-solving. |
| ⚠️ **Empathy vs sympathy vs compassion** | Empathy = cognitively/emotionally understanding another's situation; sympathy = feeling *for* someone from the outside; compassion = empathy translated into a felt desire to alleviate suffering and corresponding action. |
| ⚠️ **Tolerance** | Respect for and acceptance of diversity of belief, culture and viewpoint, distinct from mere passive non-interference. |

## 3. How these values interact under pressure (mechanism)

1. **Discretion** (see `01`) creates the space in which integrity is tested — the less an official
   is observed, the more integrity (not compliance) determines the outcome.
2. **Impartiality and non-partisanship** are tested most sharply during political transitions,
   transfers/postings (see `21`) and election-related duties, where the pull toward favouring a
   patron competes with rule-bound objectivity.
3. **Compassion toward weaker sections** must be operationalised through *specific administrative
   design* (simplified forms, doorstep delivery, vernacular communication) — good intention alone
   does not reach a citizen who cannot navigate a complex bureaucratic process.
4. ✅ 2025 GS-IV Q5(b) frames the civil servant's foundational-values role explicitly as "enabler and
   active facilitator of growth rather than a regulator" for holistic development — implying that
   dedication to service today is measured by *outcomes enabled*, not just rules enforced.
5. ✅ 2024 GS-IV Q6(a) tests "gender-specific challenges faced by female public servants" and
   measures to increase their efficiency while maintaining "high standards of probity" — showing
   that foundational values (impartiality, dedication) must be examined institutionally, not just
   individually.

## 4. Indian applications and examples

- ⚠️ A block-level officer designing a pension disbursement process with doorstep verification for
  elderly/disabled applicants operationalises compassion as service design, not sentiment.
- ⚠️ A returning officer during election duty applying identical scrutiny to all candidates,
  irrespective of ruling-party affiliation, demonstrates non-partisanship under real pressure.
- ✅ Mission Karmayogi's (NPCSCB) behavioural-competency framework is the institutional attempt to
  build these foundational values systematically into training rather than leave them to informal
  socialisation (📰 verify current course content independently before citing specifics).

## 5. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ The UPSC GS-IV syllabus explicitly lists: "integrity, impartiality and non-partisanship,
  objectivity, dedication to public service, empathy, tolerance and compassion towards the weaker
  sections" as foundational values for civil service.
- ✅ The Nolan Committee's seven principles of public life (selflessness, integrity, objectivity,
  accountability, openness, honesty, leadership) — see `09` — overlap substantially with, but are
  not identical to, the GS-IV syllabus's own list.
- ⚠️ Empathy, sympathy and compassion are conceptually distinct, though often used loosely
  interchangeably in casual speech.

## 6. UPSC traps

- ❌ Impartiality means treating every case identically regardless of context. -> It means deciding
  on merit/law without bias — it is compatible with (and often requires) affirmative, compassionate
  attention to genuinely disadvantaged applicants, which is not the same as favouritism.
- ❌ Compassion is a private emotional sentiment with no administrative content. -> In GS-IV, it must
  be shown as *translated into service design or decision* — a felt sentiment alone earns no marks.
- ❌ Non-partisanship means a civil servant should have no political opinions at all. -> It means
  personal political views must not shape official advice/action; the person may still hold views
  privately.

## 7. PYQ application

- ✅ 2025 GS-IV Q5(b): civil servant as "enabler and facilitator of growth rather than a regulator"
  for holistic development — a direct test of dedication to public service.
- ✅ 2024 GS-IV Q6(a): gender-specific challenges for women in public service and measures to sustain
  "high standards of probity" — tests impartiality/objectivity institutionally.
- ✅ 2025 GS-IV Q4(a): civil servant using "reason and critical thinking in an ethical framework" for
  welfare-scheme "social re-engineering" — tests objectivity and dedication together.

## 8. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Never answer a foundational-values question with adjectives alone ("be honest, be kind") —
  name the value precisely, show the specific institutional/procedural mechanism that operationalises
  it, and give one concrete Indian example.
- ⚠️ When a question pairs "regulator vs facilitator," argue for a calibrated role: regulate where
  law/safety/equity require it, facilitate/enable where discretion can unlock legitimate outcomes
  faster — not an either/or position.

> **Answer thesis:** Foundational civil-service values are only exam-credible when shown as
> operational design choices — impartial procedure, compassionate service delivery, non-partisan
> conduct under political pressure — not as abstract personal virtues.

## 9. Probable questions

- ⚠️ **Prelims:** Distinguish empathy, sympathy and compassion as used in civil-service ethics.
- ⚠️ **Mains (10 marks):** How can a civil servant balance impartiality with compassion towards
  weaker sections while implementing a targeted welfare scheme?
- ⚠️ **Mains (15 marks):** "A civil servant must act as an enabler and facilitator rather than a
  regulator." Discuss with specific administrative measures.
