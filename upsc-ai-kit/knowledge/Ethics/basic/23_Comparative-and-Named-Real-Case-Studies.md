## 2. Essential case summaries (retain source and date)

| Case | Source-grounded facts |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Manjunath Shanmugam** | IIM-Lucknow graduate, Indian Oil Corporation officer; resisted bribes and threats in his fight against petrol-pump adulteration; shot dead 19 November 2005, allegedly at the behest of corrupt petrol-pump owners (ARC Box 3.1). |
| ✅ **Satyendra Dubey** | National Highways Authority of India engineer; exposed rampant corruption in road construction; found dead 27 November 2003 (ARC Box 3.1). |
| ✅ **H.G. Mudgal case (1951)** | Ad hoc Parliamentary Committee found Mudgal guilty of accepting money/benefits from the Bombay Bullion Association for parliamentary favours; PM Nehru argued the case was unambiguous and moved for expulsion, which the House accepted (ARC 2.5.7.1). |
| ✅ **"Cash-for-questions" case (2005)** | Following an "Aaj Tak" television exposé (12 December 2005) of MPs allegedly accepting money to ask questions/raise matters in the House, a Lok Sabha Enquiry Committee found the conduct "unbecoming" and "unethical"; the House expelled the Members involved; the Rajya Sabha's Ethics Committee similarly recommended and secured the expulsion of one implicated Member (ARC 2.5.7.2-2.5.7.3). |
| ✅ **MKSS Jan Sunwai (Rajasthan)** | The Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan used access to employment rolls, vouchers, beneficiary lists and completion/utilisation certificates to conduct public hearings (*Jan Sunwai*), exposing false muster rolls and bills in local public works; led to Rajasthan introducing a ward-sabha-based social-audit mechanism for government programmes (ARC 5.1.4). |
| ✅ **Parivartan (Delhi)** | Used RTI to access ration-shop stock registers, exposing large-scale diversion of subsidised rice, wheat and oil from the Public Distribution System to the open market (ARC 5.1.4). |
| ✅ **Gyandoot (Madhya Pradesh)** | Low-cost intranet linking market centres and villages; local-youth-run kiosks provided agricultural produce rates, land-record copies, online grievance registration; entire project cost (~Rs 75,000 per village) borne by panchayats/community; evaluation showed reduced corruption (ARC 6.4.2). |
| ✅ **Bhoomi (Karnataka)** | Computerised ~20 million land-ownership records of 6.7 million farmers; automated mutation-notice generation with a 30-day objection window; independent 2002 evaluation showed significant efficiency gains and reduced corruption (ARC 6.4.3). |
| ✅ **CARD (Andhra Pradesh)** | Computer-Aided Administration of Registration Department; ARC cites a specific evaluation (Ramanathan and Balakrishnan, 2000) finding **no significant difference in corruption levels** between computerised and non-computerised Sub-Registrar Offices — a sobering counter-example to Bhoomi/Gyandoot (ARC 6.4.4). |

## 3. Comparative international models (ARC-cited)

| Country/Model | Source-grounded facts |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Hong Kong's ICAC** | Independent Commission Against Corruption; used sustained public-relations campaigns, a TV drama series ("ICAC Investigators"), school education packages and community outreach to shift public attitude from resigned acceptance of corruption to active reporting; the ARC (Box 5.1, and its own National Colloquium address) credits it with transforming a "passive social environment" that once condoned corruption. |
| ✅ **Singapore** | The ARC's **2007 annexure** described the then regime as up to 5 years' imprisonment and a S$10,000 fine, plus repayment and confiscation mechanisms. This is historical source material, not the current penalty. 📰 Singapore's official CPIB currently states a maximum fine of S$100,000 or imprisonment up to 5 years, or both, per corruption count; imprisonment may rise to 7 years for government-contract/public-body cases. |
| ✅ **Korea** | The 1975 General Administrative Reform Movement prohibited private-sector re-employment of civil servants dismissed for corruption, and extended this prohibition to the sons and grandsons of the corrupt official, and to whoever had originally recommended that official (ARC National Colloquium Annexure-I(2)). |
| ✅ **Finland** | Cited (ARC 3.2.1.1) as the least-corrupt nation per the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index at the time of writing, yet — like India's own PC Act — has **no formal legal definition of "corruption"** in its statutes, showing that a precise legal definition is not itself the decisive variable in controlling corruption. |
| ✅ **Thailand** | ARC's Colloquium annexure notes Thailand's Penal Code prescribes life imprisonment or the death penalty for corruption convictions, yet Thailand still "figures among countries with the highest level of corruption" at the time — illustrating that penalty severity alone does not guarantee deterrence. |
| ✅ **Japan** | Social disapproval (informal, cultural) rather than only legal sanction is described as a principal historical mechanism regulating civil-servant conduct, with senior officials/Ministers also bearing reputational consequences for subordinates' misconduct (ARC National Colloquium Annexure-I(2)). |

## 4. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ Satyendra Dubey (NHAI) died 27 November 2003; Manjunath Shanmugam (IOC) died 19 November 2005 —
  both cited by the ARC as the direct human cost motivating its whistleblower-protection recommendation.
- ✅ The Mudgal case (1951) was independent India's first major parliamentary-expulsion precedent for
  corrupt conduct; the 2005 "cash-for-questions" case was the next major precedent, involving both
  Houses' Ethics Committees.
- ✅ ARC's own CARD citation is the source-grounded counter-example showing computerisation alone does
  not guarantee reduced corruption — contrast this explicitly with Bhoomi and Gyandoot.
- ✅ Finland (least corrupt, ARC-era CPI ranking) has no formal legal definition of corruption, just
  like India's own Prevention of Corruption Act.

## 5. UPSC traps

- ❌ All IT-enabled governance reforms reduce corruption. -> ARC's own CARD citation shows a specific,
  source-grounded counter-example; always pair a success case with this sobering contrast.
- ❌ Whistleblower cases like Manjunath and Dubey led to immediate comprehensive legal protection. ->
  The Whistle Blowers Protection Act was enacted only in 2014 and remains, per current verified
  status, still not brought into force (see `10`, `19`).
- ❌ Harsher penalties always produce lower corruption (Thailand's life-imprisonment/death-penalty
  provision proves this). -> ARC's own citation shows Thailand had severe penalties yet high
  corruption at the time, illustrating those two variables are not simply, directly correlated.

## 6. PYQ application

- ⚠️ Any question on "civil society's role in fighting corruption" should cite MKSS and Parivartan
  by name with their specific mechanisms (Jan Sunwai; RTI-enabled PDS stock-register access).
- ⚠️ Any question on "e-governance and corruption" should cite Gyandoot/Bhoomi as successes and CARD
  as the ARC's own cited counter-example, to demonstrate balanced, source-grounded analysis.
- ⚠️ Any comparative-institutions question should cite Hong Kong's ICAC (attitude-change model),
  Singapore's CPIB (penalty + confiscation model), and Korea's 1975 reform (family/recommender
  liability model) as three genuinely distinct anti-corruption design philosophies.

## 7. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Never present a named case as a simplified "hero wins" story — always retain the case's precise
  source, date and (where the ARC itself notes it) limitation or mixed result.
- ⚠️ When asked for a "model" from abroad, name the specific mechanism (ICAC's public education;
  Singapore's confiscation law; Korea's extended-family employment ban) rather than a vague
  "that country is less corrupt" statement.

> **Answer thesis:** India's and the world's anti-corruption record, as the ARC itself documents,
> is a mixed one of genuine successes (MKSS, Parivartan, Bhoomi, Gyandoot, Hong Kong's ICAC) and
> sobering limitations (Manjunath's and Dubey's deaths, CARD's non-result, Thailand's severe-
> penalty/high-corruption combination) — a mature answer retains this balance and precise sourcing
> rather than a simplified narrative.

## 8. Probable questions

- ⚠️ **Prelims:** In which year were Satyendra Dubey and Manjunath Shanmugam killed, respectively?
- ⚠️ **Mains (10 marks):** What does the CARD case in Andhra Pradesh teach about the limits of
  IT-enabled anti-corruption reform?
- ⚠️ **Mains (15 marks):** Compare Hong Kong's ICAC, Singapore's CPIB and Korea's 1975 reform as
  three distinct anti-corruption institutional-design philosophies.
