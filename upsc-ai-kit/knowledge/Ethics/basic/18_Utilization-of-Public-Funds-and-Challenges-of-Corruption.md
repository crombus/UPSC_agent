**Core proposition:** ✅ ARC 3.2.1.7 — "squandering public money, including ostentatious official
life-styles" involves neither private pecuniary gain nor a specific identifiable victim, yet "the
public exchequer at large suffers and both public interest and citizens' trust in government are
undermined" — a distinct category of harm requiring its own classification (see `19`).

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Squandering public money** (ARC 3.2.1.7) | Wasteful, ostentatious or unnecessary expenditure of public funds without individual misappropriation or specific pecuniary gain to anyone, yet still harmful to the exchequer and public trust. |
| ✅ **Procurement corruption** | Inflated pricing, substandard delivery, collusive bidding or kickback-driven contract awards in government purchase of goods/services/works — historically the largest single category of public-expenditure corruption (ARC Annexure-VII(1)). |
| ✅ **MPLADS/discretionary-fund risk** (ARC 2.6.7-2.6.10) | Local Area Development Schemes empower legislators to sanction public works and authorise expenditure — the ARC argues this constitutes an executive function exercised by legislators, blurring separation of powers and creating a distinctive discretionary-fund corruption risk. |
| ⚠️ **Utilisation vs allocation** | Allocation (deciding how much money goes where) is a policy/budgetary decision; utilisation (whether allocated money is actually, efficiently and honestly spent for its intended purpose) is where most of the ethical failures examined in this topic occur. |

## 3. Mechanism: how public funds are diverted or wasted

1. **Procurement stage:** ✅ ARC's Annexure-VII(1) cites a 1999 CII survey finding 60% of 210
   surveyed private firms confirmed 2-25% of contract value as the "price payable to secure a
   government contract"; a World Bank 2003 assessment found officials estimated up to 5% and
   contractors up to 15% of contract value as the informal cost "built into the price."
2. **Discretionary-scheme stage:** ✅ ARC 2.6.7-2.6.9 argues Local Area Development Schemes
   (MPLADS/MLALADS) let legislators "sanction public works and authorize expenditure" — a
   textbook executive function — while the "District Magistrate" nominally controlling funds does
   not change the underlying separation-of-powers concern, per the ARC's own reasoning (citing Era
   Sezhiyan's critique, "MPLADS: Concept, Confusion and Contradictions").
3. **Expenditure/lifestyle stage:** ✅ ARC 3.2.1.7 identifies ostentatious official spending as a
   distinct harm category — no bribery or misappropriation occurs, yet public resources are wasted
   and citizen trust erodes.
4. ✅ 2025 GS-IV Q6(b) directly tests this topic: India's economic rise "as per IMF projection" (4th
   largest economy) alongside sectors where "allocated funds" are "either underutilised or misused,"
   asking for specific measures to "ensure accountability" and "stop leakages."

## 4. Indian applications and examples

- ✅ ARC Annexure-VII(1)'s "Weaknesses in the Procurement Regime": absence of a dedicated policy-
  making agency, absence of a central procurement law, absence of a credible complaint/grievance
  mechanism, absence of standard tender documents, preferential treatment, opaque negotiations, and
  delayed tender processing.
- ✅ ARC's own recommended correctives: a "Transparency in Procurement Act" (citing Tamil Nadu's
  1998 and Karnataka's 1999 pioneering state legislation), published evaluation criteria fixed before
  bidding, committee-based evaluation for large contracts, and increased use of IT for transparency.
- ⚠️ A Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) mechanism reduces the "utilisation" leakage risk by removing
  intermediary discretion between allocation and the intended beneficiary (see `11` for the
  accountability-mechanism framing).

## 5. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ ARC's 4th Report classifies "squandering public money" as a distinct category of official
  misconduct not currently criminalised under the Prevention of Corruption Act (see `19` for full
  legal treatment).
- ✅ Tamil Nadu's Transparency in Tenders Act (1998) and Karnataka's Transparency in Public
  Procurement Act (1999) were the first state-level procurement-transparency laws in India, per the
  ARC's citation.
- ⚠️ MPLADS-type discretionary schemes are frequently examined for their separation-of-powers
  tension (see `11`'s office-of-profit discussion for the constitutional angle).

## 6. UPSC traps

- ❌ Public-fund misuse always involves an individual pocketing a bribe. -> ARC 3.2.1.7 explicitly
  identifies "squandering" (ostentatious, wasteful spending) as harmful even without any
  identifiable personal pecuniary gain.
- ❌ MPLADS-type schemes are unconstitutional and have been struck down. -> The ARC raises a
  separation-of-powers *concern* about such schemes; verify current judicial/constitutional status
  independently rather than assuming a settled prohibition.
- ❌ Procurement corruption is mainly a problem of individual dishonesty. -> ARC's own diagnosis
  emphasises *systemic weaknesses* (absence of standard documents, absence of a complaint mechanism,
  absence of a central law) as much as individual venality.

## 7. PYQ application

- ✅ 2025 GS-IV Q6(b): India's economic growth alongside fund underutilisation/misuse — asks for
  specific accountability measures to stop leakages (a direct match to this topic).
- ⚠️ Any question on "improving public procurement" should cite the ARC's Transparency-in-
  Procurement-Act recommendation and the Tamil Nadu/Karnataka precedents by name.

## 8. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Distinguish squandering, procurement corruption and discretionary-scheme risk as three separate
  mechanisms when a question asks broadly about "misuse of public funds" — naming the specific
  mechanism earns more credit than a generic "corruption in spending" answer.
- ⚠️ Recommend specific, source-grounded correctives: standard tender documents, pre-fixed evaluation
  criteria, committee-based evaluation, IT-enabled transparency, and a dedicated procurement law.

> **Answer thesis:** Public-fund misuse in India operates through at least three distinct
> mechanisms — procurement corruption, ostentatious squandering without individual gain, and
> discretionary-fund schemes blurring separation of powers — each requiring a distinct, named
> corrective rather than a single generic anti-corruption gesture.
