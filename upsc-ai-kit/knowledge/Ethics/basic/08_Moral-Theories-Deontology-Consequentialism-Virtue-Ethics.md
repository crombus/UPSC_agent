**Core proposition:** ⚠️ These are three distinct, non-reducible ways of answering "what should I
do," each supplying a different diagnostic question — GS-IV case studies expect a candidate to
apply more than one and reconcile or choose between them explicitly.

## 2. Essential definitions

| Theory | Core claim | Key figure(s) |
|---|---|---|
| ✅ **Deontology** | An act is right or wrong based on its conformity to a rule/duty, independent of its consequences. | Kant (Categorical Imperative) |
| ✅ **Consequentialism (Utilitarianism)** | An act is right if it produces the best overall outcome/welfare, compared to alternatives. | Bentham, Mill |
| ✅ **Virtue Ethics** | An act is right if it is what a person of good character (possessing practical wisdom, *phronesis*) would do in that situation. | Aristotle |
| ⚠️ **Ethical relativism** | The (contested) view that moral standards are relative to a culture/individual, with no universal standard — usually presented in GS-IV as a foil to be critiqued, not endorsed. |
| ⚠️ **Ethical absolutism/universalism** | The view that some moral standards hold across all cultures/contexts — Kantian deontology is typically absolutist about core duties. |

## 3. Mechanism: applying each theory to a dilemma

1. **Deontology first:** identify the relevant duty/rule (truthfulness, promise-keeping, legal
   compliance, human dignity) and check whether the contemplated action violates it *regardless* of
   how good the outcome might be.
2. **Consequentialism second:** estimate the aggregate welfare effect of each option on all
   affected parties, including indirect/long-term effects (precedent-setting, trust erosion).
3. **Virtue ethics third:** ask what a person with practical wisdom and the relevant virtues
   (courage, justice, temperance, honesty) would judge appropriate *in this specific context* —
   this step captures nuance the other two can miss.
4. **Reconcile:** where all three converge, the recommendation is robust; where they diverge, state
   which theory you are prioritising and why (e.g., prioritising a rights-based duty over a
   marginal aggregate-welfare gain when fundamental dignity is at stake).

## 4. Indian applications and examples

- ⚠️ A land-acquisition officer facing pressure to under-report compensation: deontology says this
  violates a duty of honesty/fair dealing regardless of project benefits; consequentialism weighs
  the project's aggregate benefit against the displaced families' loss; virtue ethics asks what a
  just, temperate officer would do — in this case, all three converge against under-reporting.
- ⚠️ A resource-allocation decision during a shortage (vaccines, disaster relief) is classically
  consequentialist (maximise lives saved) but must be checked against a deontological floor
  (non-discrimination, dignity) and a virtue-ethics test (would a compassionate, just administrator
  design the priority list this way?).

## 5. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ Deontology = duty-based; Consequentialism = outcome-based; Virtue ethics = character-based —
  the three classical families of normative ethical theory.
- ✅ Kant is the paradigmatic deontologist; Bentham and Mill are the paradigmatic utilitarians;
  Aristotle is the paradigmatic virtue ethicist.
- ⚠️ Ethical relativism and ethical absolutism/universalism are positions about the *scope* of moral
  standards, cutting across (not identical to) the three normative theories above.

## 6. UPSC traps

- ❌ Deontology and virtue ethics are the same because both reject pure outcome-calculation. ->
  Deontology centres on conformity to a rule/duty; virtue ethics centres on the character/practical
  judgment of the agent — related but analytically distinct.
- ❌ Consequentialism ignores rights entirely. -> Rule utilitarianism and rights-respecting versions
  of consequentialism can still protect rights instrumentally, by showing that rights-protection
  produces better long-run aggregate outcomes.
- ❌ A "good" administrative decision must satisfy only one moral theory to be justified. -> GS-IV
  expects triangulation across all three; convergence strengthens, divergence requires explicit
  justification of the chosen priority.

## 7. PYQ application

- ✅ 2024 GS-IV Q3(c) (Kant quotation on law vs ethics) is a direct deontology-application question.
- ⚠️ Case-study questions (2025 GS-IV Q7, disaster-management dilemma) implicitly require running
  the decision through all three theories, even though the paper does not name them explicitly.
- ⚠️ Theory questions asking to "critically examine" a policy/statement from "the ethical point of
  view" (e.g., 2024 GS-IV Q1(a) on AI in decision-making) are best answered by explicitly naming and
  applying the relevant theory/theories rather than giving a generic moral reaction.

## 8. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Structure theory-application answers as: name the dilemma -> apply deontology -> apply
  consequentialism -> apply virtue ethics -> state convergence/divergence -> give the final,
  justified recommendation.
- ⚠️ Never present only one theory as "the" ethical answer without acknowledging the others exist —
  examiners reward theoretical range.

> **Answer thesis:** A defensible administrative-ethics judgment triangulates duty, consequence and
> character — deontology sets the non-negotiable floor, consequentialism optimises within that
> floor, and virtue ethics supplies contextual, practical judgment where rules and calculations
> under-determine the right action.

## 9. Probable questions

- ⚠️ **Prelims:** Classify a given ethical claim as deontological, consequentialist or virtue-based.
- ⚠️ **Mains (10 marks):** Apply deontological and consequentialist reasoning to a civil servant's
  decision to disclose or withhold information in the public interest.
- ⚠️ **Mains (15 marks):** "The application of Artificial Intelligence as a dependable source of
  input for administrative rational decision-making is a debatable issue." Critically examine from
  the ethical point of view using at least two moral theories.
