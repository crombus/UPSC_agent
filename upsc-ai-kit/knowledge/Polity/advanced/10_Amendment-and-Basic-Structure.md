## 0. Nature
✅ **Art 368, Part XX** — Parliament's power & procedure to amend by **addition, variation or repeal**.
✅ Indian amendment process is **neither as easy as Britain nor as rigid as USA** — a **synthesis**
(K.C. Wheare: "strikes a good balance between flexibility and rigidity"). ✅ Parliament exercises
**constituent power**, but **cannot amend the "basic structure"** (*Kesavananda Bharati*, 1973).

## 1. Procedure for amendment (Art 368) — 8 steps
✅ (1) Initiated **only in Parliament** (not state legislatures) by a **bill**. (2) By minister OR
private member; **no prior Presidential permission**. (3) Passed in each House by **special majority**
= majority of **total membership** + **two-thirds of members present & voting**. (4) **Each House
separately** — **no joint sitting** provision for deadlock. (5) If federal provisions → **ratified by
half the states** by **simple majority** (no time limit; can't be forced). (6) Presented to President.
(7) President **must assent** — cannot withhold or return (**24th Amdt, 1971** made assent obligatory).
(8) Becomes a Constitutional Amendment Act.

> 🔑 Traps: joint sitting **not** available; states ratify by **simple** majority; President has **no
> veto** on amendment bills (unlike ordinary bills).

## 2. Three types of amendments
| Type | Majority needed | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **(a) Simple majority** | Majority of members present & voting | ✅ **Outside Art 368** — not deemed amendments under Art 368 |
| **(b) Special majority** | Total-membership majority + 2/3 present & voting | Most of the Constitution incl. **FR & DPSP** |
| **(c) Special majority + state ratification** | (b) + half of state legislatures (simple majority) | Federal provisions |

### (a) By SIMPLE majority (outside Art 368) — high-yield list
✅ New states admission/formation, alteration of areas/boundaries/names; abolition/creation of
**legislative councils**; **2nd Schedule** (emoluments); quorum; MP salaries; parliamentary
procedure/privileges; use of English in Parliament; number of SC puisne judges; more SC jurisdiction;
official language; **citizenship** (acquisition/termination); elections to Parliament/state legs;
delimitation; **UTs**; **5th Schedule** (scheduled areas); **6th Schedule** (tribal areas).

### (b) By SPECIAL majority
✅ FR, DPSP, and all provisions not in (a) or (c). "Total membership" = full strength regardless of
vacancies/absentees.

### (c) SPECIAL majority + STATE ratification — the federal list
✅ (1) Election of President & its manner; (2) extent of Union & state executive power; (3) **SC &
HCs**; (4) distribution of legislative powers (Centre-state); (5) **GST Council** (added by **101st
Amdt, 2016**, Art 279-A); (6) any of the **7th Schedule** lists; (7) representation of states in
Parliament; (8) **Art 368 itself**.

## 3. Criticism of the amendment procedure (7)
✅ (1) No special body (Constitutional Convention as in USA). (2) States **can't initiate** amendments
(except resolution for creating/abolishing legislative councils). (3) Most amendments by Parliament
alone; state consent only from **half** (vs **three-fourths** in USA). (4) No time-frame for state
ratification; silent on withdrawal of consent. (5) No **joint sitting** for deadlock. (6) Process
resembles ordinary legislation (except special majority). (7) Provisions too **sketchy** → wide scope
for judiciary. ✅ Yet praised: Nehru (flexibility for a "living, vital, organic people"), Ambedkar
("facile procedure"), K.C. Wheare ("variety… wise but rarely found"), Granville Austin ("ably
conceived… merely diverse").

## 4. BASIC STRUCTURE — emergence (the case-law spine) ⭐⭐
| Case / Amendment | Year | Ruling |
|---|---|---|
| ✅ **Shankari Prasad** | 1951 | Art 368 power **includes** amending FR; "law" in Art 13 = ordinary laws only, not constitutional amendments. (Challenge: 1st Amdt / property.) |
| ✅ **Golak Nath** | 1967 | **Reversed** — FR are "**transcendental and immutable**"; amendment **is** "law" under Art 13 → Parliament **cannot** abridge FR. (Challenge: 17th Amdt / 9th Schedule.) |
| ✅ **24th Amdt** | 1971 | Parliament **can** abridge/take away FR under Art 368; such act **not** "law" under Art 13. Amended Art 13 & 368. |
| ✅ **Kesavananda Bharati** | 1973 | **Overruled Golak Nath**; upheld 24th Amdt (Parliament can amend FR) **BUT** propounded the **"basic structure" doctrine** — Art 368 constituent power **cannot alter basic structure**. **13-judge bench** (largest ever). |
| ✅ **Indira Nehru Gandhi** | 1975 | Reaffirmed doctrine; struck down part of **39th Amdt** (removing PM/Speaker election disputes from courts) as violating basic structure. |
| ✅ **42nd Amdt** | 1976 | Declared **no limitation** on constituent power; no amendment questionable in any court. |
| ✅ **Minerva Mills** | 1980 | Struck that down — **judicial review** & **limited amending power** are basic features. "A limited amending power… cannot be converted into an unlimited one." |
| ✅ **Waman Rao** | 1981 | Basic-structure doctrine applies to amendments **after 24 April 1973** (Kesavananda date). |

## 5. Elements of the Basic Structure (illustrative, court hasn't defined exhaustively)
✅ Supremacy of Constitution · sovereign-democratic-republican nature · **secular** character ·
separation of powers · **federal** character · unity & integrity · welfare state (socio-economic
justice) · **judicial review** · freedom & dignity of individual · parliamentary system · **rule of
law** · harmony/balance between FR & DPSP · principle of equality · **free & fair elections** ·
**independence of judiciary** · limited amending power · effective access to justice · essence of FRs ·
powers of **SC (Art 32,136,141,142)** · powers of **HCs (Art 226,227)**.

> ⚠️ Later additions courts have cited: judicial review, free & fair elections (Indira Gandhi 1975),
> rule of law (Indira Gandhi), federalism (S.R. Bommai 1994). The list is **open-ended** by design.

## 6. 📰 CURRENT AFFAIRS — Basic Structure at 50 (2023–24)
📰 **2023 = 50th anniversary** of *Kesavananda Bharati* (24 April 1973). 📰 **VP Jagdeep Dhankhar**
publicly questioned the doctrine — argued it **dilutes parliamentary sovereignty** and that Parliament
(as the elected sovereign) shouldn't be subject to a judicial "veto." 📰 Judiciary/legal community
reaffirmed: unlike Britain, **Indian Parliament is not supreme** — its power is bounded by the
Constitution and **judicial review** (itself part of the basic structure). This "Legislature vs
Judiciary" tension is prime **Mains/essay** material.

## UPSC Traps
- ❌ Amendment can be initiated in state legislatures → **only Parliament** (Art 368).
- ❌ Deadlock resolved by joint sitting → **no joint-sitting** provision for amendment bills.
- ❌ States ratify by special majority → **simple** majority; only **half** (not 3/4 as in USA).
- ❌ President can withhold/return an amendment bill → **must assent** (24th Amdt).
- ❌ Simple-majority changes (new states, citizenship, 5th/6th Schedule) count as Art 368 amendments → they're **outside** Art 368.
- ❌ Kesavananda gave Parliament unlimited power → it **limited** it via basic structure.
- ❌ Basic structure was first laid down in Golak Nath → **Kesavananda (1973)**; Golak Nath only made FR unamendable.
- ❌ 9th Schedule laws are fully immune → **I.R. Coelho (2007)**: post-1973 9th-Schedule laws are subject to basic-structure review.
