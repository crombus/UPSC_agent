## 1. Visual foundation

| IP instrument | Protects what? | Typical holder | Duration logic |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ **Patent** | New invention (product/process) meeting legal criteria | Inventor / assignee | 20 years from filing; not renewable forever |
| ✅ **Trademark** | Brand identifier distinguishing goods/services | Individual, firm or company proprietor | 10 years, renewable indefinitely |
| ✅ **GI** | Goods whose quality/reputation is tied to place of origin | Collective/community/association-based right | 10 years, renewable indefinitely |
| ✅ **Copyright** | Original literary, artistic, musical and related works | Author / rights holder | Term differs by work type |
| ✅ **Design** | Visual features of shape, pattern, ornament or composition | Registered proprietor | Separate design-law route |
| ✅ **Trade secret** | Confidential business information with competitive value | Enterprise / lawful possessor | No fixed statutory term; lasts while secrecy is maintained |

**Core proposition:** UPSC repeatedly tests whether the candidate can distinguish **patent**, **trademark** and **GI** as three legally different IP tools rather than treating all “rights” as one category.

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Patent** | Exclusive right granted for an invention that is new, involves inventive step and is capable of industrial application. |
| ✅ **Inventive step** | Technical advance or economic significance that makes the invention non-obvious to a person skilled in the art. |
| ✅ **Trademark** | Sign capable of graphic representation and of distinguishing the goods or services of one person from those of others. |
| ✅ **Geographical Indication (GI)** | Indication identifying goods as originating in a territory/region/locality where a given quality, reputation or characteristic is essentially attributable to that origin. |
| ✅ **Section 3(d)** | Patent-law filter that blocks mere new forms/new uses of known substances unless enhanced efficacy is shown. |
| ✅ **Compulsory licence** | Statutory authorization to use a patent without ordinary voluntary licence, subject to legal grounds and conditions. |
| ✅ **PCT** | WIPO-administered filing system for international patent applications; it is a filing route, not a global patent grant. |
| ✅ **TRIPS** | WTO agreement setting minimum standards for intellectual-property protection and flexibilities. |

## 3. Mechanism / how it works

1. ✅ For a patent in India, the invention must satisfy **novelty, inventive step and industrial applicability**.
2. ✅ Section 3 of the Patents Act excludes certain subject matter from patentability; Section 3(d) is especially important in pharmaceutical debates.
3. ✅ A patent application is filed, examined and, if compliant, granted for a time-bound exclusive right.
4. ✅ A trademark application protects brand-distinguishing signs; registration is renewable in further 10-year blocks.
5. ✅ A GI application is filed through the GI Registry with proof of geographical link, reputation/quality and producer organization details.
6. ✅ Under Section 84, compulsory licensing may be sought after three years from grant if public requirements are unmet, price is not reasonably affordable or the invention is not worked in India.
7. ⚠️ UPSC answers should connect IPR with **innovation incentives + public interest + access questions**.

## 4. Institutions and programmes

- ✅ **CGPDTM:** Controller General of Patents, Designs and Trade Marks; umbrella IP-office authority.
- ✅ **Patent Office / Trade Marks Registry / GI Registry:** administrative arms under the IP India architecture.
- ✅ **GI Registry, Chennai:** nodal registry for Geographical Indications.
- ✅ **DPIIT and CIPAM:** policy coordination and implementation support under the National IPR Policy 2016.
- ✅ **WIPO:** global IP body administering treaties such as the PCT.
- ✅ **WTO/TRIPS framework:** international rule-setting layer relevant to India’s IP obligations and flexibilities.

## 5. Indian applications, examples and limitations

- ✅ **Patents:** important in pharmaceuticals, manufacturing, biotechnology, electronics and deep-tech innovation.
- ✅ **Trademarks:** crucial for brand identity, market signalling and consumer trust.
- ✅ **GIs:** protect region-linked products such as Darjeeling Tea, Kanchipuram Silk or other place-based goods.
- ✅ **National IPR Policy 2016:** stresses awareness, stronger administration, commercialization and balance with public interest.
- ⚠️ **Limitation 1:** strong rights on paper do not automatically ensure fast enforcement or low litigation cost.
- ⚠️ **Limitation 2:** domestic innovators, MSMEs and producer groups may face knowledge and procedural barriers.
- ⚠️ **Limitation 3:** patent protection can encourage innovation, but public-health access questions remain central in sectors like pharma.

## 6. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ A **patent** protects an invention; a **trademark** protects a brand identifier; a **GI** protects place-linked goods with collective regional character.
- ✅ Patentability in India requires **novelty, inventive step and industrial applicability**.
- ✅ Section 3(d) is significant because it prevents routine evergreening claims over known substances without enhanced efficacy.
- ✅ Section 84 allows compulsory licensing after three years from grant on specific public-interest grounds.
- ✅ The Basics of Trademarks page says a registered trademark is valid for **10 years** and is renewable indefinitely for further 10-year periods.
- ✅ GI registration also runs for **10 years** and can be renewed in further 10-year blocks.
- ✅ WIPO’s PCT system is an international filing mechanism; it does **not** itself grant a world patent.
- ✅ India is a PCT contracting state under the WIPO-administered system.

## 7. UPSC traps

- ❌ **Patent, GI and trademark are interchangeable IPR labels.** -> They protect different subject matter, have different holders and work through different legal logic.
- ❌ **A GI belongs to one private company like a trademark.** -> A GI is a place-based collective right tied to producer groups and origin.
- ❌ **A patent can be renewed indefinitely like a trademark.** -> Patent protection is time-bound; trademarks can be renewed indefinitely.
- ❌ **Section 3(d) bans all pharma patents.** -> It targets evergreening-type claims lacking enhanced efficacy, not every pharmaceutical invention.
- ❌ **PCT gives one global patent valid everywhere.** -> It provides a common filing route, after which national/regional phase procedures still matter.
- ❌ **Compulsory licensing means abolition of patent rights.** -> It is a statutory public-interest exception with conditions, not full patent cancellation.

## 10. Mains framework / angles

- ⚠️ Start with the IPR ecosystem, then separate **patent / trademark / GI** clearly.
- ⚠️ Mention patentability criteria and the role of Section 3(d).
- ⚠️ Add compulsory licensing as a legally recognized public-interest flexibility.
- ⚠️ Bring in DPIIT, CGPDTM, WIPO, PCT and TRIPS.
- ⚠️ Conclude with commercialization and public-interest balance rather than taking an extreme pro- or anti-IP stance.

> **Answer thesis:** India’s IPR regime should be read as a differentiated legal architecture—patents for inventions, trademarks for brands and GIs for place-linked goods—while patent law itself contains public-interest filters such as Section 3(d) and compulsory licensing to balance innovation with access and welfare.
