**Core proposition:** ✅ VisionIAS defines coastal security as "a subset of
maritime security that involves securing the country's coasts by guarding
its maritime approaches against any threat or challenge that originates
from the sea" (PDF p. 31), and sets out a three-tier arrangement: "the
Navy and the Coast Guard" on the high seas/EEZ limits, the "Coast Guards"
protecting territorial waters, and "close coastal patrolling... by State
Marine Police" (PDF p. 35).

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Maritime terrorism** | "The undertaking of terrorist acts and activities within the maritime environment, using or against vessels or fixed platforms at sea or in port... against coastal facilities or settlements" (VisionIAS, PDF p. 31). |
| ✅ **Piracy** | By definition occurs on the high seas; India's Sunderbans shallow-water violence is analogous but not technically piracy under this definition (VisionIAS, PDF p. 31). |
| ✅ **Indian Coast Guard (ICG)** | Established 1 February 1977; formally constituted as the fourth armed force of India under the Coast Guard Act (18 August 1978), under the Ministry of Defence, tasked with maritime-zone security and protecting maritime/national interests (VisionIAS, PDF p. 33). |
| ✅ **Marine Police Force** | Created under the Coastal Security Scheme (2005); works with the ICG on a "hub-and-spoke" model, patrolling territorial waters (12 nautical miles) (VisionIAS, PDF pp. 33-34). |
| ✅ **NC3I (National Command Control Communication and Intelligence Network)** | An over-arching coastal-security network collating and disseminating vessel data to enhance maritime domain awareness (VisionIAS, PDF p. 35). |
| ✅ **Anti-Maritime Piracy Act, 2022** | Defines piracy per international convention language, applies to the high seas (including EEZ), provides for Designated Courts, extradition and court-ordered disposal of seized property (VisionIAS, PDF p. 37). |

## 3. How the coastal-security architecture works

1. **Threat categorisation:** ✅ VisionIAS's five broad categories:
   maritime terrorism, piracy/armed robbery, smuggling/trafficking,
   infiltration/illegal migration/refugee influx, and fishermen straying
   across maritime boundaries (PDF pp. 31-32).
2. **Historical build-out:** ✅ 1974 Customs Marine Organisation (CMO,
   anti-smuggling) → 1977 Indian Coast Guard → 2005 Coastal Security
   Scheme (three-layered structure) → post-26/11 multilayered surveillance
   expansion (NC3I, IMAC) → 2017 proposed Maritime Theatre Command → 2020
   first national maritime security coordinator appointed (VisionIAS, PDF
   p. 33).
3. **Three-tier jurisdictional design:** ✅ Navy/Coast Guard patrol EEZ
   limits on the high seas; Coast Guard protects territorial waters with
   vessels and aerial surveillance; State Marine Police handles close
   coastal patrolling within 12 nautical miles (VisionIAS, PDF p. 35).
4. **Post-26/11 institutionalisation:** ✅ The "mind-set that coastal
   security is not an essential component of national security eventually
   changed" only after the 2008 Mumbai attacks, after which Joint
   Operations Centres (Mumbai, Visakhapatnam, Kochi, Port Blair) were
   established, jointly manned 24x7 by Navy, ICG and Marine Police
   (VisionIAS, PDF pp. 35-36).
5. **Legal modernisation:** ✅ The Anti-Maritime Piracy Act, 2022 filled a
   gap — India previously "does not have a specific law or legal
   provision in Indian Penal Code or Criminal Procedure Code for piracy"
   (VisionIAS, PDF p. 37) — bringing India's law in line with UNCLOS
   obligations.

## 4. Institutions, laws and reference points

- ✅ **National Committee for Strengthening Maritime and Coastal Security
  (NCSMCS):** apex coordination body headed by the Cabinet Secretary,
  coordinating nearly 15 national and state agencies (VisionIAS, PDF p.
  35).
- ✅ **Information Fusion Centre-Indian Ocean Region (IFC-IOR):** hosted by
  the Indian Navy at Gurugram since 2018, sharing vessel-of-interest
  information with International Liaison Officers from partner nations
  (VisionIAS, PDF p. 36) — IR-owned diplomatic partnership dimension;
  this folder treats its operational maritime-domain-awareness function.
- ✅ **Indian Maritime Security Strategy (IMSS), 2015:** the Navy's
  strategy document envisaging coordination, SLOC security, and
  multilateral engagement (VisionIAS, PDF p. 35).
- 📰 **National Academy of Coastal Policing, Okha (established 7 July
  2026):** MHA's dedicated training institution for coastal/marine police
  personnel from nine coastal States, five Union Territories and central
  forces (BSF, CISF) — the current institutional anchor for marine-police
  training capacity.

## 5. Indian applications and examples

- ✅ VisionIAS cites the 1993 Mumbai serial blasts (explosives smuggled via
  the Raigad coast) and the 26 November 2008 attacks (ten terrorists
  infiltrating via the sea route) as the defining historical illustrations
  of coastal vulnerability (PDF p. 31).
- ✅ **PYQ mapping — verbatim (2025 Q20):** "Why is maritime security vital
  to protect India's sea trade? Discuss maritime and coastal security
  challenges and the way forward."

## 6. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ The Indian Coast Guard was established on 1 February 1977 and
  formally constituted as the fourth armed force of India by the Coast
  Guard Act, 1978.
- ✅ The Marine Police Force was created under the Coastal Security Scheme
  (2005) and patrols territorial waters up to 12 nautical miles.
- ✅ Joint Operations Centres for coastal security operate at Mumbai,
  Visakhapatnam, Kochi and Port Blair.
- ✅ The Anti-Maritime Piracy Act, 2022 filled a pre-existing legal gap —
  India previously had no specific piracy provision in the IPC or CrPC.
- 📰 India's National Academy of Coastal Policing at Okha was established
  as a subordinate MHA office on 7 July 2026.

## 7. UPSC traps

- ❌ Maritime security and coastal security are interchangeable terms. ->
  Coastal security is explicitly a *subset* of the broader maritime-
  security concept, concerned with the coastline and territorial waters,
  while maritime security also covers the EEZ, high seas and SLOCs.
- ❌ Piracy under international law includes any act of maritime violence
  anywhere at sea. -> Piracy, by definition, occurs on the high seas;
  violence in India's territorial/inland waters (e.g., the Sunderbans) is
  analogous but not technically piracy.
- ❌ The Navy is solely responsible for coastal security. -> Coastal
  security operates through a three-tier design: Navy (EEZ/high seas),
  Coast Guard (territorial waters), State Marine Police (close coastal
  patrol) — each has a distinct jurisdictional role.
- ❌ India has always had a dedicated anti-piracy law. -> A specific
  statute (the Anti-Maritime Piracy Act) was enacted only in 2022; before
  that, India relied on general provisions, not a piracy-specific law.

## 8. 📰 Current anchor

- 📰 The **National Academy of Coastal Policing, Okha**, was established
  as a subordinate office of the MHA by official order dated **7 July
  2026**, providing dedicated, specialised training to marine police
  personnel from nine coastal States, five Union Territories and central
  forces — the current institutional anchor for closing the long-
  standing marine-police training capacity gap identified even in the
  VisionIAS source's "way forward" section (PDF p. 37).
