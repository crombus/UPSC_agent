**Core proposition:** ✅ Singh explains Maoism as a doctrine to "capture
State power through a combination of armed insurgency, mass mobilization
and strategic alliances," which Mao termed the "Protracted Peoples War,"
under which "bearing of arms is non-negotiable" (PDF p. 52).

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Naxalism** | Term deriving from Naxalbari village, Darjeeling district, West Bengal, where the movement began in May 1967 under Charu Majumdar and Kanu Sanyal; refers to violence to destabilise the state through communist guerrilla groups (Singh, PDF p. 51). |
| ✅ **Left-Wing Extremism (LWE)** | The broader, current umbrella term for Naxalism/Maoism-inspired violence aimed at capturing political power by force to install a "people's government" (Singh, PDF pp. 51, 56). |
| ✅ **Five-phase spread strategy** | Preparatory → Perspective → Guerrilla → Base → Liberated phase — Maoists' own systematic method of area expansion (Singh, PDF p. 52). |
| ✅ **CPI (Maoist)** | Formed in 2004 by the merger of the People's War Group and the Maoist Communist Centre of India — historically, the principal LWE outfit (Singh, PDF pp. 53-54; book-period organisational detail). |
| ⚠️ **Red Corridor / Dandakaranya region** | Book-period term for the contiguous mineral-rich belt (parts of Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha and adjoining states) most affected by LWE violence — its precise current extent must be verified from a dated MHA source, not assumed from the book. |

## 3. How LWE causation and response work

1. **Ideological method:** ✅ Naxalites "do not seek to secede from the
   Indian Union" (unlike border-region militancy); their aim is "to
   capture political power through armed struggle" (Singh, PDF p. 52) —
   a key distinction from J&K/North-East threats.
2. **Root causes:** ✅ Singh groups causes into "evasion of ceiling laws,"
   "encroachment... of government and community lands," "non-
   regularization of traditional land rights," "land acquisition without
   appropriate compensation," "disruption of the age-old tribal... jal-
   jangal-zameen" relationship, alongside "unemployment," "poverty,"
   "infrastructure deficit," governance deficit and "social exclusion and
   alienation" (PDF p. 59).
3. **Extortion economy:** ✅ LWE is "most intense precisely in areas which
   are rich in natural mineral resources" — coal, iron, bauxite,
   manganese, nickel and copper — because these provide "enough scope for
   extortion" from government and corporate infrastructure projects
   (Singh, PDF p. 58).
4. **Development obstruction as strategy:** ✅ Singh's sharp critique:
   "Maoists have vested interest in keeping poverty alive because it
   enables them to expand their territory. They don't allow district
   administration to do any development work like building roads and
   improving electricity and water supply" (PDF p. 59) — development
   denial is instrumental to the movement's own survival, not accidental.
5. **Response architecture:** ✅ Singh's own three-part strategy —
   development strategy (infrastructure, land rights, Forest Rights Act
   implementation), security strategy (professional dominance, state-
   police primacy, specialised forces on the Greyhounds model) and
   psychological operations (media/public-perception management,
   restoring trust) (PDF pp. 63-64).

## 4. Institutions, laws and reference points

- ✅ **Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition
  of Forest Rights) Act, 2006:** cited by Singh as a rights-based
  instrument whose "coordination and implementation" in LWE-affected
  states is part of the development strategy (PDF p. 63).
- ⚠️ **Security Related Expenditure (SRE) Scheme, Special Infrastructure
  Scheme (SIS), Integrated Action Plan (IAP), Road Requirement Plan,
  Civic Action Programme (CAP):** book-period central schemes for
  LWE-affected states (Singh, PDF pp. 60-61) — verify current scheme
  names, coverage and allocations from a dated MHA source before citing
  any figure.
- ✅ **Operation Greyhound (Andhra Pradesh):** a specialised, trained state
  police force credited with substantially curbing Naxalism within
  undivided Andhra Pradesh — an institutional design precedent (Singh,
  PDF pp. 61-64).
- 📰 **MHA Rajya Sabha Starred Question 49 (4 February 2026):** reported
  that LWE violence had declined by 73% and deaths by 86% since 2010, and
  that the footprint had fallen from 126 districts in 2013 to 38 in 2024.

## 5. Indian applications and examples

## 6. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ Naxalism originated in May 1967 in Naxalbari, Khoribari and
  Phansidewa police-station areas of Darjeeling district, West Bengal,
  under Charu Majumdar and Kanu Sanyal.
- ✅ CPI (Maoist) was formed in 2004 by the merger of the People's War
  Group (Andhra Pradesh) and the Maoist Communist Centre of India (Bihar).
- ✅ The Maoist doctrine's central slogan is "Political power grows out of
  the barrel of a gun"; its strategic method is Mao's "Protracted People's
  War."
- ✅ Naxalites, unlike J&K or North-East militants, do not seek secession
  from the Indian Union; their objective is to capture state power.
- 📰 MHA's 4 February 2026 reply reports a 73% decline in violence and an
  86% decline in deaths since 2010, with the footprint reducing from 126
  districts in 2013 to 38 in 2024.

## 7. UPSC traps

- ❌ Naxalism seeks a separate sovereign state, like some border-region
  militancy. -> Singh is explicit that Naxalites do not seek secession;
  their aim is to capture political power within India through armed
  struggle.
- ❌ LWE is purely a law-and-order problem. -> Singh states plainly that
  "Naxalism is not merely a law and order issue... it is directly related
  to under-development" (PDF p. 62) — governance and development failures
  are root causes, not side issues.
- ❌ Development delivery alone will end LWE, since the movement claims to
  fight for the poor. -> Singh's own critique shows the movement has "a
  vested interest in keeping poverty alive" and actively obstructs
  development works, so development alone, without security and trust-
  restoration, is insufficient.
- ❌ Any 2013-2016-era cadre strength, district count or force-level figure
  in this book reflects the present. -> These are book-period historical
  figures; the current, dated picture must come from the MHA's 2026
  parliamentary reply or a subsequent official release.

## 8. 📰 Current anchor

- 📰 MHA's Rajya Sabha reply of **4 February 2026** reports a **73% decline
  in LWE violence and an 86% decline in deaths since 2010**, with the
  footprint reducing from **126 districts in 2013 to 38 in 2024**,
  alongside the then-stated objective of eliminating LWE by 31 March 2026.
- 📰 PIB's **June 2026 "Naxal-Free India"** explainer subsequently
  describes India as **"effectively free from Left-Wing Extremism"** after
  31 March 2026. Preserve that exact qualification: it records the
  Government's post-deadline assessment, not a claim of literally zero
  incidents or completed root-cause resolution.

## 9. PYQ application

- ✅ **2020 GS-III:** "What are the determinants of left-wing extremism in
  the Eastern part of India? What strategy should the Government of India,
  civil administration and security forces adopt to counter the threat in
  the affected areas?" — organise causes first, then assign distinct roles
  to political executive, civil administration and security forces.
- 📰 **2025 Q10 (verbatim, local paper text reproduced verbatim):** "The

  be eliminated by 2026. What do you understand by LWE and how are the
  people affected by it? What measures have been taken by the government
  to eliminate LWE?"
  - Structure: define LWE and its ideology (Section 1-2) → explain civilian
    impact (denial of development, displacement, extortion economy,

    development, psychological/trust) → anchor "measures taken" in the
    current schemes, the 4 February baseline and the qualified June 2026
    post-deadline assessment.

## 10. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Any "how are people affected by LWE" sub-question should cover: denial
  of development and basic services, displacement and livelihood loss,
  extortion burden, exposure to both extremist and state-security
  coercion, and disruption of local governance institutions (panchayats
  forced to resign, per Singh, PDF p. 55 — book-period example, cited
  illustratively).
- ⚠️ Use Singh's own three-part strategy (development, security,
  psychological operations) as the answer's structural backbone for
  "measures taken," rather than a generic list of schemes.
- ⚠️ Where asked to assess "elimination by 2026," distinguish the
  government's stated objective from verified outcome, citing the 4
  February 2026 reply's own figures rather than assuming completion.
