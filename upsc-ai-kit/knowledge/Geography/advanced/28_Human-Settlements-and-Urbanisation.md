## 2. India's urbanisation trajectory in Khullar

✅ Khullar identifies three broad phases: slow growth before 1931, medium growth from 1931-61, and rapid growth from 1961-2011. ✅ He also notes the impact of Partition, industrialisation, state capitals, new towns and later metropolitan concentration.

| Phase | Main driver | India-specific significance |
|---|---|---|
| ✅ Before 1931 | Slow urban growth | Famines, epidemics and limited industrial base |
| ✅ 1931-1961 | Medium growth | War, Partition, refugee resettlement, early planning towns |
| ✅ 1961-2011 | Rapid growth | Industrialisation, services, transport, metropolitan pull |

## 3. Rural-urban continuum, census towns and peri-urbanisation

✅ The surge in census towns shows that many settlements acquired urban characteristics before receiving full statutory municipal status. ⚠️ This is the geographic core of India's “hidden urbanisation” debate.

| Category | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| ✅ Statutory town | Urban by law and local-body status |
| ✅ Census town | Urban by demographic-economic criteria, but not necessarily by governance structure |
| ✅ Urban agglomeration | Built-up continuity spills beyond one municipal boundary |
| ⚠️ Peri-urban belt | Fast land-use conversion, commuting and service stress |

## 4. Metropolitanisation, slums and uneven urban quality

✅ Khullar notes that metropolitan growth pulls people from smaller towns and villages. ✅ He also records the slum problem as a major consequence of rapid and unplanned urban growth.

- ✅ Census 2011 provisional slum release cited by Khullar recorded **13.71 million urban slum households**, or **17.4%** of all urban households; this is a historical 2011 benchmark, not a current estimate.
- ⚠️ India therefore shows a dual urbanism: globally visible metros on one side, and large deficits in housing, drainage, mobility and formal work on the other.
- ⚠️ “Jobless urbanisation” is a useful debate phrase when population concentrates in cities faster than secure manufacturing employment grows.

## 5. Town planning and urban missions

✅ Khullar treats town planning as necessary because uncontrolled urban growth overloads housing, transport, water, sewage and the environment. ✅ Current missions continue the same logic, though with different instruments.

| Mission / update | Verified current point | Geographic reading |
|---|---|---|
| ✅ Smart Cities Mission | PIB said in July 2024 that the mission was extended to **31 March 2025**; **7,188 projects** worth about **Rs 1.44 lakh crore** were completed, around **90%** of total projects | Focus on service quality, area-based upgrading and digital-physical infrastructure |
| ✅ Smart Cities Mission | PIB noted **830 projects** worth about **Rs 19,926 crore** remained in advanced stages in July 2024 | Completion gap matters more than launch headlines |
| ✅ AMRUT 2.0 | Official progress update of January 2025 reported **8,887 projects** worth about **Rs 1,89,188 crore** approved | Water, sewerage and basic urban services remain the backbone of urban transformation |
| ✅ AMRUT 2.0 | By late January 2025, works worth about **Rs 27,183.78 crore** had been completed | Infrastructure delivery is gradual and spatially uneven |

## 6. Debate: over-urbanisation or under-urban municipal capacity?

- ⚠️ One view says Indian cities are over-urbanising without enough productive jobs, transit and affordable housing.
- ⚠️ Another view says India is not “too urban”; rather, its governance, planning and fiscal capacity are too weak for the scale of actual urban transition.
- ⚠️ A good answer should mention both: rapid concentration of people and lagging urban institutions.

## 7. Must-Know Facts (Prelims) and UPSC traps

- ✅ Census town requires population, density and non-agricultural work criteria.
- ✅ The sharp rise in towns between 2001 and 2011 owed much to census towns.
- ✅ Million-plus cities concentrate a large share of urban population and policy attention.
- ✅ Slum data from 2011 should be labelled as historical baseline, not current estimate.
- ✅ Smart-city rhetoric does not replace water, sewerage and transport fundamentals.

- ❌ Urbanisation in India is measured only by municipalities -> Census towns and UAs matter too.
- ❌ More metros automatically mean better urban development -> Metropolitan growth can deepen inequality and slum pressure.
- ❌ Smart Cities Mission is the same as AMRUT 2.0 -> Their focus and instruments differ.
