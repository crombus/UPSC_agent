## 1. Why agriculture remains a geographic question in India

✅ Indian agriculture is shaped by **monsoon seasonality, irrigation inequality, alluvial vs plateau soils, fragmented holdings, market access and procurement geography**. ✅ Khullar's treatment repeatedly links crop patterns with climate, water and regional contrasts rather than with price policy alone.

⚠️ Cross-link: for MSP economics or subsidy design, see the Economy folder; the focus here is **where production, procurement and food-security functions cluster geographically**.

## 2. Cropping seasons and their spatial logic

| Season | Time window | Geographic base |
|---|---|---|
| ✅ Kharif | Sown with SW monsoon; harvested in autumn | Rice, maize, cotton, soybean, millets in monsoon-fed belts |
| ✅ Rabi | Sown after monsoon withdrawal; harvested in spring | Wheat, mustard, gram in irrigated/alluvial north and dry winter belts |
| ✅ Zaid | Short summer season between Rabi and Kharif | Vegetables, fodder, watermelon in irrigated pockets |

✅ India does not have one agriculture; it has **agro-climatic mosaics** - canal-irrigated north-west, rice-delta east, dryland Deccan, plantation highlands and horticultural mountain belts.

    Monsoon onset -> Kharif sowing -> Harvest -> Rabi sowing on residual moisture / irrigation -> Procurement / storage

> 🔑 Trap: Kharif and Rabi are not merely calendar labels; they represent different rainfall, temperature and irrigation geographies.

## 3. Landholding, irrigation and regional inequality

✅ Official 10th Agriculture Census (2015-16) reported **146.45 million operational holdings** with an average size of **1.08 hectares**. ✅ Small and fragmented holdings raise the importance of irrigation access, procurement centres and road connectivity.

| Factor | Geographic effect |
|---|---|
| ✅ Fragmented holdings | Limits mechanisation and economies of scale |
| ✅ Canal irrigation | Strong in Punjab-Haryana-western UP and parts of Rajasthan |
| ✅ Tube-well irrigation | Strengthens assured Rabi cultivation in alluvial plains |
| ✅ Tank / rainfed dependence | More important in peninsular and plateau India |
| ⚠️ Uneven irrigation | Creates unequal resilience to monsoon failure |

### Indian soil–crop map

| Soil group | Core distribution | Agricultural linkage / trap |
|---|---|---|
| ✅ Alluvial | Indo-Gangetic-Brahmaputra plains and coastal/deltaic tracts | Rice, wheat, sugarcane, jute; khadar is newer alluvium, bhangar older |
| ✅ Black / regur | Deccan Trap areas of Maharashtra, MP, Gujarat, Telangana/Karnataka margins | Moisture-retentive clay; cotton association, but also soybean, pulses and cereals |
| ✅ Red and yellow | Peninsular crystalline uplands | Generally low in nitrogen/humus; responds to irrigation and fertilisation |
| ✅ Laterite | High-rainfall, strongly leached uplands of Ghats and parts of east/north-east India | Tea/coffee/cashew with management; laterite is not naturally fertile everywhere |
| ✅ Arid | Western Rajasthan and adjoining dry tracts | Low humus, salinity/calcareousness possible; irrigation can raise productivity but also waterlogging/salinity |
| ✅ Forest/mountain | Himalayan and hill belts; varies sharply with altitude and parent material | Horticulture, tea and forest use; not one uniform soil |
| ✅ Saline/alkaline and peaty/marshy | Poorly drained, arid-irrigated or coastal/wetland pockets | Problem soils defined by chemistry/drainage, not a single climatic zone |

## 4. Green Revolution geography

✅ The first Green Revolution was regionally concentrated in **Punjab, Haryana and western Uttar Pradesh**, later diffusing into parts of Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh and elsewhere. ✅ Its success rested on a package: HYV seeds, fertilisers, assured irrigation, extension support and procurement.

| Element | Spatial outcome |
|---|---|
| ✅ Assured irrigation | Favoured north-western alluvial plains |
| ✅ Wheat-rice rotation | Intensified surplus production in Punjab-Haryana belt |
| ✅ Procurement infrastructure | Linked surplus districts to the central pool |
| ⚠️ Path dependence | Once grain markets, mandis and storage grew, regional dominance became self-reinforcing |

⚠️ Debate: the Green Revolution was productive but spatially uneven - it deepened inter-regional gaps between irrigated surplus belts and rainfed India.

## 5. Procurement geography, NFSA and the central pool

✅ Food security in India is not only about production; it is about **surplus extraction, storage and redistribution**. ✅ FCI and state agencies procure mainly from surplus grain belts and move stocks into the central pool for PDS/NFSA distribution.

| Stage | Key institutions | Geographic pattern |
|---|---|---|
| ✅ Price recommendation | CACP + Union Government | National MSP announcement |
| ✅ Procurement | FCI + state agencies | Concentrated in surplus wheat/rice belts |
| ✅ Storage | FCI / warehousing network | Stronger near grain-surplus and logistics nodes |
| ✅ Distribution | NFSA / PDS channels | Stocks move toward deficit and population-dense regions |

    Surplus districts -> Mandis / procurement centres -> Central pool -> PDS / NFSA states

⚠️ This is why Punjab and Haryana matter beyond their area share: they are not only producing spaces but also **procurement-and-distribution anchors**.

## 7. Debates and answer-writing depth

- ⚠️ **Regional imbalance:** irrigated north-west advanced faster than dryland plateau and tribal regions.
- ⚠️ **Ecological critique:** groundwater depletion, salinisation, stubble burning and nutrient imbalance emerged in highly intensified belts.
- ⚠️ **Cropping-pattern lock-in:** MSP-procurement concentration can discourage diversification away from rice-wheat in water-stressed regions.
- ⚠️ **Food-security paradox:** India can post record output and still face local nutritional, storage or access inequalities.

⚠️ Cross-link: for ecological externalities of intensive farming, see Environment-and-Ecology; here the concern is how ecology feeds back into **regional agricultural viability**.

## 8. Must-Know Facts (Prelims) and UPSC traps

- ✅ Kharif is monsoon-linked; Rabi depends far more on winter temperatures, residual moisture and irrigation.
- ✅ Green Revolution first concentrated in Punjab-Haryana-western UP.
- ✅ Official 10th Agriculture Census: **146.45 million holdings**, average size **1.08 ha**.
- ✅ Procurement geography is more concentrated than production geography.
- ✅ FCI and state agencies move grain into the **central pool** for NFSA/PDS distribution.
- ✅ Rice-wheat surplus belts became the backbone of India's grain security.

> 🔑 Trap: MSP announcement and actual procurement geography are not the same; procurement is heavily concentrated in selected regions and crops.

- ❌ Green Revolution spread uniformly across India -> It was regionally selective.
- ❌ Record output means universal food security -> Access, storage and distribution remain decisive.
- ❌ Rainfed India is marginal in agriculture -> It remains critical for pulses, oilseeds, coarse cereals and resilience.

## 10. 📰 Current link

### CA Anchor — PM Dhan-Dhaanya Krishi Yojana's district geography

📰 **Current anchor:** PM Dhan-Dhaanya Krishi Yojana was announced in Budget 2025-26, approved by the
Cabinet on **16 July 2025** and launched on **11 October 2025**. It targets **100 districts** selected
for low productivity, low cropping intensity and low credit disbursement, using convergence across schemes.

| Current fact | Static geography link |
|---|---|
| ✅ District targeting | Agricultural backwardness is spatially concentrated |
| ⚠️ Convergence of schemes | Irrigation, credit, storage and extension must be aligned territorially |
| ✅ Procurement concentration | Surplus belts remain central to NFSA logistics |

✅ The programme reinforces a classic geography point: agricultural backwardness is territorially
clustered and needs district-specific convergence rather than one national package.
