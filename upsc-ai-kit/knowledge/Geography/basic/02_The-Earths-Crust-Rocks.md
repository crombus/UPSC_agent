## 1. Snapshot & core idea


**Foundation ‚Äî The Three Rock Families (G.C. Leong).**

‚úÖ The crust is built of three rock families defined by how they form: igneous (from cooled magma), sedimentary (from accumulated sediment) and metamorphic (pre-existing rock altered by heat & pressure). Igneous is the 'primary' rock; its debris builds sedimentary rock; heat & pressure convert either into metamorphic rock ‚Äî the essence of the rock cycle.
- ‚úÖ Plutonic (intrusive) rocks cool slowly deep in the crust ‚Üí large, easily recognised crystals (granite); exposed later by denudation.
- ‚úÖ Volcanic (extrusive) rocks pour out as lava and solidify fast ‚Üí tiny crystals; basalt forms lava sheets/plateaux (Deccan, Columbia-Snake, Iceland).
- ‚úÖ Sedimentary rocks are stratified (layered) ‚Äî usually deposited under water; layers vary from inches to many feet.
- ‚úÖ Sediment is transported by streams, glaciers, wind or even animals before deposition and cementation.
- ‚úÖ Common metamorphic pairs: shale/mudstone‚Üíslate or schist, limestone‚Üímarble, sandstone‚Üíquartzite,
  and granite or other felsic protoliths‚Üígneiss. Graphite may form by metamorphism of carbon-rich material,
  but ‚Äúcoal‚Üígraphite‚Äù is not a universal one-step rule.

**Standard ‚Äî Rock Classification, Characteristics & the Rock Cycle (Majid Husain).**

‚úÖ Majid Husain classifies igneous rocks by texture and composition ‚Äî basalt, gabbro, andesite, diorite, rhyolite, granite ‚Äî and by mode of occurrence into intrusive (plutonic) and extrusive (volcanic). Igneous rock is the most important original source of the inorganic mineral matter that later forms sedimentary rock ‚Äî so the three families are genetically linked in the rock cycle.
- ‚úÖ Sedimentary characteristics: derived from pre-existing rock (igneous the chief source) + organic matter; always layered/stratified.
- ‚úÖ Sub-aerially exposed riverine sedimentary rocks develop polygonal desiccation cracks (mud cracks) on drying.
- ‚úÖ Glacial sedimentary deposits = moraines; wind-laid fine silt = loess (e.g. northern China).
- ‚úÖ Metamorphic rocks form from pre-existing rock by change in temperature, pressure and chemical action of fluids ‚Äî altering texture AND mineral composition.
- ‚úÖ Primary porosity is generally low in crystalline metamorphic rocks, but joints, fractures and
  weathered zones may provide **secondary permeability**‚Äîimportant for groundwater and foundations.
- ‚úÖ Gneiss shows granular quartz-feldspar banding; slate shows slaty cleavage ‚Äî both aid field identification.

## 4. Must-Know Facts (Prelims)


**Foundation ‚Äî The Three Rock Families (G.C. Leong).**
- ‚úÖ Igneous = cooled magma; plutonic (large crystals) vs volcanic (small crystals).
- ‚úÖ Basalt = extrusive ‚Üí Deccan Traps; columnar basalt = Giant's Causeway (Antrim).
- ‚úÖ Sedimentary rocks = STRATIFIED (layered), mostly water-laid.
- ‚úÖ Limestone/chalk = organically formed (corals, shells); sandstone = mechanically formed.
- ‚úÖ Metamorphic key pairs: limestone‚Üímarble, sandstone‚Üíquartzite, coal‚Üígraphite.
- ‚úÖ Granite‚Üígneiss; shale‚Üíschist; clay‚Üíslate.

**Standard ‚Äî Rock Classification, Characteristics & the Rock Cycle (Majid Husain).**
- ‚úÖ Igneous rocks classified by TEXTURE + COMPOSITION (basalt, gabbro, andesite, diorite, rhyolite, granite).
- ‚úÖ Igneous rock = chief original source of sedimentary mineral matter.
- ‚úÖ Slate = low-grade metamorphism of shale (slaty cleavage).
- ‚úÖ Gneiss = high-grade regional metamorphism; banded quartz-feldspar.
- ‚úÖ Crystalline rocks commonly store groundwater in joints, fractures and weathered mantles rather than
  in abundant primary pore space.
- ‚úÖ Loess = wind-deposited silt; moraine = glacial sediment.

## 5. UPSC Traps

> üîë Trap: Do not confuse similar-looking landforms, processes, scales or Indian locations; UPSC often tests the exact mechanism and setting.


**Foundation ‚Äî The Three Rock Families (G.C. Leong).**
- ‚ùå Basalt cools slowly to form large crystals. ‚Üí Basalt is EXTRUSIVE ‚Üí rapid cooling ‚Üí small crystals; granite (plutonic) has large crystals.
- ‚ùå All sedimentary rocks come from river sand. ‚Üí They can be mechanical (sandstone), organic (limestone/chalk) or chemical; sediment may be river-, wind-, glacier- or organism-derived.
- ‚ùå Marble is an igneous rock. ‚Üí Marble is METAMORPHIC ‚Äî from limestone under heat & pressure.

**Standard ‚Äî Rock Classification, Characteristics & the Rock Cycle (Majid Husain).**
- ‚ùå Foliation/cleavage and stratification are the same. ‚Üí Foliation (slate, schist) is a metamorphic alignment; stratification is sedimentary layering ‚Äî different origins.
- ‚ùå Metamorphic-rock permeability is fixed by rock name alone. ‚Üí Fracturing, jointing and weathering
  often control secondary permeability more than the unfractured rock matrix.
- ‚ùå Source rock of every metamorphic rock is obvious. ‚Üí For quartzite/marble it is clear; for schist and gneiss the parent is often hard to determine.
