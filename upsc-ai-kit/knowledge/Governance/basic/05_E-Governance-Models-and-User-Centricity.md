## 1. Visual foundation

```text
FIVE MODELS OF E-GOVERNANCE (standard Indian public-administration typology)
1. BROADCASTING MODEL          -- disseminate information widely (govt websites, portals)
2. CRITICAL FLOW MODEL         -- route information of critical value to a targeted
                                   audience or wider public (e.g., exposing a local
                                   service failure to affected citizens/watchdogs)
3. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS MODEL  -- let citizens compare govt performance/services
                                   (e.g., published service-delivery dashboards)
4. MOBILISATION & NETWORKING   -- use digital tools for e-advocacy, citizen
   MODEL                          mobilisation and networked civil-society action
5. INTERACTIVE SERVICE MODEL   -- two-way, transactional service delivery with
                                   built-in transparency/accountability feedback
                                   (2024 PYQ focus)
```

**Core proposition:** ✅ E-governance is not one uniform activity. These five models classify
different information and interaction strategies; they are **not an official chronological
maturity ladder**. A project may combine several models, while the 2024 PYQ specifically tests
the citizen-facing, two-way **Interactive Service Model**.

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **E-governance** | The application of information and communication technology to government processes, spanning G2C, G2B, G2G and G2E interactions, to improve service delivery, transparency and efficiency. |
| ✅ **Interactive Service Model** | The e-governance model in which citizens do not merely receive information but transact directly with government (applying, paying, tracking, appealing online), with the interaction itself designed to embed transparency and accountability — not simply digitise a paper process. |
| ✅ **Broadcasting/Critical Flow/Comparative Analysis/Mobilisation Models** | The other four classical models: one-way wide dissemination; targeted critical/emergency information flow; citizen-facing comparative performance data; and civil-society digital mobilisation/advocacy respectively — together with the Interactive Service Model, they form the standard five-model e-governance typology. |
| ✅ **NeSDA** | DARPG's assessment framework. NeSDA 2021's core portal parameters are accessibility, content availability, ease of use, information security/privacy, end-service delivery, integrated-service delivery and status/request tracking. NeSDA-Way Forward separately monitors saturation of identified mandatory e-services and disseminates best practices; do not merge every related e-governance indicator into one fixed parameter list. |
| ⚠️ **User-centric design vs technology/back-end-integration bias** | User-centric design starts from the citizen's task and journey; a technology/back-end bias starts from the convenience of digitising existing departmental workflows and IT architecture — the 2025 PYQ tests exactly this built-in tension. |

## 3. How e-governance functions (mechanism)

1. **Digitisation first:** paper records and processes are converted to digital form
   (the precondition, not the goal, of e-governance).
2. **Model selection:** depending on the objective — informing, alerting, benchmarking,
   mobilising or transacting — government deploys one or more of the five models.
3. **Interactive Service Model in practice:** citizens submit applications, make payments,
   track status and receive services end-to-end online, with the platform itself logging
   timelines and enabling grievance escalation — this model is where transparency and
   accountability are structurally embedded into the transaction, not merely publicised.
4. **Assessment and improvement:** NeSDA benchmarks how well government portals across
   levels actually deliver services end-to-end (not just inform), incentivising states/
   ministries to move from informational to fully transactional service delivery.
5. **User-centricity risk:** without deliberate user-journey design, e-governance projects
   default to digitising existing back-end departmental workflows and IT systems, producing
   portals that are technically functional but difficult for an ordinary citizen to navigate.

## 4. Institutions and tools

- ✅ **Digital India programme (MeitY):** the umbrella Union government programme to
  transform India into a digitally empowered society, spanning digital infrastructure,
  governance-on-demand and digital empowerment of citizens.
- ✅ **NeSDA (DARPG):** periodic assessment plus the continuing NeSDA-Way Forward
  implementation-monitoring exercise; it should not be described as guaranteed biennial.
- ⚠️ **State/central service portals:** the practical delivery layer where the Interactive
  Service Model and user-centric design are actually tested (e.g., single-window
  license/certificate portals — see `07` for the institutional citizen-centric-administration
  angle).

## 5. Indian applications and examples

- ✅ 2024 GS-II PYQ (Q18) directly asks for an evaluation of the "Interactive Service Model"
  of e-governance in the context of transparency and accountability, not merely digital
  technology application — the model's defining feature (two-way, accountable transaction)
  must be the analytical centre of the answer.
- ✅ 2025 GS-II PYQ (Q7) directly tests the "built-in bias towards technology and back-end
  integration" versus user-centric design in e-governance projects — a design-critique
  question requiring examples of both the bias and correctives.
- ⚠️ An online land-record mutation portal that lets a citizen track status and appeal delays
  illustrates the Interactive Service Model; the same portal designed around the revenue
  department's internal database structure rather than the citizen's actual task sequence
  illustrates the back-end-integration bias.

## 6. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ The five classical e-governance models are Broadcasting, Critical Flow, Comparative
  Analysis, Mobilisation and Networking, and Interactive Service.
- ✅ NeSDA is administered by DARPG and assesses e-governance service delivery using
  parameters including accessibility, end-service delivery, integrated service delivery,
  status tracking, open government data and e-participation.
- ✅ Digital India is the umbrella MeitY programme under which most Union e-governance
  digital infrastructure initiatives are organised.

## 7. UPSC traps

- ❌ E-governance is only about putting government information online. -> That is only the
  Broadcasting Model; the Interactive Service Model requires full two-way transactional
  delivery with built-in accountability.
- ❌ Digitisation and e-governance are the same thing. -> Digitisation (converting paper to
  digital) is a precondition; e-governance requires redesigning the service/process itself
  around the citizen, not just storing it digitally.
- ❌ Technology adoption alone guarantees citizen-centric outcomes. -> The 2025 PYQ's own
  framing shows e-governance projects can be technically advanced yet biased toward
  back-end/technology convenience rather than the user's actual journey.
- ❌ NeSDA measures only website availability. -> NeSDA explicitly assesses whether a service
  is *completed* online (end-service delivery), not merely whether information about it is
  published.

## 9. PYQ application

- ✅ 2024 Q18: structure the answer as (a) define the Interactive Service Model precisely,
  distinguishing it from mere digital application of technology; (b) show how its
  multifarious interactions (application, payment, tracking, grievance) structurally embed
  transparency and accountability; (c) give a concrete Indian service-portal example;
  (d) note limitations (digital literacy, connectivity, interface design) as a balancing
  point.
- ✅ 2025 Q7: structure the answer as (a) state the built-in bias claim precisely (technology/
  back-end convenience over citizen journey design); (b) give an example of the bias and an
  example of a corrective (citizen-journey mapping, usability testing, vernacular-language
  interfaces); (c) recommend NeSDA-style ease-of-use and accessibility benchmarking as the
  institutional corrective.
- ⚠️ Test inclusion with assisted/offline access, disability accessibility, vernacular
  interfaces, low-bandwidth design, reasoned rejection and a human appeal channel.

## 10. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Always name the specific e-governance model (of the five) a question or example refers
  to before analysing it — examiners reward precise model identification.
- ⚠️ Use the "digitisation is not e-governance" distinction to open any answer criticising a
  government digital platform.
- ⚠️ Cross-link NeSDA's end-service-delivery and ease-of-use parameters directly to the
  user-centricity debate whenever a question asks for measurable improvement.

> **Answer thesis:** E-governance ranges from one-way information broadcasting to fully
> interactive, accountable service transactions; genuine citizen-centric e-governance is
> achieved only when platform design starts from the citizen's task and journey, not from the
> convenience of digitising existing departmental back-end systems.
