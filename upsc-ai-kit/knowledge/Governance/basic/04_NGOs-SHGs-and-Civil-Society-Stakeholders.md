**Core proposition:** ✅ Civil-society organisations (NGOs, SHGs, CSOs) generate "social
capital" — networks of trust and reciprocity — that state institutions can either draw on as
a governance resource or view with suspicion as a check on/challenge to state authority; how
the state frames CSOs shapes how effectively that social capital is used for development.

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Social capital** | Networks, norms of reciprocity and trust that enable collective action; commonly disaggregated (Putnam) into bonding (within a homogenous group), bridging (across different groups) and linking (between citizens/groups and formal institutions, including the state). |
| ✅ **Self-Help Group (SHG)** | A small, voluntary group (predominantly of women in the Indian rural-livelihoods context) that pools savings, accesses credit and undertakes livelihood activity collectively — the core institutional unit of DAY-NRLM. |
| ✅ **DAY-NRLM** | The Deendayal Antyodaya Yojana - National Rural Livelihoods Mission, run by the Ministry of Rural Development, which mobilises rural women into SHGs and federates them into village organisations and cluster-level federations for livelihoods, financial inclusion and social empowerment. |
| ✅ **FCRA** | The Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010 (amended 2020), administered by the Ministry of Home Affairs, which regulates the acceptance and utilisation of foreign contributions by associations/NGOs, requiring registration or prior permission before receiving foreign funds. |
| ✅ **NGO Darpan** | NITI Aayog's identification/database portal. Its Unique ID is required for Union-government grants and is used in FCRA/tax/banking compliance interfaces; it is not itself incorporation, FCRA permission or tax exemption. |
| ✅ **Pressure group** | An organised interest that seeks to influence public policy without itself seeking to form the government. Its governance role is representation, agenda-setting, expertise and scrutiny; risks include unequal access, opaque lobbying and policy capture. |
| ⚠️ **Non-state actor vs anti-State actor framing** | CSOs are, by definition, non-state actors (not part of formal government); whether a specific CSO is perceived/treated as adversarial ("anti-State") depends on its relationship to government policy and its funding/accountability transparency, not on its non-state status itself. |

## 3. How civil society contributes to governance (mechanism)

1. **Mobilisation:** SHGs and NGOs organise otherwise-dispersed citizens (especially women
   and marginalised groups) into collective units capable of accessing credit, markets and
   government schemes.
2. **Social capital as governance infrastructure:** bonding capital within an SHG builds
   trust for internal savings/credit; bridging capital across SHGs (federations) enables
   scale; linking capital connects federations to banks, markets and government departments.
3. **Empowerment and gender equity:** women's participation in SHGs builds collective
   bargaining power, financial literacy and public voice — directly advancing empowerment
   and gender equity, the exact link tested in the 2025 PYQ.
4. **Advocacy and watchdog function:** civil-society organisations also monitor government
   performance, advocate policy change and highlight rights violations — a function some
   governments perceive as oppositional rather than collaborative.
5. **Regulatory gate for foreign funding:** any NGO seeking foreign contributions must clear
   FCRA registration/prior permission, which the state uses to screen for national-security
   and public-interest concerns — a regulatory chokepoint that shapes which CSOs can operate
   at scale with international funding.
6. **Policy influence:** pressure groups use representation, research, petitions, campaigns,
   consultation and litigation to shape policy. The 2025 GS-II environmental-pressure-group
   PYQ requires evaluating both democratic voice and capture/representation deficits.

## 4. Institutions and tools

- ✅ **DAY-NRLM (Ministry of Rural Development):** mobilises rural women into SHGs, provides
  revolving funds and community investment support, and links SHG federations to bank
  credit, covering crores of rural women households across the large majority of Indian
  states/UTs. No fixed household or SHG count is asserted here, since the NRLM MIS portal's
  cumulative mobilisation figure rises continuously as the programme expands; the durable
  fact is the programme's near-universal state/UT coverage and its three-tier SHG ->
  Village Organisation -> Cluster-Level Federation design (see Section 4 of the advanced
  companion).
- ✅ **FCRA, 2010 (amended 2020):** registration/prior permission requirement, a designated
  FCRA bank account (State Bank of India, New Delhi main branch, per the 2020 amendment
  framework), and restrictions on administrative-expense proportions and sub-granting of
  foreign funds between FCRA-registered entities.
- ✅ **NGO Darpan (NITI Aayog):** voluntary/mandatory-for-grants registration portal that
  assigns a unique ID used across government-grant and linked compliance processes.
- ⚠️ **Village Organisations and Cluster-Level Federations** under NRLM aggregate SHGs
  upward, building the bridging/linking capital layer described above.

## 5. Indian applications and examples

- ✅ 2025 GS-II PYQ (Q6): "Women's social capital complements in advancing empowerment and
  gender equity. Explain" — the expected answer links SHG-based bonding/bridging capital
  directly to financial inclusion, collective bargaining and public voice for women.
- ✅ 2025 GS-II PYQ (Q8): "Civil Society Organizations are often perceived as being anti-State
  actors than non-State actors. Do you agree? Justify" — tests the non-state/anti-State
  framing distinction: the correct approach argues CSOs are structurally non-state actors,
  and any "anti-State" perception arises from advocacy/watchdog friction with government
  policy or from funding-transparency concerns (FCRA-related), not from an inherent
  anti-State character.
- ✅ 2025 GS-II PYQ (Q15): environmental pressure groups — analyse awareness, expertise,
  agenda-setting and legal/policy influence, then qualify with transparency, representational
  legitimacy and capture risks.
- ⚠️ A women's SHG federation negotiating directly with a bank for a cluster-level credit
  line demonstrates linking social capital converting into tangible financial inclusion.

## 6. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ DAY-NRLM is implemented by the Ministry of Rural Development and predominantly mobilises
  rural women into SHGs, federated into village organisations and cluster-level federations.
- ✅ FCRA, 2010 (amended 2020) is administered by the Ministry of Home Affairs and mandates
  registration/prior permission for any association receiving foreign contributions.
- ✅ NGO Darpan is a NITI Aayog portal that assigns NGOs a unique identification number, used
  in accessing government grants.
- ✅ Putnam's social capital typology distinguishes bonding, bridging and linking capital.

## 7. UPSC traps

- ❌ Social capital and financial capital are the same thing. -> Social capital refers to
  networks/trust/reciprocity enabling collective action; it can facilitate access to
  financial capital but is conceptually distinct from it.
- ❌ All civil-society organisations are "anti-State" by definition. -> CSOs are non-state
  actors by definition; adversarial perception arises from specific advocacy/policy friction
  or funding-transparency concerns, not from their non-state status itself.
- ❌ FCRA regulates all NGO funding. -> FCRA specifically regulates *foreign* contributions;
  domestic donations/CSR funding to NGOs are governed by separate frameworks (Companies Act,
  Income Tax Act — see `03`).
- ❌ NGO Darpan registration is legally identical to FCRA registration. -> NGO Darpan is a
  NITI Aayog identification/database portal (relevant mainly for government grants); FCRA
  registration is a separate Ministry of Home Affairs legal requirement specifically for
  foreign funding.
- ❌ Pressure groups and political parties are identical. -> Pressure groups influence policy
  without contesting for governmental power as their defining purpose.

## 9. PYQ application

- ✅ 2025 Q6: build the answer around bonding capital (internal SHG trust/savings), bridging
  capital (federation-level scale) and linking capital (bank/market/government access), each
  explicitly tied to empowerment and gender equity outcomes.
- ✅ 2025 Q8: take a reasoned position — argue that CSOs are structurally non-state, not
  anti-State, actors, while acknowledging that specific advocacy positions or funding
  opacity can generate state suspicion; avoid a purely one-sided "CSOs are always
  benign/always adversarial" answer.
- ✅ 2025 Q15: use **R-I-S-K** — representation, information/expertise, scrutiny, and capture
  risk — to assess an environmental pressure group rather than merely listing organisations.

## 10. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Use the bonding/bridging/linking social-capital framework as the default structuring
  device for any SHG or civil-society empowerment question.
- ⚠️ Where a question invites you to "agree or disagree," take an explicit position and
  justify it with both a supporting and a limiting example — examiners reward reasoned
  judgment over fence-sitting.
- ⚠️ Distinguish civil society's collaborative/service-delivery role (implementation partner)
  from its advocacy/watchdog role (independent check) — both are legitimate but generate
  different kinds of state response.

> **Answer thesis:** Civil-society organisations are non-state, not anti-State, actors whose
> bonding, bridging and linking social capital can be either harnessed as governance
> infrastructure or treated with suspicion — the difference lies in the transparency of their
> funding and the state's willingness to engage advocacy as feedback rather than opposition.
