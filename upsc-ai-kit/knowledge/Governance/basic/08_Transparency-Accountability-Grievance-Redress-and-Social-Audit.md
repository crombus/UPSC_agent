**Core proposition:** ✅ Transparency alone (publishing data) does not produce accountability;
accountability requires an identifiable authority that must answer, and an enforceable
mechanism that produces correction — social audit, structured grievance redress and
proactive disclosure are three distinct instruments that, together, convert visibility into
genuine answerability and remedy.

## 2. Essential definitions

| Concept | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Social audit** | A participatory public process in which a government scheme's records, expenditure and outcomes are examined jointly by the implementing agency and the community of beneficiaries/citizens, typically through public hearings ("jan sunwai"), to verify whether stated work/spending actually occurred. |
| ✅ **MGNREGA Section 17** | The Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, 2005 statutorily mandates regular social audits of all works executed under the scheme by the Gram Sabha, making social audit a legal requirement rather than a discretionary practice for this specific programme. |
| ✅ **Meghalaya Community Participation and Public Services Social Audit Act, 2017** | A state law giving statutory backing to social audits of public programmes/services more broadly (beginning with schemes such as MGNREGS), establishing Social Audit Units, provision for independent social auditors, community participation in the audit process, and mandated follow-up action on audit findings — one of the few Indian states with dedicated, generalised social-audit legislation. |
| ✅ **Grievance redress** | The institutional process by which a citizen's specific complaint about a public service/entitlement is registered, tracked, escalated and (ideally) resolved — operationalised nationally through CPGRAMS and various sector-specific mechanisms. |
| ✅ **Proactive disclosure** | Publishing information about government functioning, budgets, decisions and organisational structure without waiting for a specific information request — a preventive transparency measure distinct from RTI's demand-driven disclosure. |
| ⚠️ **Non-RTI accountability** | Accountability instruments (social audit, structured grievance redress, proactive disclosure obligations) that operate independently of, and do not require, an individual filing an RTI application — RTI's detailed statutory/institutional mechanics (CIC/SIC, appeals) are a separate, Polity/Ethics-linked topic. |

## 3. How social audit and grievance redress function (mechanism)

1. **Record verification:** social audit compares official muster rolls/expenditure records
   against actual work done and wages paid, using community knowledge to detect discrepancy.
2. **Public hearing:** findings are presented at a public forum (jan sunwai) where officials
   must respond in front of the affected community — creating direct, face-to-face
   answerability distinct from a written RTI response.
3. **Statutory backing converts audit into obligation:** MGNREGA Section 17 and the
   Meghalaya Act, 2017 make social audit a *mandated*, recurring process with institutional
   follow-up requirements, not a one-off civil-society initiative.
4. **Grievance escalation:** CPGRAMS and sector-specific grievance channels allow an
   individual complaint (distinct from a collective social audit) to be logged, tracked and
   escalated up the administrative hierarchy.
5. **Proactive disclosure reduces grievance volume upstream:** publishing budgets,
   beneficiary lists and decision rationales before citizens must ask reduces the number of
   grievances that arise purely from information asymmetry.

## 4. Institutions and tools

- ✅ **Gram Sabha (statutory social auditor under MGNREGA Section 17)** — the Act designates
  the Gram Sabha as the body to conduct social audits of works in its area.
- ✅ **Social Audit Units (Meghalaya Act, 2017 model)** — dedicated state/district-level units
  established to facilitate, conduct and supervise social audits, with provision for
  independent social auditors distinct from the implementing department itself.
- ✅ **CPGRAMS** — the Union online grievance-redress and monitoring portal (see `07` for its
  role within Sevottam specifically; here the focus is its role in the wider accountability
  ecosystem beyond Citizens' Charters).
- ⚠️ **Social Audit Units under MGNREGA nationally** — many states have established
  dedicated, functionally independent Social Audit Units (sometimes called State Social
  Audit Societies) to implement the Section 17 mandate, following broader central
  guidelines.

## 5. Indian applications and examples

- ⚠️ An MGNREGA social audit uncovering "ghost" job-card entries (wages recorded as paid to
  workers who did not actually work) illustrates the record-verification function directly.
- ⚠️ A Meghalaya-style Social Audit Unit conducting a jan sunwai on a public-service delivery
  programme (beyond MGNREGA) illustrates the generalised, statutorily backed social-audit
  model extending past the single-scheme MGNREGA requirement.
- ⚠️ A citizen escalating a delayed pension-disbursal grievance through CPGRAMS, with the
  portal logging response time against the department, illustrates individual grievance
  redress operating in parallel to (not instead of) collective social audit.

## 6. Must-Know Facts for Prelims

- ✅ Section 17 of the MGNREGA, 2005 makes social audit of all works under the scheme a
  statutory requirement, with the Gram Sabha as the designated social-audit body.
- ✅ The Meghalaya Community Participation and Public Services Social Audit Act, 2017 gives
  statutory backing to social audits of public programmes, establishing Social Audit Units
  and providing for independent social auditors and community participation.
- ✅ CPGRAMS is the Centralised Public Grievance Redress and Monitoring System.
- ✅ Proactive disclosure is distinct from RTI's request-driven disclosure model.

## 7. UPSC traps

- ❌ Social audit is a purely voluntary civil-society practice with no legal basis. -> Under
  MGNREGA Section 17 and the Meghalaya Act, 2017, social audit is a statutory obligation,
  not a discretionary civil-society initiative.
- ❌ Transparency (publishing information) automatically produces accountability. -> As the
  Master Framework notes, accountability additionally requires an identifiable authority to
  answer and an enforceable remedy — transparency alone can reveal failure without fixing it.
- ❌ Social audit and RTI are the same accountability mechanism. -> Social audit is a
  participatory, collective, scheme-specific verification process; RTI is an individual,
  demand-driven information-request mechanism (RTI's detailed mechanics are Ethics/
  Polity-owned — see study links).
- ❌ CPGRAMS is the only grievance-redress channel in India. -> Multiple sector-specific
  grievance mechanisms (banking ombudsman, consumer forums, scheme-specific helplines)
  operate alongside CPGRAMS; CPGRAMS is the principal Union cross-department portal, not the
  sole channel.

## 10. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Always distinguish social audit (participatory, collective, scheme-specific) from RTI
  (individual, request-driven) and from grievance redress (individual complaint tracking) —
  a strong answer names all three and states which is being tested.
- ⚠️ Use MGNREGA Section 17 as the strongest available example of *statutorily mandated*
  social audit, and the Meghalaya Act, 2017 as the strongest example of *generalised*
  state-level social-audit legislation beyond a single scheme.
- ⚠️ Recommend statutory backing (Meghalaya-style) as the corrective wherever a question
  describes social audit as informal, ad hoc, or without institutional follow-up.

> **Answer thesis:** Genuine accountability requires more than transparency — social audit
> converts visibility into public, community-verified answerability, and its statutory
> backing under MGNREGA Section 17 and generalised state legislation like Meghalaya's 2017
> Act is what elevates it from a voluntary civil-society practice into an enforceable
> governance instrument.
