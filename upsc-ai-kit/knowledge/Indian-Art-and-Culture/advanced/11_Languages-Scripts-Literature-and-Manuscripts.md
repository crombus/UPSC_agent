## 1. Analytical question and thesis

**Question:** What does the Sanskrit-versus-Prakrit social division in
classical Indian drama reveal about language as a marker of social
hierarchy, and how should a manuscript's UNESCO inscription year be used
as evidence distinct from the text's original composition date?
**Thesis:** ⚠️ The documented convention that "high-varna characters spoke
Sanskrit while women and Shudras spoke Prakrit" in Gupta-period plays
(*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 584) is direct evidence that language register
functioned as an *on-stage marker of social hierarchy*, not merely a
linguistic-historical curiosity — and this same evidentiary discipline
(distinguishing a text's *composition date* from its *modern
recognition/inscription date*) must be applied to UNESCO Memory of the
World entries, where, for example, the Rigveda's 2007 inscription date is
centuries removed from its actual ancient composition.

## 2. Advanced vocabulary

| Term | Precise meaning |
|---|---|
| ⚠️ **Language as social marker (on-stage convention)** | The Sanskrit/Prakrit character-speech convention in classical drama is a *textual* record of a *social* hierarchy — elite/Brahmanical characters coded through elite language, subordinate characters (women, Shudras) coded through vernacular language — usable as direct evidence in any question on language and social stratification. |
| ✅ **Composition-date vs. inscription-date distinction** | A manuscript's actual age (e.g. the Rigveda's ancient composition) is analytically distinct from its UNESCO Memory of the World *inscription* year (2007 for the Rigveda) — the inscription date marks international documentary-heritage recognition, not the text's age (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 621). |
| ⚠️ **Manuscript ecology** | The range of Memory of the World entries — from religious/philosophical texts (Bhagavad Gita, Natyashastra, Rigveda) to secular/administrative archives (Dutch East India Company records) and even folk-performance documentation (Saang Lok Collection) — shows India's documentary heritage spans religious, aesthetic, administrative and folk-performance registers simultaneously, not a single "ancient scripture" category. |
| ⚠️ **Text-layer discipline** | Oral formation, composition, redaction, earliest surviving manuscript, modern critical edition and UNESCO inscription are different dates. An epic or Sangam work rarely has one uncontested "publication year." |

## 3. Comparative framework

| Text/entry | Register/type | Inscription year | Composition-era vs. inscription-year gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ Rigveda | Ancient Vedic Sanskrit, religious | 2007 | Very large gap (ancient composition, modern recognition) |
| ✅ Panchatantra | Classical Sanskrit, didactic/fable literature | 2024 | Large gap (ancient/early-medieval composition, very recent recognition) |
| ✅ Ramcharitmanas | Awadhi, devotional retelling of Ramayana (Tulsidas) | 2024 | Medieval composition (16th century), recent recognition |
| ✅ Bhagavad Gita manuscripts | Sanskrit, philosophical | 2025 | Ancient composition, most recent recognition |
| ✅ Archives of the Dutch East India Company | Colonial administrative record | 2003 | Colonial-era composition, comparatively small gap |

⚠️ **Analytical point:** the inscription-year sequence (1997 to 2025) does
not track the texts' chronological composition order at all — a 2025
inscription (Bhagavad Gita) can cover a far older text than a 2003
inscription (Dutch colonial archives). Any Mains answer must therefore
cite **both** dates separately and never imply that later UNESCO
recognition means later composition.

## 4. Evidence, form and interpretation

✅ The book's explicit statement of the Sanskrit/Prakrit speech convention
in Gupta-period drama is a rare case in this folder of a **directly
documented linguistic marker of social hierarchy** — usable evidence for
any question connecting language, literature and social structure, and
distinct from an inference the answer would otherwise have to construct
unaided. ⚠️ Reading the Memory of the World list as a corpus (Section 3):
its range across devotional (Bhagavad Gita), aesthetic-theoretical
(Natyashastra, Sahrdayaloka-Locana, Abhinavagupta manuscripts), didactic-
narrative (Panchatantra), regional-devotional (Ramcharitmanas), Buddhist
(Gilgit Manuscript, Maitreyavyakarana), Jain (Shantinatha Charitra), and
colonial-administrative (Dutch East India Company archives) registers
demonstrates that India's "documentary heritage," in UNESCO's own
framing, is not limited to a single religious or literary genre — an
advanced answer should name at least two different registers to
demonstrate this range rather than listing only well-known religious
texts.

✅ R.S. Sharma treats Sangam texts as powerful but bounded evidence:
compiled c. CE 300-600 with earlier layers, they illuminate towns, trade,
crafts, war, social ranking and hero-stone culture, but do not provide a
coherent dynastic chronicle. Satish Chandra similarly shows medieval
Persian, Sanskrit and regional languages interacting through translation,
Bhakti, court patronage and bilingual authors. "Vernacular rise" therefore
means expanded literary publics, not the disappearance of Sanskrit or
Persian.

## 9. ❌ Attribution, chronology and numeric traps

- ❌ Treating a Memory of the World inscription year as the text's
  composition date. -> These are entirely separate events; always cite
  both where relevant.
- ❌ Assuming the Sanskrit/Prakrit on-stage convention applied uniformly
  across all periods and genres of Indian drama. -> The book documents
  this specifically for Gupta-period plays; do not generalise it
  unqualified to all Indian theatre across all eras (cross-reference
  topic 10 for theatre's own historical development).
- ❌ Giving a single exact date to a layered oral/textual corpus. ->
  Distinguish composition strata, compilation/redaction and surviving
  manuscripts.
- ❌ Describing regional-language growth as a zero-sum victory over
  Sanskrit/Persian. -> Satish Chandra documents coexistence, translation
  and mutual influence.
- ❌ Treating India's Memory of the World entries as exclusively religious
  texts. -> The list includes colonial administrative archives and
  folk-performance documentation alongside religious/philosophical texts.

## 10. Mains answer frameworks

**Central thesis:** Language and manuscript questions should be argued
through documented evidence (the Sanskrit/Prakrit on-stage social-
hierarchy convention) and through a disciplined separation of a text's
composition date from its modern recognition date (Memory of the World
inscription year).

1. **Cite the Sanskrit/Prakrit on-stage convention** directly wherever a
   question connects language and social hierarchy.
2. **Always separate composition date from inscription/recognition
   date** when citing a Memory of the World entry.
3. **Name at least two different registers** (religious, administrative,
   aesthetic, folk) from the Memory of the World list to demonstrate
   range rather than repeating only the most famous entries.
4. **Cross-reference topic 03's Pallava dossier** for the Dandin/
   *Avantisundari Katha* literary-evidence point.
5. **Close with a current-verification caveat** on the exact entry count,
   since UNESCO's list is periodically updated.
