## 1. Analytical question and thesis

**Question:** What can Harappan built form actually prove about Harappan
society, and where must a rigorous answer stop short of inference the
evidence cannot support?
**Thesis:** ⚠️ Harappan urbanism is best argued through *what its
architecture demonstrates directly* (standardisation, civic engineering,
functional zoning) rather than through speculative claims about religion,
kingship or social hierarchy that the physical evidence alone cannot
confirm — the book's own explicit denial of confirmed "temples or palaces"
(*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 33) is the model for this evidentiary discipline.

## 2. Advanced vocabulary

| Term | Precise meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Functional zoning** | The citadel/lower-town split is a functional (administrative/public vs. residential) division inferred from building type and elevation, not a confirmed caste or varna hierarchy. |
| ⚠️ **Standardisation as state capacity** | Uniform brick dimensions and a shared grid layout across geographically distant sites (Harappa, Mohenjo-daro, and others) imply some coordinating authority or shared technical tradition — the precise nature of that authority (centralised state vs. shared urban culture) remains debated. |
| ✅ **Ritual cleansing inference** | The Great Bath's scale and design "indicate the importance of ritualistic cleansing in their culture" (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 34) — this is the book's own inferential (not directly attested) reading of the structure's likely social function. |
| ⚠️ **Absence of monumental religious/royal architecture** | A negative finding (no confirmed temple/palace) is itself analytically significant — it distinguishes Harappan civic ideology from contemporaneous Egyptian/Mesopotamian god-king monumentalism, without implying the Harappans lacked religion or authority. |

## 3. Comparative framework

| Dimension | Harappan civilisation | Contemporary Egypt/Mesopotamia | Analytical point |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ Monumental religious/royal structures | Not confirmed (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 33) | Pyramids, ziggurats, palace-temple complexes attested | ⚠️ Suggests a different (or archaeologically less visible) expression of authority and religion, not its absence |
| ✅ Civic engineering emphasis | Drainage, granaries, standardised bricks | Present but typically subordinate to monumental building programmes | ⚠️ Harappan civic investment appears comparatively weighted toward shared infrastructure over individual monument-building |
| ✅ Planning regularity | Strict grid, right-angle roads | Variable; often organised around temple/palace axis | Harappan planning is unusually systematic for its period |

## 4. Evidence, form and interpretation

✅ The book itself models a careful evidentiary boundary: it reports the
citadel's granaries, pillared halls and Great Bath as facts of form, and
explicitly notes only that some citadel buildings "might have been" elite
residences — using conditional language rather than asserting it as
settled (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 33). ⚠️ The correct advanced-answer technique
is to reproduce this same evidentiary caution: describe what the form
demonstrates (engineering competence, standardisation, functional
zoning), and flag as inference anything about religion, kingship or
rigid social hierarchy that the structures alone cannot prove. ✅ The
"Meluha" identification and Mesopotamian seal finds (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p.
35) are used as external corroboration of Harappan long-distance trade
contact — a rare case where an external source (Mesopotamian references)
supports an inference about the civilisation's economic reach.

## 9. ❌ Attribution, chronology and numeric traps

- ❌ Treating "might have been the residence of rulers and aristocrats"
  (book's own conditional language, PDF p. 33) as a confirmed fact. ->
  The book itself hedges this; an advanced answer should preserve that
  hedge, not convert it into certainty.
- ❌ Asserting a specific political structure (e.g. "a centralised Harappan
  empire") from standardisation alone. -> Standardisation supports an
  inference of *some* coordinating mechanism; it does not by itself prove
  a specific state form.
- ❌ Treating the absence of temples/palaces as evidence of an areligious
  or apolitical society. -> It indicates a different or less
  monumentally-visible expression, not absence.

## 10. Mains answer frameworks

**Central thesis:** Harappan architecture should be argued as an advanced
civic-engineering achievement demonstrated directly by its evidence, with
explicit evidentiary caution around any claim (temple, palace, rigid
hierarchy) the built form alone cannot confirm.

1. **Open with the grid-plan/standardisation fact** as the strongest,
   least contestable claim.
2. **Present citadel/lower-town zoning and the Great Bath**, preserving
   the book's own conditional language on elite residence.
3. **Use the drainage system** as the clearest evidence of coordinated
   civic priority.
4. **Make the comparative point** against Egyptian/Mesopotamian
   monumentalism as the advanced-tier differentiator.
5. **Close by naming the evidentiary boundary** explicitly — what the
   architecture proves versus what remains inferential — rather than a
   generic concluding sentence.
