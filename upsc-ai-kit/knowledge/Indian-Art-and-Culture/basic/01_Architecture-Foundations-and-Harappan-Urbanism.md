## 1. Snapshot and syllabus fit

✅ This topic supplies the vocabulary every later architecture file in this
folder reuses (material, plan, elevation, function) and answers the direct
2025 Mains question on Harappan architecture. ⚠️ Its exam value is
twofold: as a stand-alone Prelims/Mains topic on the Indus Valley
Civilisation (IVC), and as the foundation for tracing how Indian
architecture evolved from Harappan urban planning through Mauryan,
temple, Indo-Islamic and colonial phases (topics 02-05).

## 2. Essential vocabulary

| Term | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Citadel** | The upraised, western part of a Harappan town, used for large-dimension buildings such as granaries, administrative buildings and pillared halls (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 33). |
| ✅ **Lower town** | The larger, lower-lying residential part of a Harappan town, containing small one-roomed houses for working-class residents alongside larger private houses (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 34). |
| ✅ **Great Bath** | The most famous public bath of the Harappan civilisation, excavated at Mohenjo-daro, surrounded by galleries and rooms (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 34). |
| ✅ **Standardised burnt brick** | Harappan builders used burnt mud bricks of standardised dimensions, laid in many layers and joined with gypsum mortar (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 33). |
| ✅ **Drainage system** | Small house drains connected to larger covered street drains, with cesspits at regular intervals, allowing regular cleaning (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 34). |
| ⚠️ **"Meluha"** | Many scholars argue Mesopotamians of the Tigris-Euphrates valley called the Indus Valley Civilisation "Meluha"; Indus seals have been found in Mesopotamia, evidencing trade contact (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 35). |

## 3. Core classification

✅ The book classifies the evolution of Indian architecture, by time, into
five broad periods that this folder's architecture topics (01-05) trace in
sequence: Harappan; Mauryan and post-Mauryan (rock-cut and stupa
architecture); temple architecture (Nagara/Dravida/Vesara); Indo-Islamic
architecture (Sultanate, provincial, Mughal, regional styles); and modern/
colonial and post-Independence architecture (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 32).

✅ Harappan town planning itself shows a threefold building classification:
dwelling houses, public buildings, and public baths (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p.
33).

## 4. Foundation narrative

✅ Harappa and Mohenjo-daro are the two major Harappan sites and "among the
earliest and finest examples of urban civic planning," with a "planned
network of roads, houses, and drainage systems" (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 32).
✅ Towns were laid out in a rectangular grid, with roads running north-south
and east-west, cutting each other at right angles (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 33).
❌ "Indus Valley Civilisation sites do not have large monumental structures
such as temples or palaces for rulers unlike the Egyptian and Mesopotamian
Civilisation" (*Nitin…pdf*, PDF p. 33) — a direct correction to any answer
that assumes Harappan cities had palace or temple complexes as settled
fact. ⚠️ Structures conventionally labelled "granaries" used raised platforms
and ventilation, but their exact function is an archaeological
interpretation rather than an inscriptionally confirmed name. ✅ Most
buildings had private wells and ventilated bathrooms, and
some lower-town houses had stairs suggesting double storeys (*Nitin…pdf*,
PDF p. 34).

✅ **Regional variation matters:** Mohenjo-daro's Great Bath and drainage,
Harappa's large brick platforms, Dholavira's fortified multi-part plan and
extensive water-storage works, Lothal's basin (conventionally called a
dockyard, with function debated), and Kalibangan's distinctive planning
show a shared urban vocabulary without identical city plans. R.S. Sharma
confirms grid planning, household bathing/drainage and Dholavira's
fortification, while also stressing that the undeciphered script sharply
limits claims about polity and belief (*India's Ancient Past*, PDF pp.
94-101).

## 7. Exact PYQ application

✅ **2025 Q1, verbatim:** "Discuss the salient features of the Harappan
architecture. (Answer in 150 words)" (`UPSC Mains 2025 GS Paper 1.txt:8-10`).
⚠️ A strong 150-word answer should: (i) open by naming the grid-plan town
layout; (ii) describe citadel/lower-town differentiation with the
granary/Great Bath example; (iii) describe the drainage system as the
civilisation's most distinctive feature; (iv) close with the standardised-
brick observation and the honest caveat that no confirmed palace or temple
structure has been identified. ❌ No 2024 Mains question directly tests this
topic.

## 9. ❌ Traps and corrections

- ❌ Harappan buildings can be confidently labelled "palaces" or "temples."
  -> The book explicitly states no such large monumental religious/royal
  structures have been confirmed, unlike Egypt or Mesopotamia.
- ❌ Harappan town planning was irregular or organic. -> It followed a
  strict rectangular grid with roads at right angles.
- ❌ The drainage system was a minor detail. -> It is repeatedly
  highlighted as the civilisation's most advanced and distinctive
  engineering feature, alongside standardised bricks.
- ❌ All Harappan houses were uniform in size and status. -> The citadel/
  lower-town division and variation in house size (including some
  double-storeyed houses) show social and functional differentiation.
- ❌ "Granary," "dockyard" and "ritual bath" are ancient Harappan labels.
  -> They are modern archaeological interpretations based on form and
  context; use "conventionally identified as" where function is debated.
- ❌ The horned figure on one seal proves a settled proto-Shiva cult. ->
  R.S. Sharma explicitly calls the Pashupati identification doubtful
  because horned deities occur elsewhere and the expected bull is absent
  (*India's Ancient Past*, PDF p. 100).

## 10. Mains framework

1. Name the site pair (Harappa, Mohenjo-daro) and the grid-plan feature
   first.
2. Describe citadel (granaries, pillared halls, Great Bath) vs. lower town
   (small houses, wells) as the core structural distinction.
3. Use the drainage system as the standout engineering example.
4. Note standardised brick and gypsum mortar as the material/technique
   detail.
5. Close with the honest caveat: no confirmed palace/temple has been
   identified, distinguishing Harappan civic architecture from
   contemporary Egyptian/Mesopotamian monumental architecture.
