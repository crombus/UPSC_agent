## 1. What this paper actually tests

✅ Both recent papers require **two essays, one topic chosen from each
of two displayed Sections**, each about **1000–1200 words** — 2024
prints "(125 × 2 = 250)"; 2025's extraction does not show a marks line,
but the two-essay, two-section structure is explicit and identical in
both years (see `README.md` for exact wording). ⚠️ Read as a skill test,
the paper rewards: accurate decoding of a compressed proposition,
disciplined multidimensional thinking, one defensible and sustained
thesis, argument tested against counter-view, safe and proportionate
evidence, coherent architecture, and a conclusion that resolves rather
than restates. ❌ It is not a GS-answer-in-disguise: headings, scheme
lists and factual stacking without a sustained argument under-use the
paper's actual test.

## 2. The eight-engine answer chain

```text
DECODE       -> keywords, definitions, implied relationship, tension, scope
EXPAND       -> dimensions: person/family/community/state/world;
                past/present/future; material/institutional/ethical/
                ecological/digital
THESIS       -> one qualified, defensible central argument (not a list of themes)
ARGUMENT MAP -> proposition -> mechanism -> illustration -> qualification -> synthesis
LENS PASS    -> governance/economy/society/science-tech/environment/
                culture-history/ethics/IR - use only lenses the thesis needs
EVIDENCE     -> one claim, one function, one verified source per illustration
STRUCTURE    -> 10-12 purposeful paragraphs: claim -> development -> link
CONCLUSION   -> return to the transformed thesis; state a feasible,
                humane, forward-looking synthesis
   |
   v
REVISION PASS -> relevance sweep -> topic-drift check -> evidence-risk
                 check -> transition check -> language check
```

Each stage has its own basic/advanced pair: decode = `02`/`03`; expand =
`04`; thesis/argument = `05`/`08`; lenses = `10`/`11`; evidence = `09`/
`12`; structure/conclusion = `06`/`07`; revision = `14`/`15`; execution
under time = `13`; practice = `16`.

## 3. Paper-selection engine

⚠️ Choice is a strategic decision, not a preference poll. Prefer the
topic where you can, within a few minutes, articulate: (a) one qualified
thesis, not just "a subject I like"; (b) a multidimensional causal
chain, not a single-cause story; (c) at least two to three credible,
non-fabricated Indian illustrations; (d) a non-forced, earned conclusion.
❌ Do not choose by "which topic sounds like my favourite GS subject" —
a topic that *feels* familiar but yields only one dimension produces a
thin essay; a topic that feels unfamiliar but decodes into a clear
tension (aphorisms especially) is often safer. Full decision matrix in
`basic/01` and `advanced/01`.

## 4. Decode engine (used before any brainstorming)

1. Identify every content word and its plain-language meaning.
2. State the implied relationship the sentence asserts (causal,
   conditional, comparative, or definitional).
3. Name the central tension the prompt sets up (e.g. destination vs.
   process; restraint vs. intervention; simplicity of idea vs.
   complexity of implementation).
4. State the scope: is this a claim about individuals, institutions,
   states, civilisations, or all of these at different points?
5. Draft one sentence that could serve as a working thesis — to be
   tested and refined in the expansion and argument stages.

Full method in `02` (quote decoding) and `03` (issue-based scoping).

## 5. Expansion engine

| Axis | Range to check |
|---|---|
| Scale of actor | Person → family/community → institution → state → global order → civilisation |
| Time | Historical origin → present manifestation → plausible future trajectory |
| Domain | Material/economic; institutional/governance; ethical/values; ecological; digital/technological; cultural |
| Direction of effect | Intended effect → unintended consequence → distributional effect (who benefits, who bears cost) |

⚠️ A prompt need not be expanded along every axis — expand until you can
support the chosen thesis with two or three well-developed dimensions,
not eight shallow ones. Full method in `04`.

## 6. Argument engine

```text
PROPOSITION  (the qualified thesis)
     |
MECHANISM    (why/how it operates - the causal or conceptual link)
     |
ILLUSTRATION (one verified, functionally relevant example)
     |
QUALIFICATION (the limit, exception, or counter-case)
     |
SYNTHESIS    (a reasoned resolution, not an unresolved "on the other hand")
```

⚠️ Repeat this chain once per major paragraph-cluster (typically 3–5
times across the essay), not once for the whole essay. Full method in
`05` (thesis/argument map) and `08` (counterargument/synthesis).

## 7. Lens engine

Use only the lenses the thesis actually needs: governance, economy,
society, science and technology, environment, culture/history, ethics,
international relations. ⚠️ A strong essay typically draws on three to
five lenses in real depth rather than a token line from all seven — a
lens without a developed mechanism and example is decorative. Full
method and scale calibration in `11`.

## 8. Evidence engine

**Rule:** one claim → one function → one verified source per
illustration; no unsupported numerical clutter. ✅ The 2024-2025 papers
supply no author names for any of their 16 prompts (see `README.md`); this
folder therefore never attaches an author to a prompt-quotation unless
independently verified by a primary/authoritative source (`09`, `16`).
❌ Quotation-dumping (stacking famous names to sound erudite) and false
precision (invented statistics) are both treated as failure modes, not
style choices — see Section 9 below and `12`.

## 9. Structure and conclusion engine

⚠️ Roughly 10–12 purposeful paragraphs for a 1000–1200-word essay, each
doing one job: claim, development (mechanism + illustration), and a link
to the next paragraph. ❌ Headings, numbered lists and GS-style
subheadings are not used in a continuous essay — they are a scaffolding
habit imported from GS answers that undercuts flow. The conclusion
**returns to the thesis in its tested, refined form** (not a verbatim
repeat of the introduction) and closes on a feasible, humane,
forward-looking synthesis rather than a slogan. Full method in `06`
(introduction/conclusion) and `07` (macro-structure/transitions).

## 10. Revision engine

Before submitting (mentally, in a timed drill, or on paper), sweep for:

1. **Relevance** — does every paragraph answer the exact prompt, not a
   nearby topic?
2. **Topic drift** — has the essay quietly become a general essay on
   (say) "technology" or "ethics" rather than the stated proposition?
3. **Evidence risk** — any invented statistic, unverifiable "latest"
   claim, or misattributed quotation?
4. **Transitions** — does each paragraph open by linking to the previous
   idea, not merely starting a new topic?
5. **Language** — precise, concise, free of ornamental vocabulary that
   obscures meaning.

Full method in `14` (language/style) and `15` (self-evaluation).

## 13. Source and risk-ledger protocol

- **Prompt wording:** quote the official/corrected ledger, not raw OCR; no
  author attribution unless independently verified against a primary/
  authoritative source (see `README.md`, `09`, `16`).
- **Familiar aphorisms found elsewhere on the web with uncertain
  attribution:** ❌ do not attribute; use only as an unattributed
  proposition, or omit.
- **Named quotations from thinkers:** use only after verifying exact
  wording, author, translation and context against a primary or
  authoritative source.
- **Current/dated facts:** cite a source and a date; if it cannot
  survive that check, omit it rather than risk false precision.
- **Refresh rule:** any illustration flagged 📰 in `12` should be
  re-checked against a current source before reuse in a live attempt,
  since a dated anchor can become stale between drafting and exam day.
