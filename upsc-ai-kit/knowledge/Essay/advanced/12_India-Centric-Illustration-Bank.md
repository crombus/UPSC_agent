## 1. Advanced proposition and boundary

**Proposition:** ⚠️ a mature illustration bank is organised by
**function**, not by subject label — the same verified example can serve
different essay functions across different prompts, provided each use
states its own claim, function and limitation afresh. **Boundary:** ❌
this topic does not replace the GS subject modules' factual depth
(Section 12 links, not duplicates) — it governs how illustrations are
selected, reused across prompts, and refreshed for currency.

## 2. Concept definition and taxonomy

- **Single-function example:** used for one specific claim only (most
  basic-file entries).
- **Multi-function example:** the same underlying fact, reused across
  different prompts with a genuinely different function each time (see
  Section 3).
- **Illustration reuse risk:** using the same example in both required
  essays, or repeatedly across practice attempts, without varying its
  stated function — signals a shallow illustration repertoire.
- **Refresh flag:** a marker on any illustration whose currency depends
  on an evolving policy situation, requiring a check against a dated
  source before live use.

## 3. Tension pairs and hidden assumptions

⚠️ Multi-function reuse, worked example — the **Right to Information
Act, 2005**:

| Prompt/context | Function |
|---|---|
| 2024-B6 "Power tests character" | Illustrates institutional accountability constraining discretionary power |
| 2025-A1 "Truth knows no color" | Illustrates a legal mechanism for equal access to official information regardless of the requester's status |
| 2024-A4 "The doubter is a true man of science" | Illustrates institutionalised scrutiny/verification as a civic analogue to scientific doubt |

⚠️ **Hidden assumption to avoid:** that reusing a familiar example
signals efficiency; if the *function* is not restated distinctly for
each prompt, the reuse reads as recycling rather than range.

## 4. Levels of analysis and temporal-spatial scale

⚠️ Classify each bank entry by the scale at which its function operates
— individual (a documented personal-conduct episode), institutional (an
Act, agency, or programme), state (a national policy stance), or
civilisational (a long-run historical pattern) — and match this to the
scale identified for the prompt in `11`, to avoid using an
institutional-scale example to illustrate a claim pitched at the
individual scale.

## 5. Stakeholder map and distributional effects

⚠️ For each illustration, note which stakeholder group is centred by the
example and which is not — e.g. the Chipko-movement illustration centres
forest-dependent community agency; it says less about, and should not be
overstated as resolving, state-level industrial-policy trade-offs. An
advanced essay states this explicitly rather than letting the example
imply a broader resolution than it supports.

## 6. Causality, mechanisms and feedback loops

```text
SELECT CANDIDATE EXAMPLE (recalled, verifiable)
        |
        v
STATE ITS MECHANISM  (what causal/structural link does it demonstrate?)
        |
        v
MATCH MECHANISM TO SPECIFIC PARAGRAPH CLAIM (05/07)
        |
        v
STATE THE LIMITATION explicitly (what it does NOT prove)
        |
        v
IF THE EXAMPLE TOUCHES A LIVE POLICY SITUATION -> apply REFRESH FLAG (13)
```
⚠️ Skipping the "match mechanism to specific claim" step is the most
common source of fact-dumping even among advanced writers who have
already memorised good examples.

## 7. Necessary/sufficient conditions and exceptions

⚠️ A verified, functionally matched illustration is necessary for a
strong body paragraph but not sufficient on its own — the mechanism and
qualification (`05`) must still be argued, not merely implied by the
example's presence. **Exception:** for very tightly reasoned aphorism
essays (e.g. 2025-A3, "thought finds a world and creates one also"), a
single, deeply developed illustration may outperform two shallower ones.

## 8. Paradox, dialectic and counterfactual test

**Paradox:** the examples that most impress on paper are often the least
"famous" ones — a precisely matched, honestly limited example
outperforms a grand but loosely connected one (e.g. invoking a major
global institution vaguely, versus a smaller, precisely relevant Indian
episode). **Counterfactual test:** "If I swapped this example for any
other broadly similar one, would my specific claim still be equally
well-supported?" If yes, the example was interchangeable filler, not a
precisely matched illustration.

## 9. Ethical conflict and synthesis

⚠️ Where an illustration involves a contested or sensitive episode,
present it with due limitation and without partisan framing — the essay
should use the example's structural/functional lesson, not take a side
in an unrelated political controversy the essay is not actually arguing
about.

## 10. Evidence/source-risk and India application

⚠️ Every entry in this bank requires confirmation against its linked
source module or a primary/authoritative source before live use. This
folder treats entries as well-known, non-statistical candidate
illustrations; it does not independently certify their current accuracy.
Do not add a specific
current number (funding amount, ranking, percentage) to any bank entry
without a dated, verifiable source at the time of use (`09`).

## 11. Advanced structure, paragraph sequencing and style

⚠️ When using a multi-function example (Section 3) across the same
essay's two argument-map cycles (an unusual but sometimes valid choice),
make the distinct function explicit each time it reappears, so the
repetition reads as a deliberate callback rather than as running out of
material.

## 12. ❌ Failure modes and revision protocol

- ❌ **Reuse without restated function** (Section 3). → Protocol: always
  state the specific claim being illustrated on each reuse.
- ❌ **Overstated proof value** (Section 5). → Protocol: name the
  stakeholder/scale the example does not speak to.
- ❌ **Stale refresh-flagged example used without a currency check.** →
  Protocol: verify against the linked source module or a dated source
  before using in a live attempt (Section 13).
