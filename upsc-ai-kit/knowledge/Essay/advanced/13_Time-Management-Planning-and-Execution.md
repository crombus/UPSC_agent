## 1. Advanced proposition and boundary

**Proposition:** ⚠️ time-budgeting should be personalised from diagnosed
bottlenecks (measured in practice, per `basic/13` Section 12), not
copied from a generic split — a candidate whose actual weakness is slow
thesis-formation needs a different allocation than one whose weakness is
over-long drafting. **Boundary:** ❌ this topic does not teach the
underlying skills (`01`–`12`) — it teaches how to sequence and recover
them under a real clock.

## 2. Concept definition and taxonomy

- **Bottleneck phase:** the phase that most consistently overruns its
  planned allocation across your own timed attempts.
- **Recovery protocol:** a pre-decided, specific action taken when a
  phase overruns mid-attempt (Section 6), rather than an improvised
  panic response.
- **Two-essay budget interdependence:** the fact that overrunning on
  essay one directly reduces available time for essay two — the budget
  must be tracked jointly, not per-essay in isolation.

## 3. Tension pairs and hidden assumptions

- **Perfectionism vs. completion:** the hidden assumption that spending
  extra time refining the first essay improves the overall paper; in
  practice, both essays must be completed and one must be chosen from each
  displayed section, so overrunning on the first directly compromises the
  second.
- **Fixed template vs. adaptive budget:** the assumption that a single
  time-split (Section 6 of the basic file) should be memorised and
  applied identically to every prompt; in fact issue-based prompts
  (`03`) may need more scoping time, while well-decoded aphorisms (`02`)
  may need less.

## 4. Levels of analysis and temporal-spatial scale

⚠️ Track time at three levels: the whole-paper level (both essays plus
any shared reading/choice time), the single-essay level (choice through
revision), and the paragraph level (drafting pace per planned paragraph,
from `07`'s paragraph-count plan) — a bottleneck can appear at any of
these levels and needs a level-appropriate fix.

## 5. Stakeholder map and distributional effects

⚠️ Not directly applicable in the usual sense — the only "stakeholder"
in this topic's own scope is the candidate's own performance across two
essays; the relevant distributional question is how minutes are
allocated fairly between the two required essays rather than
concentrated in one.

## 6. Causality, mechanisms and feedback loops — recovery protocols

```text
PHASE OVERRUNS its planned time
        |
        v
DIAGNOSE: which phase, and by how much?
        |
   choice/decode overrun --------> commit to current-best thesis now (05, Section 8)
   planning overrun --------------> compress to a 3-4 line argument map, skip further options
   drafting overrun ---------------> cut to fewer, better-developed paragraph clusters (07)
   revision overrun (i.e. no time left) --> do only the relevance + evidence-risk sweep (14/15),
                                              skip the language-polish pass
```
⚠️ Deciding these recovery actions *in advance* (not in the moment of
panic) is what separates an advanced execution plan from a merely
aspirational one.

## 7. Necessary/sufficient conditions and exceptions

⚠️ A pre-decided recovery protocol (Section 6) is necessary for
resilient execution but not sufficient — it must be paired with honest,
ongoing time-tracking (checking elapsed time at natural checkpoints, e.g.
after choice, after planning) to be triggered at all. **Exception:** if a
candidate's diagnosed bottleneck is consistently the *same* phase across
many practice attempts, it is often more efficient to fix the underlying
skill (e.g. faster decoding via `02`) than to keep compensating with a
recovery protocol every time.

## 8. Paradox, dialectic and counterfactual test

**Paradox:** spending slightly more time on planning than feels
comfortable usually *reduces* total time to a finished essay, because it
prevents a mid-draft structural rewrite, which is far more costly than
an unhurried planning phase. **Counterfactual test:** "If I had stopped
planning two minutes earlier, would my draft have hit a structural
problem I did not foresee?" If yes, planning time was actually
under-allocated, not over-allocated, even though it felt slow at the
time.

## 11. Advanced structure, paragraph sequencing and style

⚠️ Under a compressed drafting phase (Section 6), prefer cutting the
*number* of paragraph clusters (e.g. from four dimensions to three)
over cutting the *depth* of each remaining cluster — a shorter, well-
argued essay outperforms a longer one where every paragraph is
under-developed.

## 12. ❌ Failure modes and revision protocol

- ❌ **No pre-decided recovery protocol**, leading to panic-improvised
  cuts mid-draft. → Protocol: memorise Section 6's table before any
  timed attempt.
- ❌ **Ignoring diagnosed, repeated bottlenecks** and re-applying the
  same generic split every time. → Protocol: adjust your personal time
  budget based on your own logged pattern (Section 7 exception).
- ❌ **Treating the second essay's time budget as "whatever is left."**
  → Protocol: pre-commit to a hard cap for essay one regardless of how
  well it is going.

## 13. 📰 Dated anchor / practice lab / transfer task

📰 **Dated anchor:** not applicable — execution/time-management method
is stable and does not depend on current-affairs content.

**Practice lab:** Run one full timed attempt (choice through a completed
draft) for any prompt in `../README.md`, deliberately forcing an
overrun in one phase, and apply Section 6's recovery protocol for that
phase to completion.

**Transfer task:** Repeat the same forced-overrun drill for a different
phase on a separate occasion, and compare which recovery protocol felt
most natural to execute under pressure; log the result and any
first-time diagnosed bottleneck in `16`'s error log.
