## 1. Purpose and scope

This topic covers only the essay's opening and closing paragraphs — the
body's internal flow and transitions are covered in `07`. It assumes a
thesis has already been chosen (`05`).

## 2. Core terms in plain language

- **Relevant opening:** an opening that engages the prompt's actual
  tension (from `02`/`03`) within the first sentence or two.
- **Forced anecdote:** a fabricated or generic human-interest story used
  to "hook" the reader without genuine connection to the thesis.
- **Earned conclusion:** a closing that follows logically from the
  body's argument, not one that could be pasted onto a different essay.

## 3. ✅ Exam facts / source basis

- ✅ Both audited papers require essays of about 1000–1200 words with no
  printed structural requirement for introduction/conclusion length or
  form (see `../README.md`).
- ⚠️ The techniques below are pedagogical, not derived from any
  official UPSC style guide — none exists in the audited corpus.

## 4. The central idea and common misreading

⚠️ **Central idea:** the introduction should state or clearly imply the
thesis's tension quickly; the conclusion should return to that tension
transformed by the argument, not merely repeated. ❌ **Common misreading:**
opening with a fabricated personal anecdote ("I remember when my
grandfather told me...") disconnected from any real argument, or closing
with a generic call to action ("we must all work together") that could
end almost any essay on any topic.

## 5. Basic decomposition questions

1. What is the prompt's tension, in one sentence, that my opening should
   surface?
2. Can I open with the prompt's own tension restated in my own words,
   rather than an anecdote?
3. What has my argument *added* to the thesis by the end of the essay
   that the introduction did not yet know?
4. Does my conclusion state a feasible, forward-looking synthesis, or
   just a summary?
5. Would my conclusion still make sense if pasted onto a different essay
   on a different prompt? (If yes, it is too generic.)

## 6. Dimension-expansion grid

| Prompt (example) | Opening move | Closing move |
|---|---|---|
| 2025-A1 "Truth knows no color." | State the tension: objectivity claimed vs. perspective-shaped perception | Synthesis: plural voice, common evidentiary standard |
| 2024-A3 "Happiness is the path." | State the tension: destination-chasing vs. present conduct | Synthesis: flourishing as ongoing process and social condition |
| 2025-B5 "Muddy water… leaving it alone." | State the tension: restraint vs. intervention | Synthesis: diagnostic wisdom — knowing which situations need which |

## 7. India-first illustration starters

⚠️ An opening may use a brief, real Indian reference (an institution, a
documented episode) instead of an abstract statement, provided it is
recalled, not invented, and directly tied to the tension. Full
discipline: `12`.

## 8. Thesis options and selection

⚠️ The chosen thesis (from `05`) should be visible, at least in
implication, by the end of the introduction — a reader should be able to
predict roughly what the essay will argue, without the essay stating
"in this essay I will argue..." mechanically.

## 9. Simple essay architecture

⚠️ A workable shape: introduction (1 paragraph, tension + implied
thesis), body (per `07`), conclusion (1 paragraph, transformed thesis +
synthesis). Avoid a two-paragraph introduction or a conclusion that
introduces a brand-new idea not developed in the body.

## 10. Opening, body and closure moves

- **Opening move:** state the tension in your own words; optionally
  ground it in one recalled illustration; imply the thesis.
- **Closure move:** restate the thesis in its tested, qualified form;
  state the synthesis; end on a feasible, humane, forward-looking note
  — not a slogan.

## 11. ❌ Traps and repair moves

- ❌ **Forced/fabricated anecdote opening.** → Repair: open with the
  prompt's own tension in your own words instead.
- ❌ **Introduction that reveals no thesis at all.** → Repair: ensure a
  reader could state, even roughly, what you will argue after reading
  paragraph one.
- ❌ **Conclusion that merely repeats the introduction verbatim.** →
  Repair: restate the thesis in its *tested* form, showing what the body
  argument added.
- ❌ **Conclusion that introduces a brand-new, undeveloped claim.** →
  Repair: the conclusion should synthesise material already argued in
  the body, not add new content.

## 12. Timed micro-drill and self-check

**5-minute drill:** For any chosen thesis, write only the opening
sentence and the closing sentence (not the full essay). Check that the
opening states a tension (not an anecdote) and the closing states a
synthesis (not a repeat).

**Self-check:** Read your closing sentence in isolation. Does it clearly
belong to *this* essay's specific argument, or could it end almost any
essay on "change," "balance" or "progress" in general? If the latter,
rewrite it to reference your specific thesis and mechanism.
