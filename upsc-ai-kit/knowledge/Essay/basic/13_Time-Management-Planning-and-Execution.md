## 1. Purpose and scope

This topic covers execution mechanics — how to allocate the paper's
available time across choice, planning, drafting and revision for two
essays. It assumes the analytical topics (`01`–`12`) are already
understood; here they are sequenced under a clock.

## 2. Core terms in plain language

- **Choice-and-decode time:** minutes spent scanning prompts and
  selecting (`01`) plus initial decoding (`02`/`03`).
- **Planning time:** minutes spent building the argument map (`05`) and
  paragraph plan (`07`) before drafting prose.
- **Drafting time:** minutes spent writing continuous prose for the
  chosen structure.
- **Revision buffer:** minutes reserved at the end for the sweep
  described in `14`/`15`.

## 3. ✅ Exam facts / source basis

- ✅ Both audited years require two essays, about 1000–1200 words each,
  one from each Section (see `../README.md`).
- ⚠️ Neither paper prescribes how time should be split across choice,
  planning, drafting and revision — the time allocations below are a
  pedagogical starting plan to be adapted after timed practice, not an
  official UPSC prescription.

## 4. The central idea and common misreading

⚠️ **Central idea:** allocate time deliberately across all four phases
(choice, planning, drafting, revision) for *both* essays, rather than
spending most of the time on the first essay and rushing the second. ❌
**Common misreading:** starting to write immediately after reading the
prompt, skipping planning entirely, and discovering the structure has no
clear thesis only after several paragraphs are already drafted.

## 5. Basic decomposition questions

1. How much total time do I have for both essays combined?
2. How much time will I spend on choice-and-decode versus planning
   versus drafting versus revision, for each essay?
3. Am I allocating roughly similar planning discipline to both essays,
   or favouring the one I feel more confident about?
4. What is my trigger to stop planning and start drafting (e.g. a
   completed argument map)?
5. What is my trigger to stop drafting and start revising (e.g.
   reaching the upper end of the word range)?

## 6. Dimension-expansion grid

| Phase | Approx. share of time per essay (illustrative starting split) |
|---|---|
| Choice and decode | Short, front-loaded — enough to run `01`'s checks and `02`/`03`'s decoding |
| Planning (thesis + argument map + paragraph plan) | A meaningful block — this is where most quality is determined |
| Drafting | The largest single block, but should not consume all remaining time |
| Revision buffer | A protected, non-negotiable block at the end (see `14`/`15`) |

⚠️ Adjust this illustrative split after your own timed diagnostics
(Section 12) — it is a starting point, not a fixed rule.

## 8. Thesis options and selection

⚠️ Cap thesis-selection time (`05`) explicitly — if no thesis has
emerged after a reasonable, pre-decided number of minutes, commit to the
strongest available option rather than continuing to search for a
"perfect" one.

## 9. Simple essay architecture

⚠️ Use the paragraph-count plan from `07` as your drafting checklist —
knowing you have, say, 10–12 planned paragraphs helps you pace drafting
time evenly rather than over-writing the introduction and rushing the
conclusion.

## 10. Opening, body and closure moves

⚠️ Protect enough time to write a deliberate conclusion (`06`) — a common
time-pressure failure is a rushed, generic final paragraph because all
remaining time was spent on the body.

## 11. ❌ Traps and repair moves

- ❌ **Skipping planning to "save time."** → Repair: even a compressed
  plan (thesis + 3–4 line argument map) is faster overall than
  discovering structural problems mid-draft.
- ❌ **Over-investing in the first essay, leaving little time for the
  second.** → Repair: pre-commit to a time cap per essay and track it.
- ❌ **No protected revision buffer.** → Repair: treat the revision
  sweep (`14`/`15`) as non-negotiable, not optional "if time permits."
- ❌ **Continuing to search for a better thesis indefinitely.** → Repair:
  set a decision deadline (Section 8) and commit.

## 12. Timed micro-drill and self-check

**Timed drill:** Using a real clock, run the full choice→decode→
plan→draft(partial)→revise cycle for one prompt from `../README.md`
within a fixed total time you set for yourself in advance. Record actual
minutes spent per phase.

**Self-check:** Compare your actual phase-by-phase time against your
planned split (Section 6). Which phase consistently overruns? Adjust
your next attempt's plan specifically for that phase, and log the
pattern for `16`'s error log.
