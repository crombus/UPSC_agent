## 1. Snapshot & core idea

**Foundation — why Harsha matters after the Guptas.**

✅ After Gupta decline and Huna pressure, north India did not remain unified; Harsha of the Pushyabhuti line briefly restored a large north-Indian kingdom from **AD 606 to 647**, with **Kannauj** becoming the political centre. RS Sharma treats Harsha as important because his age is illuminated by **Banabhatta's `Harshacharita`**, **Xuanzang/Hsuan Tsang's account**, and Harsha's own inscriptions.
- ✅ Harsha's early career is reconstructed from Bana, supplemented by Xuanzang, who stayed in India for about fifteen years.
- ✅ Xuanzang left China in **AD 629**, studied at **Nalanda**, spent time at Harsha's court, travelled widely, and returned in **AD 645** with Buddhist texts.
- ✅ Harsha's inscriptions mention taxes and officials, so the period is not known only from literary praise.
- ⚠️ Calling Harsha the "last great Hindu emperor" is unsafe: his power was regionally limited and his religious patronage included Buddhism.

**Standard — eastern India and the land-grant transition.**

✅ RS Sharma links the spread of civilisation in **Orissa, Bengal and Assam** to Gupta/post-Gupta cultural contact, inscriptions, land grants, rural expansion, and the formation of local state systems.
- ✅ North Bengal and north-west Orissa came under Gupta influence; elsewhere Gupta-era dates in inscriptions show cultural-political contact.
- ✅ Inscriptions in eastern Madhya Pradesh, Orissa, Bengal and Assam were often land grants made for religious purposes to Brahmanas or Buddhist establishments.
- ✅ New states in Bengal maintained elephants, horses and boats in military camps, suggesting organised political power.
- ⚠️ The larger trend is the shift from imperial uniformity to **regional state formation + agrarian expansion**.

## 4. Must-Know Facts (Prelims)

- ✅ Harsha ruled **AD 606–647**; Pushyabhuti base was Thanesar, but Kannauj became central.
- ✅ Bana wrote **`Harshacharita`**; Xuanzang provides an external account.
- ✅ Xuanzang came to India in **AD 629**, studied at **Nalanda**, and returned in **AD 645**.
- ✅ Harsha's inscriptions refer to taxes and officials.
- ✅ The post-Gupta east shows land grants, Gupta-era dating, and formation of new states.
- ✅ Eastern India included Orissa, Bengal and Assam in the spread of civilisation framework.

## 5. UPSC Traps

> 🔑 Trap: UPSC can mix **source-author pairs**, **dates**, and **regional transition**.

- ❌ `Harshacharita` was written by Xuanzang. → It was written by **Banabhatta**; Xuanzang was the Chinese pilgrim.
- ❌ Harsha was a pan-Indian ruler like Ashoka. → His influence was large in north India but not pan-Indian.
- ❌ Nalanda is only Gupta-period and irrelevant to Harsha. → Xuanzang came to study at Nalanda in Harsha's age.
- ❌ Eastern India was outside early historic change. → RS Sharma links Orissa, Bengal and Assam to inscriptions, land grants and state formation.

## 6. 📰 Current link

⚠️ **Current-link discipline:** Cite new Ratnagiri finds only from the excavation report. The safe linkage is eastern India's major role in post-Gupta Buddhist, agrarian and regional-cultural history.

## 7. Mains angles

- ⚠️ Harsha is best read as a **post-Gupta restorer**, not a Maurya/Gupta-scale empire builder.
- ⚠️ Xuanzang shows how travel accounts help reconstruct political, religious and educational history.
- ⚠️ Eastern India's transition illustrates how land grants, religion and agrarian expansion integrated frontier regions.
