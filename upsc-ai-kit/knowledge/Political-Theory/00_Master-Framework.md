# Political Theory - Master Conceptual Framework

> **Purpose:** Connect every Political Theory topic before studying the detailed files.
> **Core question:** When a question asks about the state, rights, liberty, equality, justice,
> power or democracy, which competing *conceptual family* (liberal, Marxist, communitarian,
> feminist, Gandhian, pluralist, anarchist) is being invoked, and what does each family say about
> the unit of analysis, legitimacy, freedom, justice and the proper role of the state?
> **Grounded in:** O. P. Gauba, *An Introduction to Political Theory*, Fifth Edition (2009),
> read directly across all 25 chapters; selectively supplemented by the supplied
> *Socio-Political Philosophy* PDF for secularism, multiculturalism, punishment and additional
> rights distinctions, and by verified local Philosophy Optional PYQs (2022-2025).
> ✅ = source-grounded | ⚠️ = analytical inference/synthesis | 📰 = dated current anchor.

## 2. Five master questions used across every topic

| # | Master question | What it tests | Spectrum of answers |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **What is the unit of analysis?** | Whose interest/agency is the theory built around? | Individual (liberal) → class (Marxist) → community (communitarian/Gandhian) → gendered subject (feminist) → association (pluralist) → state (statist/organic) → humanity (cosmopolitan/internationalist) |
| 2 | **What makes authority legitimate?** | Source of rightful rule | Consent (liberal contract) → law (legal-rational) → common good (communitarian/organic) → class emancipation (Marxist) → participation (participatory/deliberative democrat) → justice (Rawlsian) → moral self-rule (Gandhian conscience) |
| 3 | **What is freedom?** | Concept of liberty being used | Non-interference/negative liberty (classical liberal) → self-development/positive liberty (welfare liberal, Idealist) → non-domination (republican-adjacent reading) → emancipation from class/gender hierarchy (Marxist/feminist) → self-rule/swaraj (Gandhian) |
| 4 | **What is justice?** | Justice criterion being applied | Procedure/due process (legal justice) → entitlement from just acquisition/transfer (Nozick) → fairness behind a veil of ignorance (Rawls) → need-based distribution and abolition of exploitation (Marxist) → varied egalitarian and welfare principles (socialist) → recognition (feminist/subaltern) → embedded community good (communitarian) |
| 5 | **What should the state do?** | Scope-of-state question | Minimise/protect rights only (classical liberal, neoliberal/libertarian) → enable welfare (welfare liberal) → coordinate associations (pluralist) → transform structural injustice (Marxist, feminist, post-colonial) → wither away (classical Marxist end-state) → decentralise into self-governing units (Gandhian, anarchist) |

**Use these five questions as the opening diagnostic for any theory question:** identify which
unit of analysis, legitimacy source, liberty concept, justice criterion and state-role the
question is implicitly invoking, before writing a single line of the answer.

### Cross-cutting logic check: necessary is not sufficient

Before concluding, test whether the condition named in the question is merely necessary, actually
sufficient, both, or neither. Examples: legal validity is necessary for an enforceable legal right
but not sufficient for moral legitimacy; free elections are necessary for liberal democracy but not
sufficient without choice, liberties and accountability; alterability is necessary before an
inequality becomes a practical target of reform but not sufficient to prove it unjust; the Rawlsian
difference principle cannot justify inequality unless equal basic liberties and fair equality of
opportunity have already been secured.

## 3. Reusable comparison matrices

### 3.1 Ideologies (see 02-06)

| Ideology | Unit | Freedom | State's role | Property | Key names |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Classical liberalism | Individual | Negative liberty | Minimal/night-watchman | Strongly protected | Locke, Bentham, J.S. Mill (early) |
| Welfare liberalism | Individual (socially situated) | Positive liberty (conditions for self-development) | Enabling/redistributive | Regulated, taxed for welfare | T.H. Green, Hobhouse, Rawls |
| Neoliberalism / market liberalism | Individual, market actor | Negative liberty, competition, spontaneous order | Rolled-back but still market-framing | Strongly protected | Hayek, Friedman |
| Libertarianism | Self-owning individual | Non-coercion and strong negative liberty | Minimal protective state; rectification of unjust title remains necessary | Strong entitlement subject to just acquisition/transfer/rectification | Nozick |
| Marxism | Class | Emancipation from class domination | Instrument of ruling class, eventually to wither away | Abolition of private ownership of means of production | Marx, Engels; neo-Marxist: Frankfurt School, Althusser |
| Democratic socialism/social democracy | Individual within society | Positive liberty via redistribution | Active welfare/regulatory state, achieved constitutionally | Mixed; means of production may be socially regulated | Fabians, Hobhouse, Tawney, Laski |
| Fascism (Gauba: pathology, not a mainstream tradition) | Nation/state, not the individual | Denied; "true liberty" recast as subjection to state authority | Totalitarian, leader-led, all-powerful | ⚠️ No coherent general property doctrine; Marxist/Laski critiques interpret fascist rule as protecting concentrated economic control | Mussolini, Hitler (Nazism as a variant); critiqued by Gramsci, Laski |
| Anarchism | Individual/voluntary association | Freedom from all coercive authority | To be abolished; voluntary cooperation instead | Varies (Proudhon critical of property; Kropotkin communal) | Proudhon, Bakunin, Kropotkin |
| Gandhism | Individual moral conscience within community | Swaraj (self-rule, moral) | Minimal, decentralised (village swaraj); trusteeship over coercion | Trusteeship, not abolition | M. K. Gandhi |
| Feminism (liberal/radical/socialist) | Gendered subject | Freedom from patriarchal constraint | Varies: reform (liberal) to structural transformation (radical/socialist) | Socialist feminism links property relations to patriarchy | Wollstonecraft, Mill, Firestone, Millett, Rowbotham |
| Communitarianism | Community/embedded self | Freedom realised through social membership | Perfectionist/common-good oriented (⚠️ contested vs liberal neutrality) | Subordinate to communal/common good | ⚠️ synthesis position, not a single named school in Gauba |

### 3.2 State theories (see 10, 13)

| Theory | State is... | Origin story | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Organic/Idealist | An ethical whole, more than its members | Natural/historical growth | Realise the common good, moral development |
| Social-contract/liberal | An artificial device created by consent | Contract among individuals | Protect rights, resolve the state-of-nature problem |
| Laissez-faire liberal | Night-watchman | Minimal contractual necessity | Protect life, liberty, property only |
| Welfare-liberal | Enabling/redistributive agency | Extension of liberal contract | Secure positive freedom and welfare |
| Marxist/class | Instrument of the dominant economic class | Emerges with class division of society | Maintain class rule; destined to wither away |
| Pluralist | One association among many | Coordinating body for group interests | Arbitrate between associations, not supreme over them |
| Feminist | Historically patriarchal structure | Reflects and reproduces gender hierarchy | ⚠️ Contested: reform vs transform |
| Post-colonial | Colonial-legacy structure | Inherited institutional form | ⚠️ Contested: developmental vs continuing domination |
| Gandhian | Concentration of coercive power, morally suspect | Grows with centralisation | Ideally minimal; replaced by self-ruling village communities |

### 3.3 Sovereignty (see 11, 12)

| Dimension | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Legal sovereignty | The determinate authority whose command is law (Austin's formulation) |
| Political sovereignty | The actual, diffuse power behind and shaping legal sovereignty |
| Popular sovereignty | Ultimate authority resting in the people/general will (Rousseau) |
| Internal sovereignty | Supremacy over all persons/associations within the territory |
| External sovereignty | Independence from outside control in the community of states |
| De jure sovereignty | Sovereignty in law/right | 
| De facto sovereignty | Sovereignty in actual effective control |

### 3.4 Power (see 15)

| Concept | Meaning | Distinguishing test |
|---|---|---|
| Power | Capacity to secure compliance or produce effects | Can be coercive, does not require rightfulness |
| Authority | Power regarded as rightful (Gauba: **authority = power + legitimacy**) | Compliance without need for coercion |
| Influence | Persuades without coercion | No sanction attached |
| Legitimacy | The quality that makes power be accepted as rightful | Produces willing, not merely coerced, obedience |
| Hegemony (Gramscian) | Domination through consent secured via ideology/culture | Operates without visible coercion |
| Developmental power (Macpherson) | Power used to expand human capacities | Contrasted with purely extractive power |

### 3.5 Citizenship (see 16)

| Dimension | Contrast |
|---|---|
| Universal citizenship | Same rights/status for all members, regardless of group identity |
| Differentiated citizenship | Group-specific rights/recognition (feminist/subaltern critique of a falsely neutral universal) |
| Formal citizenship | Legal status/membership alone |
| Substantive citizenship | Actual capacity to exercise the rights that status confers |
| Civil rights (Marshall) | Rights necessary for individual freedom (person, speech, property, justice) |
| Political rights (Marshall) | Right to participate in the exercise of political power |
| Social rights (Marshall) | Right to a modicum of welfare/economic security and social heritage |

### 3.5A Rights theories and scope (see 17)

| Issue | Precise position |
|---|---|
| Natural rights | Moral claims grounded in human nature, reason or purpose; powerful against unjust law but variable in interpretation |
| Legal rights | Rights recognized and enforced by the state; sufficient for legal status, not by itself for moral justification |
| Personality / ideal rights | Conditions necessary for development of personality; Barker's synthesis requires both personality-grounding and legal guarantee for a full right |
| Historical rights | Claims crystallized through custom and development; longevity alone cannot validate oppressive customs |
| Social-welfare rights | Rights justified by common welfare; the central objection is who has power to define welfare |
| Human rights / democratic rights / civil liberties | Human rights are the widest umbrella; democratic participation rights and civil liberties are analytically distinct, overlapping branches. Gauba's nested-circle diagram should not erase his own text's distinction between political and legal arenas |

### 3.6 Justice (see 19, 20)

| Dimension | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Legal justice | Justice according to settled law |
| Political justice | Fair distribution of political power/participation |
| Socio-economic justice | Fair distribution of social/economic goods |
| Procedural justice | Fairness of the process/rules followed |
| Distributive/substantive justice | Fairness of the actual outcome/share received |
| Recognitive justice | ⚠️ Fairness as acknowledgement of group/identity-based claims (feminist/subaltern synthesis) |

### 3.7 Democracy (see 22, 23)

| Model | Core claim |
|---|---|
| Liberal democracy | Constitutional government, civil liberties, competitive elections, independent judiciary, majority rule with minority rights |
| Elitist democracy (Schumpeter-type) | Democracy is competition among elites for the people's vote, not rule by the people directly |
| Pluralist democracy | Democracy as competition/bargaining among organised groups |
| Participatory democracy | Democracy requires active citizen participation beyond periodic voting |
| Marxist democracy | Formal/liberal democracy masks class rule; genuine democracy requires ending class division |
| Radical/deliberative democracy (Habermasian) | Legitimacy from reasoned public deliberation, not aggregation of pre-formed preferences alone |

### 3.8 Change and development (see 23)

| Mode | Core claim |
|---|---|
| Revolutionary change (Marxist) | Qualitative transformation via class struggle, breaking existing structures |
| Evolutionary/incremental change (liberal) | Gradual, constitutional reform within existing structures |
| Market-model development | Growth-led, private-enterprise-driven development |
| Welfare-model development | State-enabled redistribution alongside growth |
| Socialist-model development | Planned, socially owned/directed development |
| Gandhian development | Decentralised, village-centred, need-based, non-materialist development |
| Sustainable/political development | ⚠️ Later syntheses linking development to institutional capacity and ecological limits |

### 3.9 Secularism, multiculturalism and punishment (extensions in 10 and 14)

| Concept | Necessary distinction | Core caution |
|---|---|---|
| Secularism | Religious plurality is a social fact; secularism is a normative political arrangement for freedom and equality across and within religions | Non-theocracy is necessary but not sufficient; Indian secularism permits principled engagement against intra- and inter-religious domination |
| Multiculturalism | Descriptive diversity differs from normative recognition of diversity | Group recognition cannot become immunity for internal domination; individual rights and gender equality remain tests |
| Punishment | Retribution looks backward to desert; deterrence looks forward to prevention; reform looks forward to rehabilitation | Guilt does not by itself settle severity; proportionality constrains punishment, while deterrence remains an empirical claim rather than an automatic effect of harshness |

## 4. Thinker comparison matrix (cross-cutting, avoid caricature)

| Thinker | Reductive caricature to avoid | More accurate, qualified position |
|---|---|---|
| Hobbes | "Simple authoritarian who wants a tyrant" | Justifies near-absolute sovereign power as the *rational remedy* to the state of nature's insecurity, grounded in individual self-preservation and consent, not personal glorification of a ruler |
| Rousseau | "Simple majoritarian; general will = whatever the majority votes" | General will is the common/rational interest distinct from the mere sum of particular wills; popular sovereignty is inalienable and not identical to any incumbent government's decisions |
| Marx | "Strict economic determinist; base mechanically fixes superstructure" | Historical materialism as a framework of base-superstructure *interaction*, with later Marxist scholarship (and Gauba's own note) recognising superstructural relative autonomy |
| Gandhi | "Simple pacifist with no political theory" | Ahimsa/satyagraha as an active, disciplined method of resistance; swaraj as moral self-rule; trusteeship as a proposed alternative to both unchecked capitalism and coercive state socialism |
| Liberalism | "Always anti-state/pro-market" | Spans laissez-faire to strongly redistributive welfare liberalism; united by individual moral worth, limited/constitutional government and rule of law, not by a fixed view of state size |
| Feminism | "One unified movement with one demand" | Liberal (equal opportunity/legal reform), radical (patriarchy as autonomous power system) and socialist (patriarchy plus capitalism) feminism differ sharply on cause and remedy |
| Communitarianism | "Anti-individual, wants forced conformity" | Critiques the liberal *abstract, unencumbered self*, arguing identity and value are constituted through community; does not necessarily deny individual rights or endorse majoritarian conformity |
| Rawls | "Wants equal outcomes for everyone" | Two principles: equal basic liberties, then a second principle containing fair equality of opportunity and the difference principle in that priority order. Benefit to the least advantaged is not sufficient if prior liberty or opportunity conditions fail |
| Nozick | "Opposed to justice itself" | Entitlement theory requires just acquisition, just transfer and rectification; a present voluntary transfer is not sufficient if the title rests on prior injustice. He opposes patterned/end-state redistribution, not justice itself |

## 5. Universal answer spine (use in every Mains-style answer)

1. **Define** the core concept precisely, in the vocabulary of the question (not a story-first opening).
2. **Locate competing views** — name at least two rival theoretical families/thinkers relevant to the question.
3. **Explain mechanisms/thinkers** — how each position actually reasons to its conclusion (not just a label).
4. **Identify the tension** — where the positions genuinely conflict (unit of analysis, legitimacy, freedom, justice, or state-role).
5. **Apply a neutral illustration** — a timeless, hypothetical or clearly dated example; never present the book's 2009-era examples as current fact.
6. **Qualify/criticise** — note the strongest objection to each position, avoiding caricature.
7. **Synthesise** — state a reasoned, qualified conclusion or note where the debate remains genuinely open.

  in the book are **book-period illustrations**.

  dated sources — never inferred from or silently substituted into the 2009 text's own examples.

## 7. Study sequence

1. **Foundations of the discipline (01-02):** what political theory is, and what ideology is.
2. **Ideological family survey (03-06):** liberalism/neoliberalism, Marxism/neo-Marxism,
   socialism/anarchism/Gandhism, feminism.
3. **Politics and method (07-09):** nature of politics and communitarianism; approaches and
   behaviouralism; interdisciplinary analysis.
4. **State and sovereignty (10-13):** state/civil society/nation, sovereignty/pluralism,
   globalisation's challenge, diverse perspectives on the state.
5. **Obedience, power and membership (14-17):** obligation/resistance/law, power/authority/
   legitimacy, citizenship, human rights/civil liberties.
6. **Core ideals (18-21):** liberty/equality/property, justice (concepts, then diverse
   perspectives), common good/community.
7. **Democracy and change (22-23):** representation/liberal democracy, then contemporary models
   of democracy and theories of change/development.
8. **Cross-link outward** to `Polity`, `Governance`, `Ethics`, `International-Relations` and
   `Philosophy/paper-2/socio-political` only for the applied/institutional/syllabus-specific
   layer, per each file's "Cross-links and boundaries" / "Cross-links and exclusions" section.
