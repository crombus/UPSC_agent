## 1. One-screen advanced map

```text
WHY OBEY?
   force -> too coercive
   divine right -> too theological
   prudence -> order over ideal justice
   consent -> strongest liberal route
   common good -> idealist correction
   class / anarchist critique -> obligation against the state
   Gandhian conscience -> principled, public, non-violent disobedience

WHAT IS LAW?
   prescriptive rule
   debated by:
   natural law / positivism / historical evolution / sociological purpose

FINAL TEST
   legality != morality
   restraint can protect liberty
   rule of law restrains arbitrariness
```

- ✅ Gauba treats political obligation and concept of law as linked because obedience cannot be understood without knowing what counts as valid law, just law and limited government (PDF pp.280-300).
- ⚠️ The advanced theme is that neither legality nor obedience is morally self-authenticating.

## 2. Scope, assumptions and conceptual boundary

- ✅ Gauba frames political obligation as the problem of the logical and moral grounds of obeying political authority, including taxes, participation, jury service and military duty (PDF p.280).
- ✅ He then shifts to jurisprudence because the citizen's problem is not only whether to obey, but also what kind of norm law is and how it differs from morality and sheer force (PDF pp.289-300).
- ⚠️ This file is conceptual and normative: it does **not** catalogue Indian statutes, constitutional cases or administrative remedies.
- ✅ The chapter's practical assumption is anti-romantic: real obedience is demanded by governments staffed by fallible officials, not by abstract ideals alone (PDF pp.287-288).

## 3. Direct-book argument reconstruction

- ✅ Gauba first dismisses force majeure as a full theory of obligation because fear may secure compliance but cannot secure morally reasoned obedience (PDF pp.280-281).
- ✅ Divine-right theory fails on similar grounds: it sanctifies authority without allowing rational scrutiny of unjust rule (PDF p.281).
- ✅ Conservative thought improves matters by appealing to prudence, continuity and legitimacy, but it still leans toward obedience because disorder is seen as worse than many defects of government (PDF pp.281-282).
- ✅ Consent theory is the major liberal answer: people obey because authority is of their own making, yet Hobbes, Locke and Rousseau convert that premise into very different scopes of obligation (PDF pp.282-284).
- ✅ Idealism radicalizes obligation in Hegel but moderates it in Green, who ties obedience to common good and denies unconditional claims by government (PDF pp.284-286).
- ✅ Marxism and anarchism deny genuine political obligation to the coercive class-state, though Marxism ties duty to class struggle and anarchism to justice without government (PDF p.286).
- ✅ Gandhi then gives Gauba's most disciplined account of resistance: unjust law may be broken, but only openly, non-violently, after persuasion fails, with willingness to suffer penalty and without sectional selfishness (PDF p.287).
- ✅ Gauba's conclusion adds a final caution: resistance must be publicly reasoned, proportionate, constitutionally preceded and not so destructive that it unleashes chaos worse than the grievance itself (PDF pp.287-288).
- ✅ The law chapter then reclassifies the field: scientific law describes regularities, prescriptive law governs conduct, and jurisprudence debates whether law is moral reason, sovereign command, historical growth or social engineering (PDF pp.289-294).
- ✅ The final movement reconciles law with morality and liberty only conditionally: law may restrain to protect equal freedom and social welfare, but arbitrary or morally stagnant law becomes tyrannical; hence rule of law becomes crucial (PDF pp.295-300).

## 4. Definitions and distinctions

- ✅ **Political obligation:** a duty owed to political authority, not necessarily to every government action as such (PDF p.280).
- ✅ **Legitimacy:** though not fully theorized here, Gauba's conservative and rule-of-law discussions imply that rightful authority is different from naked power (PDF pp.281-282, 298-300).
- ✅ **Unlimited obligation:** obligation with no effective right of resistance or withdrawal, typical of force, divine-right and strong absolutist formulations (PDF pp.280-285).
- ✅ **Limited obligation:** obedience constrained by rights, common good, constitutional trust or conscience (PDF pp.283-287).
- ✅ **Negative obligation / anti-obligation:** refusal to concede moral obedience to the coercive class-state or organized authority as such (PDF p.286).
- ✅ **Civil disobedience:** public, principled, non-violent breach of unjust law with acceptance of punishment and general respect for law (PDF p.287).
- ✅ **Descriptive law:** a discovered regularity in nature or social science, tested as true or false (PDF p.289).
- ✅ **Prescriptive law:** a humanly made rule, backed by sanctions, telling people what to do or refrain from doing (PDF pp.289-290).
- ✅ **Positive law:** law expressing the sovereign will through proper enactment and enforceable sanction (PDF p.290).
- ✅ **Rule of law:** governance by known, general, prospective and legally restrained norms rather than arbitrary will (PDF pp.298-300).

## 5. Thinker-by-thinker positions

- ✅ **Hobbes:** obligation is broad because the sovereign is the rational antidote to insecurity; once natural powers are transferred, revolt reopens anarchy (PDF pp.283-284).
- ✅ **Locke:** obligation is conditional because government is a trust for protecting life, liberty and property; breach of trust legitimizes replacement (PDF pp.283-284).
- ✅ **Rousseau:** obligation is strict but self-imposed because sovereignty resides in the general will rather than an external ruler; civic freedom replaces raw natural liberty (PDF pp.283-284).
- ✅ **Hegel:** by elevating the state as divine reason embodied, he collapses moral freedom into obedience and risks unconditional statism (PDF p.284).
- ✅ **T.H. Green:** obligation is owed only to laws that serve the common good; society, not government alone, is the moral pivot (PDF pp.284-286).
- ✅ **Harold Laski:** Gauba uses him in conclusion to remind us that claims of state pre-eminence are in practice claims of fallible governments (PDF p.288).
- ✅ **Marxists:** obligation is redirected toward the working class and revolutionary solidarity, not toward the capitalist state (PDF p.286).
- ✅ **Anarchists:** the individual owes justice and cooperation but not obedience to organized coercive authority (PDF p.286).
- ✅ **Thoreau:** personal refusal to support injustice makes conscience a direct source of justified non-compliance (PDF p.287).
- ✅ **Gandhi:** civil disobedience must aim at change of heart, not destruction; it presupposes respect for law in general and opposition only to specific injustice (PDF p.287).
- ✅ **Austin:** law is sovereign command backed by sanction; international law becomes only positive morality on this test (PDF p.291).
- ✅ **Kelsen:** validity comes from proper promulgation and conformity to a basic norm within a hierarchy of norms (PDF pp.291-292).
- ✅ **Hart:** law is better understood as a structure of rules, not only coercive commands, and its standards of validity rest on social fact (PDF p.292).
- ✅ **Dworkin:** rules are insufficient in hard cases; judges also rely on principles that belong to law's working reality (PDF p.292).
- ✅ **Savigny / Maine / Pound:** law is historical growth, social evolution and social engineering, not merely command (PDF pp.292-294).

## 6. Competing perspectives

| Perspective | Ground of obligation / legality | What it gets right | What it misses |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ **Force theory** | ✅ Fear of superior coercion (PDF pp.280-281). | ✅ Explains compliance under domination. | ❌ Cannot explain moral obligation. |
| ✅ **Divine-right view** | ✅ Sacred source of rule (PDF p.281). | ✅ Explains traditional reverence. | ❌ Denies critical judgment and secular legitimacy. |
| ✅ **Conservative prudence** | ✅ Order, continuity and legitimacy are worth preserving (PDF pp.281-282). | ✅ Warns against reckless revolution. | ⚠️ Can tolerate too much injustice for the sake of order. |
| ✅ **Consent theory** | ✅ Authority is justified because it is self-imposed or agreed to (PDF pp.282-284). | ✅ Makes obedience intelligible in liberal terms. | ⚠️ Once-for-all consent is unstable unless continuously renewed. |
| ✅ **Idealist common-good view** | ✅ Obedience is owed to laws serving common good (PDF pp.284-286). | ✅ Reconnects law with morality. | ⚠️ Risks abstraction if common good is invoked by officials without public control. |
| ✅ **Marxist view** | ✅ No duty to class-state; real duty runs against domination (PDF p.286). | ✅ Reveals class bias behind "public" authority. | ⚠️ Underplays plural moral communities outside class. |
| ✅ **Anarchist view** | ✅ Organized authority lacks moral title (PDF p.286). | ✅ Keeps coercion under relentless suspicion. | ⚠️ Hard to institutionalize stable large-scale order. |
| ✅ **Gandhian view** | ✅ Conscience can justify law-breaking under strict moral discipline (PDF p.287). | ✅ Best account of principled resistance. | ⚠️ Demands unusual moral restraint from both leaders and followers. |
| ✅ **Natural-law jurisprudence** | ✅ Valid law should answer to higher justice (PDF pp.290-291). | ✅ Preserves moral criticism of law. | ⚠️ Can lack procedural precision. |
| ✅ **Positivism** | ✅ Validity depends on source/form, not moral content alone (PDF pp.291-292). | ✅ Gives technical clarity to legal systems. | ❌ Can detach legality from legitimacy too sharply. |

## 7. Internal debates and criticisms

- ✅ Gauba's objection to force and divine-right theories is foundational: compliance under terror or theology is not yet moral obligation (PDF pp.281-282).
- ✅ Hobbes, Locke and Rousseau all use consent, but consent does not do the same work in each system; the same word masks radically different models of sovereignty and resistance (PDF pp.283-284).
- ✅ Gauba's own comparative judgment clearly favours Locke's version because it ties government to ongoing consent and rights-protection rather than one decisive original surrender (PDF p.284).
- ✅ Hegelian statism collapses state and moral reason too easily; Gauba explicitly warns how such thinking can be recruited by fascistic demands for blind obedience (PDF p.284).
- ✅ Green corrects idealism by relocating moral priority in society and common good, yet his view still presupposes a relatively unified moral consciousness that may be difficult to identify in plural societies (PDF pp.285-286).
- ✅ Marxist and anarchist positions are powerful as critiques of domination, but they raise the practical problem of what institutional order replaces the rejected authority (PDF p.286).
- ✅ Gandhi's doctrine solves part of that problem by insisting on publicity, non-violence and acceptance of punishment, thereby distinguishing principled resistance from opportunistic disorder (PDF p.287).
- ✅ In jurisprudence, positivism secures legal certainty, but Dworkin's critique shows that adjudication often relies on principles that cannot be reduced to rule-application alone (PDF p.292).
- ✅ Historical and sociological jurisprudence recover law's social roots and ends, but they also risk weakening the sharpness of legality if everything is absorbed into custom or social purpose (PDF pp.292-294).
- ✅ Gauba's rule-of-law discussion adds a final criticism of procedural minimalism: if law becomes unjust, opaque or arbitrary, legality alone cannot rescue it (PDF pp.298-300).

## 9. Application without current-affairs dependence

- ⚠️ When faced with a hypothetical unjust emergency order, first ask: is the issue about legal validity, moral legitimacy, or both?
- ⚠️ When faced with a tax-resistance problem, distinguish Thoreau-style conscience, Gandhian civil disobedience and revolutionary anti-state politics.
- ⚠️ When faced with a law restricting conduct, ask whether the restraint protects equal liberty, resolves civil conflict, regulates economic abuse or secures welfare; this follows Gauba's own fourfold reasoning on law and liberty (PDF pp.297-298).
- ⚠️ When faced with a hard judicial case, note that positivist rule-application may be supplemented by principle-based reasoning, which is where Dworkin becomes useful (PDF p.292).
- ⚠️ When faced with a question on rule of law, move beyond slogan and list the operational tests: notification, generality, prospectivity and restrained power (PDF p.298).

## 12. Factual-risk checks

- ❌ Do **not** treat force-based compliance as the same thing as political obligation; Gauba rejects that equation (PDF pp.280-281).
- ❌ Do **not** say Hobbes, Locke and Rousseau all generate either fully unlimited or fully identical consent; Gauba's discussion sharply differentiates them (PDF pp.283-284).
- ❌ Do **not** describe civil disobedience as secret, violent, or penalty-evading; that would negate Gauba's Gandhi-Thoreau model (PDF p.287).
- ❌ Do **not** equate analytical jurisprudence with the denial of all morality; Hart and Dworkin themselves complicate Austin-style command theory (PDF pp.291-292).
- ❌ Do **not** say rule of law is only about procedure and never about rights-protection; Gauba reads Dicey more broadly than that (PDF pp.299-300).
- ❌ Do **not** duplicate Indian statute/case material here; this file supplies the conceptual tests by which such material should later be judged.
