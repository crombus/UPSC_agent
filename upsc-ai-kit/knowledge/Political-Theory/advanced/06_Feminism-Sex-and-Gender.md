## 2. Scope, assumptions and conceptual boundary

- ✅ This file remains theory-centred: patriarchy, sex/gender, streams of feminism and the status of women in public life. (PDF pp.84-93)
- ✅ Gauba includes many numerical and representational illustrations from the late twentieth century; they must be treated as historical/book-period material rather than current data. (PDF pp.86, 91-93)
- ⚠️ The point here is not current gender-data reporting, but the conceptual tools needed to analyse gendered inequality.

## 3. Direct-book argument reconstruction

- ✅ Step 1: feminism is introduced as protest against the inferior status of women created by patriarchy. (PDF p.84)
- ✅ Step 2: Gauba separates sex from gender so that biological difference is not confused with social hierarchy. (PDF pp.84-86)
- ✅ Step 3: masculinity and femininity are shown as culturally coded patterns that reproduce male dominance. (PDF p.85)
- ✅ Step 4: using Rousseau's natural/conventional inequality distinction, Gauba shows gender inequality to be socially alterable. (PDF p.86)
- ✅ Step 5: early feminism is reconstructed through Wollstonecraft and Mill as a demand for rights, education, property and civic equality. (PDF p.88)
- ✅ Step 6: contemporary feminism is classified into liberal, radical and socialist strands. (PDF pp.88-90)
- ✅ Step 7: Gauba then extends the issue from rights to representation, citizenship and gender justice. (PDF pp.90-93)

## 4. Definitions and distinctions

- ✅ **Patriarchy:** male domination extending from household authority into labour market and public life. (PDF p.84)
- ✅ **Sex:** biological division of humans into male and female. (PDF pp.84-85)
- ✅ **Gender:** the cultural organization of expectations, roles and status attached to sex. (PDF pp.84-85)
- ✅ **Masculinity / femininity:** socially coded personality-traits and behaviours that naturalize domination/submission. (PDF p.85)
- ✅ **Natural inequality / conventional inequality:** Rousseau's distinction used by Gauba to show that gender hierarchy belongs largely to the second category. (PDF p.86)
- ✅ **Equal rights view vs compensatory-equality view:** one stresses same laws, the other supports differentiated protections and representation-enhancing measures. (PDF pp.92-93)

## 5. Thinker-by-thinker positions

- ✅ **Mary Wollstonecraft:** rejects female inferiority and insists that women, as rational beings, deserve education, property and civic rights. (PDF p.88)
- ✅ **J.S. Mill:** argues that women are not less talented than men and should enjoy full legal and political equality. (PDF pp.84, 88)
- ✅ **Shulamith Firestone:** radical feminist who treats reproductive biology as the ultimate historical basis of female subordination and sees technology as enabling escape from that dependence. (PDF p.89)
- ✅ **Kate Millett:** reads male-female relations as political power relations sustained by ideology, authority and economic force. (PDF p.89)
- ✅ **Sheila Rowbotham:** socialist feminist who ties women's liberation to anti-capitalist struggle and the recovery of women's hidden history. (PDF pp.89-90)
- ⚠️ Together these thinkers move the debate from rights-based equality, to patriarchy as power, to patriarchy-plus-capitalism as a combined structure.

## 6. Competing perspectives

- ✅ **Liberal feminism:** focuses on equal opportunity and the removal of formal discrimination. (PDF p.88)
- ✅ **Radical feminism:** insists that women's oppression is primary and cannot be dissolved into race or class alone. (PDF p.89)
- ✅ **Socialist feminism:** combines patriarchal analysis with Marxist class analysis. (PDF pp.89-90)
- ⚠️ Liberal feminism asks whether barriers are open; radical feminism asks who holds power over bodies and sexuality; socialist feminism asks how capitalism and patriarchy reinforce each other.
- ⚠️ For UPSC, the richest contrast is between formal equality, sexual power and structural political economy.

## 7. Internal debates and criticisms

- ✅ Gauba's own presentation shows that formal rights do not exhaust the feminist question because representation and public participation remain unequal even after suffrage. (PDF pp.91-93)
- ✅ Firestone's account gives biology a very strong explanatory role, but that is one radical position, not a universal feminist doctrine. (PDF p.89)
- ✅ Socialist feminism widens explanation but risks submerging specifically gendered experiences inside general anti-capitalist politics unless carefully handled. (inferred from PDF pp.89-90)
- ⚠️ The enduring debate is whether patriarchy is best seen as a legal exclusion, a sexual power system, or a system intertwined with class and labour.
- ❌ Treating any one school as "the" feminist position produces caricature and weak answers.

## 8. Comparative matrix

| Issue | Liberal feminism | Radical feminism | Socialist feminism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Main problem | ✅ Formal exclusion and discriminatory barriers. (PDF p.88) | ✅ Patriarchal sexual power and subordination. (PDF p.89) | ✅ Combined effect of patriarchy and capitalism. (PDF pp.89-90) |
| Key thinkers in Gauba | ✅ Wollstonecraft, Mill. (PDF p.88) | ✅ Firestone, Millett. (PDF p.89) | ✅ Rowbotham. (PDF pp.89-90) |
| View of equality | ✅ Equal opportunity and equal rights. (PDF p.88) | ✅ Deep restructuring of sex-power relations. (PDF p.89) | ✅ Equality requires transforming labour, family and class relations. (PDF pp.89-90) |
| Main analytical lens | ✅ Law, education, profession, representation. (PDF p.88) | ✅ Reproduction, ideology, domination. (PDF p.89) | ✅ Wage labour, domestic labour, history of oppression. (PDF pp.89-90) |
| Major risk in exam answers | ❌ Reducing feminism to legal reform alone. | ❌ Generalizing Firestone's biological emphasis to all feminism. | ❌ Collapsing gender into class and losing feminist specificity. |

## 9. Application without current-affairs dependence

- ⚠️ If asked why equal voting rights did not automatically create equal political power, use Gauba's move from citizenship-in-law to actual representation.
- ⚠️ If asked whether social roles are natural, use sex/gender and natural/conventional inequality to show why many hierarchies are alterable.
- ⚠️ If asked to compare feminist schools, organize the answer by obstacle identified: law, patriarchy or capitalism-plus-patriarchy.
- ⚠️ A timeless example is the difference between biological maternity and the culturally enforced idea that only women should bear domestic-care burdens.
