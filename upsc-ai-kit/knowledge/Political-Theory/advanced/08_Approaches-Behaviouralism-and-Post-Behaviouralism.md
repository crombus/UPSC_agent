## 1. One-screen advanced map

- ✅ Gauba separates method from approach, then empirical from normative, before classifying the main approaches. (PDF pp.108-112)
- ✅ Traditional approaches centre on philosophy, history, law and institutions. (PDF pp.112-117)
- ✅ Behaviouralism reorients the discipline toward observable political behaviour, scientific procedure and interdisciplinary tools. (PDF pp.118-121)
- ✅ Post-behaviouralism criticizes the withdrawal into pure method and adds relevance, action and value-conscious responsibility. (PDF pp.121-122)
- ✅ Political analysis then branches into systems, structural-functional, communications, decision-making and Marxian analysis. (PDF pp.122-134)
- ⚠️ Topic 08 is the methodological hinge of the module because it tells you how every later topic may be investigated differently.

## 2. Scope, assumptions and conceptual boundary

- ✅ This chapter is about approaches to inquiry, not a direct substantive theory of liberty, justice or the state. (PDF pp.108-134)
- ✅ Gauba warns against equating empirical with contemporary or normative with traditional in a rigid way. (PDF pp.109-110)
- ✅ He ends by refusing a complete estrangement between empirical and normative inquiry. (PDF p.112)
- ⚠️ Conceptually, this topic builds on the module's theory/philosophy/science distinction as mapped in `Political-Theory/README.md` and organized in `Political-Theory/00_Master-Framework.md`.
- ⚠️ Boundary: the models here are presented as tools of analysis, not as timeless proofs that one method must replace all others.

## 3. Direct-book argument reconstruction

- ✅ Gauba begins with conceptual hygiene: method concerns how to inquire, whereas approach concerns both how and what to inquire into. (PDF pp.108-109)
- ✅ He then historicizes the field: pre-Second-World-War approaches are called traditional, while later approaches are contemporary, but this is not the same as facts-versus-values. (PDF pp.109-110)
- ✅ Next he clarifies empirical and normative inquiry by content, showing that verification, sense-experience and instrumental value differ from moral preference and intrinsic value. (PDF pp.110-112)
- ✅ He reconstructs the traditional cluster through four approaches: philosophical, historical, legal and institutional. (PDF pp.112-117)
- ✅ He then narrates behaviouralism as a post-war American movement with earlier roots in Wallas, Bentley and Merriam, eventually summarized by Easton's eight tenets. (PDF pp.118-121)
- ✅ The post-behavioural revolution is presented as Easton's internal correction: keep science, but reconnect it to public crises, values and action. (PDF pp.121-122)
- ✅ Finally, Gauba extends the contemporary chapter into models of analysis—systems, structural-functional, communications, decision-making and Marxian analysis—each highlighting different variables. (PDF pp.122-134)

## 4. Definitions and distinctions

| Distinction | Advanced note |
|---|---|
| Method / approach | ✅ Methods obtain and use data; approaches choose problems and relevant data as well. (PDF pp.108-109) |
| Traditional / contemporary | ✅ A historical distinction about dominant styles of inquiry, not a perfect facts-values binary. (PDF pp.109-110) |
| Empirical / normative | ✅ Empirical claims are observable and testable; normative claims express preferred orders or moral principles. (PDF pp.110-112) |
| Form / content | ✅ Gauba explicitly shows that the wording "is" or "ought" cannot by itself classify a statement. (PDF pp.110-111) |
| Value-neutral / value-indifferent | ⚠️ Behaviouralism aimed at separating empirical explanation from ethical evaluation, but Gauba's post-behavioural section shows that this never meant the discipline could ignore the consequences of what it chose to study or neglect. |

## 5. Thinker-by-thinker positions

| Thinker | Position in this topic |
|---|---|
| Vernon Van Dyke | ✅ Defines approach as criteria of selection and philosophical analysis as clarification plus evaluation. (PDF pp.108-109, 113) |
| Leo Strauss | ✅ Reasserts political philosophy as knowledge of facts and standards of the good, and criticizes value-free behaviouralism. (PDF pp.112, 114, 121) |
| Graham Wallas | ✅ Uses psychology to challenge the overly rational image of man in politics and urges attention to actual behaviour. (PDF p.118) |
| Arthur Bentley | ✅ Pushes toward analysis of informal group processes rather than institutions in isolation. (PDF p.118) |
| Charles Merriam | ✅ Calls for scientific rigour, interdisciplinary borrowing and policy-science orientation. (PDF pp.118-119) |
| G.E.G. Catlin and Harold Lasswell | ✅ Develop value-free power analysis and politics as the study of who gets what, when, how. (PDF p.119) |
| David Easton | ✅ Defines behavioural tenets, then leads the post-behavioural turn and systems analysis. (PDF pp.120-125) |
| Gabriel Almond | ✅ Builds structural-functional analysis on the political-system framework. (PDF pp.127-129) |
| Karl Deutsch | ✅ Turns communication and cybernetics into a model of political analysis. (PDF pp.129-131) |
| Marx / Engels | ✅ Offer a non-autonomous, base-superstructure analysis in which politics must be traced to class structure. (PDF pp.133-134) |

## 6. Competing perspectives

- ✅ Philosophical approach clarifies concepts and evaluates institutions against standards of the good, right and just. (PDF pp.113-114)
- ✅ Historical approach studies either historical laws/processes or the history of political thought in relation to its context. (PDF pp.114-115)
- ✅ Legal approach studies politics through law, constitutional framework and legally valid authority, while remaining limited in capturing informal forces. (PDF pp.115-116)
- ✅ Institutional approach studies the organization and functioning of government, parties and related institutions, yet risks over-description and neglect of informal processes. (PDF pp.116-117)
- ✅ Behaviouralism privileges observation, regularities, verification, techniques, quantification, value-neutrality, systematization, pure science and integration. (PDF pp.120-121)
- ✅ Post-behaviouralism shifts from knowledge-for-its-own-sake toward relevance, applied knowledge, action and concern with value choice. (PDF pp.121-122)
- ✅ Marxian analysis rejects the autonomy of politics and instead studies its hidden basis in the mode of production and class struggle. (PDF pp.133-134)

## 7. Internal debates and criticisms

- ✅ Gauba records the criticism that behaviouralism privileges reliable micro-level questions over broader but less tractable questions. (PDF p.121)
- ✅ Post-behaviouralism arises because excessive methodological sophistication left the discipline distant from war, dictatorship, nuclear threat and urgent public crises. (PDF pp.121-122)
- ✅ Legal and institutional approaches remain valuable, but each is limited by relative neglect of informal behaviour, violence, social forces or broader explanation. (PDF pp.116-117)
- ✅ Systems analysis is useful comparatively, yet Gauba notes its weakness in analysing political power and mass political behaviour such as voting. (PDF pp.125-126)
- ✅ Structural-functional analysis is criticized for conservatism, weak power analysis, and projecting liberal-democratic benchmarks onto developing societies. (PDF p.129)
- ✅ Communications theory is useful but limited for ideology, power structure, allocation of resources, violence and revolution. (PDF pp.130-131)
- ❌ Post-behaviouralism allows unsupported moral assertion. -> ✅ Gauba presents it as value-conscious science, not as an escape from disciplined inquiry. (PDF pp.121-122)

## 8. Comparative matrix

| Approach | Core question | Preferred data or unit | Strength | Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Philosophical | ✅ What is right, just or conceptually coherent? (PDF pp.113-114) | ✅ Concepts, arguments, standards. (PDF pp.113-114) | ✅ Clarifies ends and critiques institutions. (PDF pp.113-114) | ⚠️ Cannot by itself supply behavioural verification. |
| Historical | ✅ How did ideas or political processes emerge over time? (PDF pp.114-115) | ✅ Past events and texts. (PDF pp.114-115) | ✅ Restores context and continuity. (PDF pp.114-115) | ✅ Risks living on inherited ideas without new synthesis. (PDF p.115) |
| Legal | ✅ What do laws and constitutions authorize? (PDF pp.115-116) | ✅ Legal provisions, procedures, judgments. (PDF pp.115-116) | ✅ Captures binding framework and validity. (PDF p.116) | ✅ Misses extra-legal behaviour and informal forces. (PDF p.116) |
| Institutional | ✅ How are political offices and agencies organized and related? (PDF pp.116-117) | ✅ Organs of government and parties. (PDF pp.116-117) | ✅ Gives politics disciplinary identity. (PDF p.116) | ✅ Over-descriptive; neglects informal processes and individuals. (PDF p.117) |
| Behavioural | ✅ How do actual actors behave, and what regularities can be found? (PDF pp.118-121) | ✅ Voters, leaders, attitudes, participation, quantified data. (PDF pp.119-121) | ✅ Scientific rigour and explanatory ambition. (PDF pp.120-121) | ✅ Method-heavy, micro-biased, value-neutral. (PDF pp.121-122) |
| Post-behavioural | ✅ How can rigorous inquiry address urgent social problems? (PDF pp.121-122) | ✅ Behavioural findings plus public problems and value questions. (PDF pp.121-122) | ✅ Relevance and action. (PDF pp.121-122) | ⚠️ Can be vague if relevance is asserted without analytical discipline. |
| Marxian | ✅ What class structure and mode of production generate politics? (PDF pp.133-134) | ✅ Economic structure, class relations, historical stages. ## 9. Application without current-affairs dependence

- ⚠️ A university election can be studied philosophically by asking what fair representation requires.
- ⚠️ The same election can be studied historically by tracing how student institutions evolved on that campus.
- ⚠️ It can be studied legally by examining the rules, eligibility and valid procedures.
- ⚠️ It can be studied institutionally by mapping offices, committees and roles.
- ⚠️ It can be studied behaviourally by surveying turnout, attitudes, campaigning and peer influence.
- ⚠️ It can be studied post-behaviourally by asking whether that knowledge helps solve exclusion, violence or distrust in the institution.
- ⚠️ It can be studied through systems analysis as inputs, outputs and feedback, or through decision-making analysis by tracking who chose what among which alternatives.
