## 1. One-screen advanced map

- ✅ Gauba structures the chapter as a deliberate movement from liberal fairness, to libertarian entitlement, to socialist and anti-state critiques, and finally to feminist, subaltern and communitarian enlargements of justice. (PDF pp.446-473)
- ✅ Rawls tries to moralize distribution without abandoning liberal freedom. (PDF pp.446-450)
- ✅ Nozick historicizes holdings and moralizes property against welfare redistribution. (PDF pp.450-452)
- ✅ Marxism relocates injustice from pattern of shares to capitalist exploitation and class power. (PDF pp.452-459)
- ✅ Democratic socialism retains social justice but refuses dictatorship and insists on democratic freedoms. (PDF pp.459-461)
- ✅ Anarchism radicalizes anti-domination by treating coercive authority as itself the generator of injustice. (PDF pp.461-464)
- ✅ Feminist and subaltern perspectives expose how formally equal systems can still encode structured subordination. (PDF pp.464-469)
- ✅ Communitarianism attacks the abstract self and asks whether justice can be intelligible apart from historically situated communities and shared goods. (PDF pp.469-473)
- ⚠️ Advanced takeaway: the dispute is not merely about "who gets more," but about what counts as a person, a wrong, a good, and a legitimate site of distribution.

## 2. Scope, assumptions and conceptual boundary

- ✅ The chapter is about rival perspectives on justice, not a single authorial doctrine that settles them. (PDF p.446)
- ✅ Gauba's discussion of Rawls and Nozick belongs to late-20th-century Anglo-American debate, while Marxist, anarchist, feminist, subaltern and communitarian discussions widen the normative frame. (PDF pp.447-473)
- ⚠️ "Justice" here includes distributive, recognitive and anti-domination concerns even where Gauba's own phrasing remains distributive.
- ⚠️ The chapter therefore has a widening logic: from fair shares, to title, to exploitation, to gender, to hegemony, to embedded community.
- ⚠️ Boundary: examples involving USSR, China, East Europe, UN reports and development rankings are book-period illustrations and cannot be silently updated into current evidence.
- ⚠️ Boundary: communitarian critique of abstract selves must not be turned into endorsement of authoritarian homogenization.

## 3. Direct-book argument reconstruction

- ✅ Gauba begins by saying modern political theory is deeply tied to the problem of justice and immediately lists eight major perspectives. (PDF p.446)
- ✅ He first presents Rawls because liberal justice tries to preserve liberty while accommodating equality and fraternity through a procedural device. (PDF pp.446-450)
- ✅ Rawls's method abstracts agents from social location through the original position and veil of ignorance so that chosen principles are fair to the least advantaged. (PDF pp.447-448)
- ✅ Gauba then turns to Nozick, who rejects patterned distribution and asks instead how specific persons came to hold specific goods. (PDF pp.450-452)
- ✅ The Marxist section shifts the terrain: if capitalist production rests on surplus extraction, then "fairer distribution" within capitalism may merely soften, not remove, injustice. (PDF pp.452-459)
- ✅ Democratic socialism revises Marxism by preferring evolutionary change, limited socialization and the coexistence of socio-economic justice with liberal freedoms. (PDF pp.459-461)
- ✅ Anarchism presses further by claiming the coercive state itself corrupts natural sociability and blocks just voluntary association. (PDF pp.461-464)
- ✅ Feminism and subaltern theory then insist that justice must see systematic subordination—gendered, classed, caste-like, cultural and hegemonic—not just contractual exchange. (PDF pp.464-469)
- ✅ Communitarianism closes the chapter by attacking liberal neutrality, the isolated self and one-size-fits-all distribution, and by recovering embedded selves, shared traditions and sphere-specific meanings of goods. (PDF pp.469-473)

## 4. Definitions and distinctions

| Distinction | Advanced note |
|---|---|
| Procedural fairness / historical entitlement | ✅ Rawls justifies outcomes through fair choice of principles, whereas Nozick justifies holdings through their actual path of acquisition and transfer. (PDF pp.447-452) |
| Substantive liberty / formal liberty | ✅ Gauba presents liberal-Rawlsian justice as concerned with substantive liberty, but libertarian justice as focused on formal liberty and limited state. (PDF pp.446, 450) |
| Patterned distribution / anti-patterned distribution | ⚠️ This is the cleanest advanced shorthand for Rawls-versus-Nozick, though Gauba himself explains it through distribution rules versus modes of acquisition. (PDF pp.447-452) |
| Exploitation / inequality | ✅ For Marxism, the central wrong is not bare inequality but unpaid surplus labour made possible by capitalist property relations. (PDF pp.453-455) |
| Recognition / redistribution | ⚠️ Feminist and subaltern sections show that justice cannot be reduced to income or wealth alone because power, dignity, participation and status are also unequally distributed. (PDF pp.464-469) |
| Isolated self / situated self | ✅ Liberal justice is criticized for abstracting individuals from social ends; communitarian justice sees persons as embedded in social roles, practices and traditions. (PDF pp.469-472) |
| Simple equality / complex equality | ✅ Walzer rejects uniform equalization of all goods and instead prevents one sphere, especially money, from dominating all other spheres. (PDF pp.472-473) |

## 5. Thinker-by-thinker positions

| Thinker | Position used in this topic |
|---|---|
| Rawls | ✅ Justice as fairness, chosen behind a veil of ignorance, yields lexical priority to liberty, then fair opportunity, then the difference principle. (PDF pp.447-450) |
| Nozick | ✅ Justice is title-based, not end-state-based; self-ownership and voluntary exchange limit legitimate redistribution. (PDF pp.450-452) |
| Marx and Engels | ✅ Capitalism is unjust because labour creates value while capital appropriates surplus; communism aims to abolish private ownership of major productive assets and class rule. (PDF pp.453-459) |
| Lenin / Mao | ✅ Gauba uses them to explain book-period socialist-state functions such as socialization, planning and ideological-cultural transformation in transition. (PDF pp.456-458) |
| Laski / Durbin | ✅ Democratic socialism seeks social justice through democratic method, welfare provision and civil liberties. (PDF pp.460-461) |
| Proudhon / Bakunin / Kropotkin / Tolstoy / Gandhi / Bookchin | ✅ These anarchist or anarchist-adjacent figures share suspicion of coercive authority, though they diverge on violence, property and moral regeneration. (PDF pp.462-464) |
| Wollstonecraft / J.S. Mill | ✅ Early feminism demands equal rational and civic standing for women against inherited legal-political exclusion. (PDF pp.465-466) |

| MacIntyre / Sandel / Taylor / Walzer | ✅ Communitarian thinkers dispute the atomistic self, liberal neutrality and universal distributive formulas detached from community meanings. (PDF pp.470-473) |

## 6. Competing perspectives

- ✅ Rawlsian liberalism asks what fair persons would choose if stripped of bargaining advantage. (PDF pp.447-448)
- ✅ Libertarianism asks whether each holding arose from self-ownership, just acquisition and voluntary transfer. (PDF pp.450-452)
- ✅ Marxism asks who owns production, who performs labour and who captures surplus. (PDF pp.453-455)
- ✅ Democratic socialism asks how social justice can be constitutional, democratic and welfare-oriented without dictatorship. (PDF pp.459-461)
- ✅ Anarchism asks whether coercive institutions themselves are the deepest injustice. (PDF pp.461-464)
- ✅ Feminism asks whether apparently neutral arrangements are actually built on gendered subordination and exclusion from power. (PDF pp.464-468)
- ✅ Subaltern theory asks who produces social wealth yet remains marginalized because elite hegemony naturalizes unequal reward. (PDF pp.467-469)
- ✅ Communitarianism asks whether justice can be understood without a prior account of shared ends, virtues and the meanings of goods in a community. (PDF pp.469-473)
- ⚠️ The progression also shows a movement from individual-centered justice theories to structurally and socially embedded justice theories.
- ⚠️ Together, these perspectives widen justice from distribution of goods to structure of ownership, authority, recognition, dignity and belonging.

## 7. Internal debates and criticisms

- ✅ Gauba records collectivist criticism that Rawls may legitimate capitalism by allowing large inequalities so long as the worst-off improve somewhat. (PDF p.448)
- ✅ He records Marxist criticism that Rawls's veil of ignorance detaches justice from real class and property relations. (PDF p.449)
- ✅ He records libertarian criticism that Rawls sacrifices liberty and incentives for equality. (PDF p.449)
- ✅ He records communitarian criticism that Rawls's neutrality evades the pursuit of the common good. (PDF p.449)
- ✅ Gauba partly answers these by reading Rawls as a convergence of libertarian, egalitarian and communitarian elements. (PDF pp.449-450)
- ✅ Nozick is criticized for treating welfare taxation as morally suspect while leaving structurally generated market injustice untouched. (PDF p.452)
- ✅ Gauba's own Marxist conclusion is double-edged: communism is ethically attractive, yet new dominance may reappear through bureaucratic or political power even after private property is abolished. (PDF p.459)
- ✅ Democratic socialism is normatively attractive but institutionally fragile because it depends on strong public opinion, resources and incentives. (PDF p.461)
- ✅ Anarchism is criticized as fascinating but programmatically weak, overly optimistic about human nature and inattentive to regulatory needs of large modern societies. (PDF p.464)
- ✅ Walzer's communitarianism is criticized by Gauba for strong moral reasoning but weak political machinery. (PDF p.473)
- ⚠️ Feminist and subaltern critiques additionally expose a blind spot in many older distributive theories: persons enter distribution already marked by hierarchy, dependence and misrecognition.
- ❌ Communitarian critique = rejection of rights. -> ✅ Gauba's communitarian section critiques liberal abstraction and neutrality, not the very possibility of norm-guided justice. (PDF pp.469-473)

## 9. Application without current-affairs dependence

- ⚠️ In a scholarship-distribution question, Rawls asks whether the rule protects fair opportunity and the least advantaged; Nozick asks whether anyone's earnings are being unjustly appropriated to fund it.
- ⚠️ In a land-reform question, Marxism asks whether ownership patterns institutionalize exploitation; democratic socialism asks how reform can be constitutional and welfare-enhancing.
- ⚠️ In a workplace-gender question, feminist justice asks whether apparently neutral rules reproduce exclusion from power, promotion or property.
- ⚠️ In a question on caste- or class-based exclusion, subaltern justice asks whether the dominant group's worldview has been normalized as universal interest.
- ⚠️ In a question on admission, office, health care or political office, Walzer's "complex equality" helps show why money should not purchase every social good.
- ⚠️ In a question on village self-rule or decentralization, anarchist and Gandhian strands can be contrasted with socialist and welfare-state expectations about regulation.

- ⚠️ Prompt: "Discuss communitarian critique of liberal justice." -> Contrast right over good, isolated self over embedded self, and simple equality over complex equality.

- ⚠️ Prompt: "Why is recognition central to justice?" -> Use feminist and subaltern sections to show that invisible hierarchies distort both reward and dignity.
