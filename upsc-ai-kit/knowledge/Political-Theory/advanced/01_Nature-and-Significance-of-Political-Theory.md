## 2. Scope, assumptions and conceptual boundary

- ✅ This debate emerges inside Gauba's broader definition of political theory as including both political science and political philosophy; it is not a separate problem from the opening definition (Gauba, PDF pp.17-20, 23-27).
- ✅ The behavioural challenge is directed chiefly against traditional speculation, historical classicism and explicit normativity, not against all theory whatsoever (Gauba, PDF pp.24-25).
- ⚠️ The key exam boundary is this: post-behaviouralism criticizes value-neutral closure, but it does not reject empirical inquiry or scientific discipline.
- ⚠️ The decline/revival debate is best read as a dispute over the proper balance between explanation and justification, not as a simple fight between "modern" and "old" writers.

## 3. Direct-book argument reconstruction

1. ✅ Gauba reports David Easton's 1953 complaint that traditional political theory relied on speculation, lacked acute observation of political reality, stayed tied to classics and history of ideas, and had little relevance for contemporary politics (Gauba, PDF p.24).
2. ✅ Easton argued that there had been no outstanding political philosopher after Marx and J.S. Mill and that political scientists lacked research tools capable of explaining fascism, communism and policy relevance in wartime decision-making (Gauba, PDF p.24).
3. ✅ His remedy was behavioural political science: causal theory, closer integration with other social sciences, and suspension of the quest for values in favour of explaining political behaviour (Gauba, PDF p.24).
4. ✅ Gauba then notes Easton's own 1969 shift: the post-behavioural revolution asked political science to address contemporary crisis and restored concern with values while retaining scientific investigation (Gauba, PDF pp.24-25).
5. ✅ Alongside Easton, Cobban described decline under both capitalist and communist systems and still insisted that political science must evolve criteria of judgment beyond social-science methodology alone (Gauba, PDF p.25).
6. ✅ Lipset went further by claiming that in the United States the long search for the good society had effectively ended because liberal democracy itself approximated it in operation (Gauba, PDF p.25).
7. ✅ Against this, Strauss argued that positivist political science ignored normative issues and flattened qualitative distinctions among values; Germino identified revival with political philosophy; Marcuse warned that a natural-science style social science could become an instrument of social control (Gauba, PDF pp.25-27).
8. ✅ Gauba closes by saying that, since the 1970s, political science and political philosophy increasingly test and inform each other rather than remaining sealed apart (Gauba, PDF p.27).

## 4. Definitions and distinctions

| Concept | Advanced clarification |
|---|---|
| Causal theory | ✅ A theory explaining relations of cause and effect, central to Easton's behavioural ambition (Gauba, PDF p.24). |
| Behaviouralism | ✅ A scientific approach centred on actual political behaviour, causal explanation and methodological affinity with other social sciences (Gauba, PDF pp.18-19, 24-25). |
| Post-behaviouralism | ✅ Easton's 1969 turn toward applying science to crisis and reconnecting inquiry with values and public relevance (Gauba, PDF pp.24-25). |
| Positivism | ✅ The view that scientific method is the only source of true knowledge and that knowledge rests on sense-experience (Gauba, PDF p.26). |
| Political philosophy | ✅ Critical inquiry into right and wrong, right order, justification and perennial social questions, not reducible to behavioural metrics (Gauba, PDF pp.19-20, 26-27). |
| Detachment | ✅ Germino's point is that detachment is not the same as ethical neutrality; one may critically engage without becoming partisan ideology (Gauba, PDF p.26). |

## 5. Thinker-by-thinker positions

| Thinker | Position to retain |
|---|---|
| David Easton (1953) | ✅ Traditional theory was speculative, historically bound and methodologically weak; political science needed causal, behavioural, social-scientific foundations (Gauba, PDF p.24). |
| David Easton (1969) | ✅ Political science should become practically relevant to crisis-ridden society and revive concern with values without discarding scientific inquiry (Gauba, PDF pp.24-25). |
| Alfred Cobban | ✅ Political theory had declined under both capitalist and communist systems, yet politics still required criteria of judgment that narrow methodology alone could not supply (Gauba, PDF p.25). |
| Seymour Martin Lipset | ✅ In mid-twentieth-century United States, democracy was presented as the good society in operation, which implied reduced need for grand normative searching (Gauba, PDF p.25). |
| Leo Strauss | ✅ Positivist political science is itself a symptom of decline because it evades the hierarchy and seriousness of normative questions (Gauba, PDF pp.25-26). |
| Dante Germino | ✅ Revival means recovery of political philosophy as critical inquiry into right order, beyond reductionist behaviouralism and beyond ideology (Gauba, PDF pp.26-27). |
| Herbert Marcuse | ✅ Over-measured social science can normalize the status quo by converting critique into observable, administrative categories (Gauba, PDF p.27). |
| David Held | ✅ The systematic tasks of political theory remain necessary so politics is not abandoned to ignorance or naked will to power (Gauba, PDF p.23). |

## 6. Competing perspectives

| Perspective | Strongest claim | Gauba's treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Behaviouralist | ✅ Valid theory should explain political behaviour causally and scientifically (Gauba, PDF p.24). | ✅ Gauba takes the scientific demand seriously but does not let it exhaust theory (Gauba, PDF pp.18-19, 27). |
| Positivist/value-neutral | ✅ Values are subjective preferences or beyond reliable knowledge (Gauba, PDF pp.16-17, 26). | ✅ Rejected as too narrow because public policy needs value-judgment and justification (Gauba, PDF pp.17, 23). |
| Classical normative/traditionalist | ✅ Political inquiry must ask about right order, justice and the good society (Gauba, PDF pp.19-20, 26). | ✅ Preserved, but not as licence for ungrounded speculation. |
| Post-behavioural synthesis | ✅ Facts and methods matter, but relevance, values and crisis-response matter too (Gauba, PDF pp.24-25, 27). | ✅ This is the chapter's most balanced settlement. |

## 7. Internal debates and criticisms

- ✅ Easton's early criticism bites hardest where traditional theory merely repeats classic texts without explaining contemporary political behaviour; Gauba does not deny that problem (Gauba, PDF p.24).
- ✅ Yet Easton's own post-behavioural turn is itself evidence that pure behavioural closure was insufficient for political inquiry in times of crisis (Gauba, PDF pp.24-25).
- ✅ Cobban and Lipset represent different decline narratives: one says modern systems marginalize theory; the other says liberal democracy has already solved the central normative search, at least in affluent Western context (Gauba, PDF p.25).
- ✅ Strauss rejects behavioural flattening of value-rankings; Germino rejects any reunion that strips theory of perennial moral concerns; Marcuse rejects metrics that convert critique into social control (Gauba, PDF pp.25-27).
- ⚠️ The debate therefore has two levels: a methodological dispute over evidence, and a civilizational dispute over whether politics can ever avoid argument about ends.
- ⚠️ Gauba's own synthesis is closer to "critical empiricism" than either crude positivism or free-floating moralism.

## 8. Comparative matrix

| Issue | Early behavioural critique | Normative revival critique | Gauba's settlement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Problem in old theory | ✅ Too speculative, too historicist, too detached from causal analysis (Gauba, PDF p.24). | ✅ Science-first inquiry neglects right and wrong, hierarchy of values and critical vision (Gauba, PDF pp.25-27). | ⚠️ Both criticisms capture part of the truth. |
| Role of values | ✅ Suspend or minimize them in science-first inquiry (Gauba, PDF p.24). | ✅ Restore them as central to political philosophy (Gauba, PDF pp.25-27). | ✅ Values are indispensable for ends and policy judgment (Gauba, PDF pp.23, 27). |
| Role of empirical method | ✅ Central and foundational (Gauba, PDF p.24). | ✅ Not rejected, but subordinated to broader critical purposes by many revivalists (Gauba, PDF pp.26-27). | ✅ Essential for understanding facts and strengthening means (Gauba, PDF p.27). |
| Public relevance | ✅ Behavioural science wanted policy relevance and decision-making status (Gauba, PDF p.24). | ✅ Critics wanted relevance in the sense of speaking truth to power and resisting status quo legitimation (Gauba, PDF pp.26-27). | ⚠️ Relevance requires both explanatory power and normative courage. |
| Final verdict | ❌ Political theory is dead. | ❌ Science is useless. | ✅ Neither claim holds; theory survives as an integrated enterprise (Gauba, PDF p.27). |

## 9. Application without current-affairs dependence

- ⚠️ If asked to analyse a policy on preventive detention, a behavioural approach can study compliance, opposition and institutional effects, but a philosophical approach must still ask whether liberty is being unjustifiably restricted.
- ⚠️ If asked whether elections deepen democracy, science can measure turnout and participation, while philosophy must judge whether those patterns amount to meaningful freedom, equality and legitimacy.
- ⚠️ If a regime claims "efficiency" as its justification, behavioural data may show administrative output, but political philosophy asks whether efficiency without justice or consent is politically defensible.

## 12. Factual-risk checks

- ✅ Easton's 1953 and 1969 positions are distinct and should not be merged; Gauba explicitly presents a later post-behavioural correction (Gauba, PDF pp.24-25).
- ❌ Post-behaviouralism rejects science. -> ✅ Gauba says it revives values without compromising scientific method for the study of facts (Gauba, PDF p.19).
- ❌ Political theory means only normative theory after the revival. -> ✅ Gauba's final line still keeps political science and political philosophy in complementary relation (Gauba, PDF p.27).
- ✅ Lipset's "good society" claim is a mid-twentieth-century contextual thesis about Western democracy, not a timeless factual conclusion for all societies (Gauba, PDF p.25).
- ⚠️ Marcuse, Strauss and Germino are best used as correctives to scientism, not as excuses for evidence-free assertion.
