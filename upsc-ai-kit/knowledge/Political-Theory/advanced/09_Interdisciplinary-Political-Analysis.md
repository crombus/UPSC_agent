## 1. One-screen advanced map

```text
POLITICAL CLAIM
   |
   +--> HISTORICAL CHECK: did it hold across time/cases?
   +--> ECONOMIC CHECK: what resource conflicts and incentives shape it?
   +--> SOCIOLOGICAL CHECK: what groups, norms and structures carry it?
   +--> PSYCHOLOGICAL CHECK: what attitudes, fears and identities sustain it?
   +--> PHILOSOPHICAL CHECK: is it conceptually clear and normatively defensible?
   |
   v
INTEGRATED POLITICAL JUDGMENT
   = explanation + verification + evaluation
```

✅ Gauba's conclusion is that political inquiry should be based on and verified from all relevant social sciences, not on a single partial approach. (PDF p.146)

## 2. Scope, assumptions and conceptual boundary

- ✅ The chapter treats politics as a social process and the political system as an open system linked to a wider social environment. (PDF pp.137-139)
- ✅ Interdisciplinarity here means selective, purposive borrowing directed toward political understanding, not intellectual tourism across disciplines. (PDF p.137)
- ❌ It does **not** mean that political theory is absorbed into economics, sociology or psychology; Gauba explicitly rejects merger into a single master science. (PDF p.146)
- ⚠️ This file therefore stays at the level of concept-routing: how a political theorist chooses evidence and checks arguments.
- ⚠️ Boundary rule: governance can illustrate administrative consequences, and economics can explain material mechanisms, but this topic is about how those inputs are used in political analysis.

## 3. Direct-book argument reconstruction

1. ✅ Gauba first defines disciplines and then marks the social sciences as especially suited to interdisciplinary work because they study overlapping aspects of human society. (PDF p.136)
2. ✅ He then specifies three functions of interdisciplinarity in political science: use other disciplines' findings, verify political theories through them, and contribute back to their understanding. (PDF p.137)
3. ✅ He warns that such widening must remain focused on politics; labour relations or crowd psychology matter here only insofar as they affect political outcomes. (PDF p.137)
4. ✅ The methodological push comes from empirical orientation: observable facts, causal explanation, generalization and prediction displaced purely ideal-state speculation. (PDF p.138)
5. ✅ Behavioural analysis widened the unit of study from formal institutions to the behaviour of voters, elites, bureaucrats, judges and groups. (PDF pp.138-139)
6. ✅ Systems analysis further required study of inputs, outputs and feedback between political and social systems. (PDF p.137, PDF p.139)
7. ✅ Political science thus began using other disciplines' data, theories and models, including elite theory, base-superstructure analysis, structural-functionalism, problem-solving models and market analogies. (PDF pp.139-140)
8. ✅ Gauba then demonstrates the specific uses of history, economics, sociology, psychology and philosophy for political analysis. (PDF pp.141-146)
9. ✅ He ends by saying single-discipline approaches remain partial, while the interdisciplinary approach aims at an integrated view without abolishing disciplinary boundaries. (PDF p.146)

## 4. Definitions and distinctions

| Pair | Advanced distinction |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Empirical / Normative** | Empirical inquiry explains what exists through facts and causal relations; normative inquiry evaluates what ought to be through values and standards. (PDF p.138) |
| ✅ **Political science / Political philosophy** | Political science helps discover facts and operational tools; political philosophy clarifies goals, obligation, justice and the moral worth of systems. (PDF pp.145-146) |
| ✅ **Interdisciplinary / Single-discipline** | Interdisciplinary analysis checks a political claim against multiple relevant bodies of evidence; single-discipline analysis remains partial. (PDF p.146) |
| ✅ **Use of data / Use of models** | Data include literacy, income distribution, kinship patterns and attitudes; models include elite theory, structural-functionalism and economic bargaining analogies. (PDF pp.139-140) |
| ❌ **Broader scope / Loss of focus** | Gauba permits broader scope only when the main focus remains political. (PDF p.137) |

## 5. Thinker-by-thinker positions

- ✅ **Aristotle:** the classical "master science" image blurred normative and empirical aspects and did not sharply separate politics from wider social life. (PDF p.146)
- ✅ **Marx and Engels:** early systematic interdisciplinarians because they related politics to technology, production and class structure. (PDF p.137)
- ✅ **Heinz Eulau:** political behaviour must be studied within wider social, cultural and personal contexts. (PDF p.139)
- ✅ **Seymour Martin Lipset:** political development depends on social mobilization, value change, kinship structure and class/status relations. (PDF p.140)
- ✅ **Auguste Comte:** science itself emerges from philosophical foundations; Gauba uses this to soften a rigid philosophy/science divide. (PDF p.145)
- ✅ **David Easton:** his political-system framework is borrowed from sociology, showing direct interdisciplinary transfer into political science. (PDF p.140)
- ✅ **Harold Lasswell:** Gauba cites the problem-solving model as an example of psychology feeding political analysis. (PDF p.140)

## 6. Competing perspectives

- ✅ **Historical perspective:** treats the past as a storehouse of data and causal patterns for political verification. (PDF p.141)
- ✅ **Political-economic perspective:** looks at production, distribution, exchange, economic demands and the state's role in reconciling them. (PDF pp.142-143)
- ✅ **Political-sociological perspective:** studies the political subsystem in relation to social structures, norms, leadership and participation. (PDF pp.143-144)
- ✅ **Political-psychological perspective:** explains attitudes, personality, learning, propaganda and extremist appeal. (PDF pp.144-145)
- ✅ **Political-philosophical perspective:** clarifies ends, values, obligation, good life and the moral standing of political systems. (PDF pp.145-146)
- ⚠️ These are best seen as complementary lenses: explanation improves when their evidence is combined rather than ranked in a fixed hierarchy.

## 7. Internal debates and criticisms

- ✅ Gauba notes older claims like "history is past politics" as oversimplifications because political science has gained an independent identity. (PDF p.141)
- ✅ He also presents the science/philosophy split as exaggerated because science itself originates in philosophy and still requires philosophical validation of ends. (PDF p.145)
- ❌ **Economic reductionism:** Marxian or market models can illuminate politics, but the chapter does not say economics alone settles political analysis. (PDF pp.140, 142-143, 146)
- ❌ **Behaviouralist reductionism:** focus on observable behaviour corrected older formalism, but Gauba also records the later revival of justice, liberty, equality and community. (PDF pp.145-146)
- ⚠️ A useful criticism of pure interdisciplinarity is diffusion of focus; Gauba pre-empts this by insisting on purposeful relevance to politics. (PDF p.137)

## 8. Comparative matrix

### Evidence-routing matrix

| Route | What it contributes | What it verifies or clarifies | Example political-theory use | What it cannot settle alone |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ **Historical verification** | Past cases, sequences, roots and patterns. (PDF p.141) | Whether a political theory survives rule-and-exception testing. (PDF p.141) | Testing claims about democracy, state formation or international conflict. | ❌ Present moral worth of a system. |
| ✅ **Political economy** | Production, distribution, exchange, resource conflict and policy incentives. (PDF pp.142-143) | Whether material interests and economic demands explain political conflict or policy. | ✅ **Political sociology** | Social structures, status, values, institutions, participation and leadership. (PDF pp.143-144) | Whether a political arrangement fits the social basis that sustains it. | ✅ **Political psychology** | Attitudes, personality, learning, propaganda and leadership style. (PDF pp.144-145) | Whether mass appeal, obedience or extremism can be traced to psychological mechanisms. | ✅ **Philosophical clarification/evaluation** | Conceptual clarity, ends, values and moral appraisal. (PDF pp.145-146) | Whether concepts like justice, liberty or obligation are coherent and defensible. ## 9. Application without current-affairs dependence

- ⚠️ **Hypothetical 1:** "Why does one democracy survive while another collapses?" -> route through history for antecedents, economics for inequality, sociology for cleavages, psychology for authoritarian appeal, and philosophy for legitimacy.
- ⚠️ **Hypothetical 2:** "Should a state regulate markets for welfare?" -> route through economics for incentives and distribution, sociology for class effects, and philosophy for justice and common good.
- ⚠️ **Hypothetical 3:** "Why do citizens obey harmful propaganda?" -> route through psychology for attitude formation, sociology for group identity, and philosophy for truth and responsibility.
- ⚠️ The point is not to pile disciplines mechanically, but to ask which discipline supplies the missing evidence in a political argument.
