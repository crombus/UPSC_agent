## 1. One-screen conceptual map

```text
POLITICAL QUESTION
     |
     +--> HISTORY --> roots, sequences, verification over time
     +--> ECONOMICS --> production, distribution, conflict over resources
     +--> SOCIOLOGY --> groups, institutions, status, political culture
     +--> PSYCHOLOGY --> attitudes, personality, propaganda, leadership
     +--> PHILOSOPHY --> concepts, values, ends, moral evaluation
     |
     v
INTEGRATED POLITICAL ANALYSIS
     |
     +--> explains facts better
     +--> tests claims against wider evidence
     +--> keeps politics as the main focus
```

✅ Gauba defines an interdisciplinary approach as relying on more than one discipline for a given problem and drawing on related disciplines where phenomena overlap. (PDF p.136)

## 2. Why this topic matters

- ✅ Political science studies politics as a social process, so it must use findings, theories and models from other social sciences where they illuminate political phenomena. (PDF pp.136-137)
- ✅ Interdisciplinary inquiry widens perspective, helps verify political claims through other social sciences, and also enables political science to contribute back to them. (PDF p.137)
- ✅ The chapter argues that single-discipline approaches are partial, whereas an interdisciplinary approach seeks an integrated view of politics. (PDF p.146)
- ⚠️ For UPSC, this topic prevents shallow answers that explain democracy, state action or mass behaviour through only law, only morality or only economics.

## 3. Essential definitions

| Term | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Discipline** | A branch or field of learning. (PDF p.136) |
| ✅ **Interdisciplinary approach** | Use of more than one discipline to study a problem, especially where common or overlapping phenomena are involved. (PDF p.136) |
| ✅ **Empirical approach** | Focus on observable facts, explanation, correlation, generalization and prediction. (PDF p.138) |
| ✅ **Normative approach** | Focus on values, prescriptions and what ought to be done. (PDF p.138) |
| ✅ **Political economy** | Analysis of political aspects of economic policy-making and of the mutual impact of economic forces and political decisions. (PDF p.143) |
| ✅ **Political sociology** | Study of political institutions, processes and ideologies in relation to social structures and modes of thought. (PDF p.144) |
| ✅ **Political psychology** | Use of psychological models and techniques to analyse political behaviour, attitudes, learning, socialization, leadership and propaganda. (PDF p.145) |
| ✅ **Political philosophy** | Inquiry into moral and substantive dimensions of politics, including values, obligation, good life and the worth of political systems. (PDF p.146) |

## 4. Core argument / chapter spine

1. ✅ Gauba begins by treating social sciences as related disciplines dealing with common human problems, which makes interdisciplinarity especially suitable to politics. (PDF p.136)
2. ✅ He insists that political inquiry should borrow from other disciplines purposefully, always to understand political problems, not randomly for their own sake. (PDF p.137)
3. ✅ Empirical orientation, behavioural analysis and systems analysis pushed political science toward other social sciences because real politics cannot be grasped by ideal-state speculation or formal institutions alone. (PDF pp.138-139)
4. ✅ Political science therefore uses data, theories and models from history, economics, sociology, psychology and philosophy. (PDF pp.139-146)
5. ✅ Yet the conclusion is not merger into one master science; the integrated approach keeps politics central while using all relevant disciplines for verification and understanding. (PDF p.146)

## 5. Essential distinctions

- ✅ **Interdisciplinary vs random borrowing:** Gauba warns that expansion across disciplines must be purposeful and well-conceived. (PDF p.137)
- ✅ **Empirical vs normative:** empirical inquiry asks what is; normative inquiry asks what ought to be. (PDF p.138)
- ✅ **Political science vs political philosophy:** scientific-behavioural studies help find facts and tools, while political philosophy helps determine goals of public policy. (PDF pp.145-146)
- ❌ **Interdisciplinary = dissolving political theory into economics or sociology.** Gauba rejects this; the approach integrates relevant disciplines but does not merge them into one master science. (PDF p.146)
- ⚠️ **Governance boundary:** governance explains delivery mechanisms; this topic uses governance-like evidence only when it sharpens a political concept.
- ⚠️ **Economy boundary:** economics explains market and production mechanisms; political theory uses it to understand conflict, welfare, class and policy choice, not to replace economics as a discipline.

## 6. Thinkers and positions

- ✅ **Aristotle:** the classical approach treated politics as the "master science" and did not clearly separate normative from empirical analysis. (PDF p.146)
- ✅ **Marx and Engels:** Gauba credits them with an early systematic interdisciplinary approach by locating political behaviour in socio-economic factors such as production and class structure. (PDF p.137)
- ✅ **Heinz Eulau:** political behaviour analysis must be interdisciplinary because political action occurs in a wider social, cultural and personal context. (PDF p.139)
- ✅ **Seymour Martin Lipset:** political development cannot be studied without social mobilization, value change, kinship patterns, caste and class relations. (PDF p.140)
- ✅ **Auguste Comte:** science itself is rooted in philosophy; Gauba uses Comte to question any rigid opposition between science and philosophy. (PDF p.145)

## 7. Illustrative examples

- ✅ Gauba says a political scientist assessing whether democracy will be sustained may need data on literacy, income distribution, ownership patterns, religious attitudes, family structure and rural-urban composition. (PDF p.139)
- ✅ Labour-management relations or crowd psychology should be studied politically only when they illuminate their impact on politics. (PDF p.137)
- ✅ Political development requires understanding social mobilization and value change, not merely constitutions or legal forms. (PDF p.140)
- ✅ Political communication, propaganda, charismatic leadership and public opinion formation draw directly on political psychology. (PDF pp.144-145)
- ⚠️ A good UPSC answer on welfare politics can therefore move from economic scarcity, to class/group conflict, to mass attitudes, and finally to philosophical evaluation of justice.

## 8. Comparison table

| Discipline | What it mainly contributes | Typical political-theory use | Limit if used alone |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ **History** | Verification through past events, sequences and long-term causation. (PDF p.141) | Testing a theory about state formation or democracy against historical cases. | ❌ May become mere chronicle if not politically interpreted. |
| ✅ **Economics** | Insight into production, distribution, exchange, scarcity and economic conflict. (PDF pp.142-143) | Understanding welfare state claims, class conflict, policy choice and political economy. | ❌ Cannot by itself settle legitimacy or justice. |
| ✅ **Sociology** | Group life, institutions, status, attitudes, participation and political culture. (PDF pp.143-144) | Explaining political socialization, cleavage, leadership and legitimacy. | ❌ Risks reducing politics to social structure alone. |
| ✅ **Psychology** | Motives, attitudes, personality, propaganda and leadership appeal. (PDF pp.144-145) | Explaining extremist appeal, public opinion and charismatic authority. | ❌ Cannot alone explain institutions or economic conflict. |
| ✅ **Philosophy** | Conceptual clarification, values, moral foundations and ends of public life. (PDF pp.145-146) | Clarifying liberty, equality, obligation, justice and the goals of policy. | ❌ Without empirical grounding, it can become purely speculative. |

## 9. UPSC traps and corrections

- ❌ **"Interdisciplinary study means political science has lost its identity."** -> ✅ Gauba says political science remains focused on politics while using other disciplines for broader and better-grounded analysis. (PDF pp.137, 146)
- ❌ **"Empirical political science made philosophy irrelevant."** -> ✅ Gauba says political philosophy regained prominence after post-behaviouralism and still helps define policy goals. (PDF pp.145-146)
- ❌ **"History and political science are the same thing."** -> ✅ Gauba treats this older formula as an oversimplification; history is used selectively and critically. (PDF p.141)
- ❌ **"One discipline can fully explain politics."** -> ✅ The chapter concludes that philosophical, historical, legal, economic, sociological and psychological approaches are partial when used alone. (PDF p.146)

## 11. Revision capsule / answer spine

> ⚠️ **Answer spine:** Politics is an open, socially embedded process; therefore political analysis must borrow evidence and models from history, economics, sociology, psychology and philosophy, while still keeping the political question at the centre.

- ✅ Define interdisciplinarity as purposeful use of more than one discipline. (PDF p.136)
- ✅ Show why empirical orientation, behaviouralism and systems analysis pushed political science outward. (PDF pp.138-139)
- ✅ Explain the contribution of each major discipline in one line. (PDF pp.141-146)
- ✅ End with Gauba's conclusion: single-discipline approaches are partial; integration is necessary, but merger is not. (PDF p.146)
- ⚠️ In Mains, move from **evidence** -> **explanation** -> **normative evaluation**.
- ⚠️ In Prelims, remember the paired terms: political economy, political sociology, political psychology, political philosophy.
