## 1. One-screen conceptual map

- ✅ Method means procedure of inquiry; approach means procedure plus criteria for selecting problems and data. (PDF pp.108-109)
- ✅ Traditional and contemporary are historical labels; they do not exactly coincide with normative and empirical. (PDF pp.109-110)
- ✅ Empirical inquiry studies what can be observed and tested; normative inquiry judges what ought to be preferred or prescribed. (PDF pp.110-112)
- ✅ Behaviouralism pushed political science toward scientific study of actual political behaviour, regularities, verification and quantification. (PDF pp.118-121)
- ✅ Post-behaviouralism kept scientific gains but demanded relevance, action and value-conscious responsibility. (PDF pp.121-122)
- ⚠️ UPSC key: this topic tells you not only what politics is, but how different scholars choose to study it.

## 2. Why this topic matters

- ✅ Gauba shows that disputes in political theory are often methodological before they become ideological. (PDF pp.108-122)
- ✅ It helps distinguish description from prescription without collapsing one into the other. (PDF pp.110-112)
- ✅ It introduces the major traditional approaches—philosophical, historical, legal and institutional—and the later behavioural turn. (PDF pp.112-122)
- ⚠️ This topic directly extends the theory/science distinction mapped for the module in `Political-Theory/README.md` and `Political-Theory/00_Master-Framework.md`.
- ⚠️ In Optional answers, clean method language often separates average answers from high-scoring answers.

## 3. Essential definitions

| Term | Exam-ready meaning |
|---|---|
| Method | ✅ The procedure of inquiry by which reliable knowledge and conclusions are obtained. (PDF p.108) |
| Approach | ✅ A wider frame that includes method plus criteria for selecting problems, questions and relevant data. (PDF pp.108-109) |
| Empirical statement | ✅ A claim about an observable situation that can be tested or verified by repeated observation. (PDF pp.110-111) |
| Normative statement | ✅ A claim expressing a preference, duty or moral principle about what ought to be valued or prescribed. (PDF pp.110-112) |
| Behaviouralism | ✅ A scientific orientation focusing on actual political behaviour, especially of actors such as voters, leaders and groups. (PDF pp.118-121) |
| Post-behaviouralism | ✅ A corrective within behavioural political science stressing relevance, action and concern for values. (PDF pp.121-122) |

## 4. Core argument / chapter spine

- ✅ Gauba begins by separating method from approach so that students do not confuse tools of inquiry with frames of inquiry. (PDF pp.108-109)
- ✅ He then warns that traditional/contemporary and normative/empirical are not identical pairs. (PDF pp.109-110)
- ✅ He clarifies that empirical vs normative turns on the content of the claim, not just the grammatical presence of "is" or "ought". (PDF pp.110-112)
- ✅ The traditional approaches are then outlined: philosophical, historical, legal and institutional. (PDF pp.112-117)
- ✅ The contemporary shift is marked by behaviouralism, post-behaviouralism and several models of political analysis. (PDF pp.117-134)
- ⚠️ The chapter's deeper message is integrative: political science is weakened both by value-blind technique and by unsupported moral assertion.

## 5. Essential distinctions

- ✅ Method vs approach: methods obtain and use data; approaches decide which questions and data count as relevant. (PDF pp.108-109)
- ✅ Traditional vs contemporary: the first cluster is associated with philosophy, history, law and institutions, while the second focuses more on process, behaviour and analysis models. (PDF pp.109-110, 117-118)
- ✅ Empirical vs normative: empirical asks what is observable and testable; normative asks what is right, just or preferable. (PDF pp.110-112)
- ✅ Form vs content: a sentence using "ought" may still be empirical if it states an instrumentally testable condition, while a sentence using "is" may still be normative if it defines a value like justice. (PDF pp.110-111)
- ✅ Pure science vs relevance: behaviouralism stressed reliable inquiry; post-behaviouralism added action and public responsibility. (PDF pp.121-122)

## 6. Thinkers and positions

| Thinker / stream | Position in this chapter |
|---|---|
| Vernon Van Dyke | ✅ Clarifies the distinction between approach and method and explains philosophical analysis as conceptual clarification plus evaluation. (PDF pp.108-109, 113) |
| Leo Strauss | ✅ Defends political philosophy as inseparable from facts and values and criticizes behavioural value-neutrality. (PDF pp.112, 114, 121) |
| Graham Wallas and Arthur Bentley | ✅ Early forerunners of behaviouralism who shift attention toward actual behaviour and informal processes. (PDF p.118) |
| Charles Merriam | ✅ Calls for scientific rigour, interdisciplinarity and behavioural inquiry. (PDF pp.118-119) |
| Harold Lasswell | ✅ Develops politics as the analysis of power and policy. (PDF p.119) |
| David Easton | ✅ Gives both the behavioural framework and the later post-behavioural call for relevance and action. (PDF pp.120-122) |

## 7. Illustrative examples

- ✅ "Everybody ought to vote if democracy is to work" is empirical in content because its claim can be checked against political functioning. (PDF pp.110-111)
- ✅ "Justice is treating equals equally..." is normative in content even though it appears in the form of an "is" statement. (PDF p.110)
- ✅ Legal analysis of Indian politics would ask what constitutional provisions authorize institutions and actions. (PDF pp.115-116)
- ✅ Institutional analysis would map legislature, executive, judiciary, parties and their formal relations. (PDF pp.116-117)
- ⚠️ Behavioural analysis of the same political scene would ask how voters, leaders, parties and interest groups actually behave.

## 8. Comparison table

| Approach cluster | Core claim | Main focus | Limitation flagged by Gauba |
|---|---|---|---|
| Traditional | ✅ Politics is illuminated through philosophy, history, law and institutions. (PDF pp.109-117) | ✅ Concepts, thought, legal framework, institutions. (PDF pp.113-117) | ✅ Often descriptive or normative without enough behavioural/process analysis. (PDF pp.117-118) |
| Behavioural | ✅ Political science should study actual political behaviour scientifically through regularities, verification, techniques and quantification. (PDF pp.118-121) | ✅ Individuals, attitudes, participation, communication, decision-processes. (PDF pp.119-121) | ✅ Risk of excessive micro-focus, method-fetish and value-neutral distance from urgent public issues. (PDF pp.121-122) |
| Post-behavioural | ✅ Science should remain rigorous but also relevant, action-oriented and attentive to value-construction. (PDF pp.121-122) | ✅ Social problems, public responsibility, applied knowledge. (PDF pp.121-122) | ⚠️ It must not become a licence for untested moralism; relevance still needs disciplined inquiry. |

## 9. UPSC traps and corrections

- ❌ Method and approach are synonyms. -> ✅ Approach is wider because it includes selection-criteria for problems and data. (PDF pp.108-109)
- ❌ Traditional always means normative, and contemporary always means empirical. -> ✅ Gauba explicitly rejects that mechanical equation. (PDF pp.109-110)
- ❌ Any sentence with "ought" is normative. -> ✅ Its content may still be empirical if it states an instrumentally testable condition. (PDF pp.110-111)
- ❌ Value-free means politically consequence-free. -> ✅ Behavioural inquiry can shape what gets studied and ignored, which is why post-behaviouralism raised the issue of relevance. (PDF pp.121-122)
- ❌ Post-behaviouralism means abandoning science for activism. -> ✅ Easton presents it as retaining behavioural gains while reconnecting them to problem-solving. (PDF pp.121-122)

## 11. Revision capsule / answer spine

- ✅ Method = procedure; approach = procedure plus problem/data selection. (PDF pp.108-109)
- ✅ Empirical vs normative depends on content, not grammar alone. (PDF pp.110-112)
- ✅ Traditional approaches: philosophical, historical, legal, institutional. (PDF pp.112-117)
- ✅ Behaviouralism: scientific study of actual behaviour, eight key tenets, micro-level reliability. (PDF pp.118-121)
- ✅ Post-behaviouralism: relevance, action and responsible value concern without discarding scientific gains. (PDF pp.121-122)
- ⚠️ Best answer line: political inquiry needs both disciplined evidence and defensible value judgment.
