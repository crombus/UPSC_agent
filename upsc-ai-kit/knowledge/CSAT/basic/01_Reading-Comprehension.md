# Reading Comprehension - MUST-DO

> **Subject:** CSAT | **Tier:** Must-Do (foundation) | **Family:** Comprehension (largest three-year family).
> **Core skill:** answer strictly from the passage - central idea, inference, assumption, tone/scope.
> **Grounded in:** audited UPSC CSAT PYQ sets 2024-2026 (Set A) - the largest family in each audited

## 1. Visual foundation

```text
STEM FIRST  ->  what is asked?
   |             (idea / inference / assumption / tone / detail)
   v
READ PASSAGE ONCE  ->  mark: claim, scope words, contrast words
   |
JUDGE EACH OPTION AGAINST THE TEXT ONLY
   |
   +--> stated & on-scope?      keep
   +--> true but not stated?    reject (outside knowledge)
   +--> too broad / too narrow? reject
   +--> distorts the passage?   reject
   v
CHOOSE the option the passage can DEFEND
```

**Core proposition:** in a passage-only paper the winning option is the one **entailed by the
text**, not the one that is true in the real world.

## 2. The four question types and their tests

| Type | The stem sounds like | The correct option must... |
|---|---|---|
| ✅ **Central idea / main message** | "best summarises / crux / the author mainly says" | cover the **whole** passage, not one sentence. |
| ✅ **Inference** | "can be inferred / most logical inference" | be **forced to be true** if the passage is true. |
| ✅ **Assumption** | "the argument assumes / rests on" | be an **unstated premise** the argument needs. |
| ✅ **Tone / attitude / scope** | "the author's tone / attitude / this passage is about" | match the **wording and range** of the text. |

> 🔑 **Trap:** many wrong options are **factually correct** yet not supported by the passage. In a
> passage-only item, "true" is irrelevant; only "stated or entailed" wins.

## 3. Method (step by step)

1. **Read the stem first.** Knowing the type tells you what to hunt for.
2. **Read the passage once, actively.** Underline the author's **main claim**, **scope words**
   (some, most, all, only), and **contrast words** (but, however, although) - answers hide near these.
3. **Predict** the answer in your own words before looking at options.
4. **Test each option** against the text with the four filters (stated / scope / distortion /
   outside-knowledge).
5. **If two survive,** pick the **more modest, better-supported** one; extreme wording usually loses.

## 4. Distractor families (learn to name them)

| Distractor | How to spot it |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Out-of-passage truth** | A real fact the passage never states. |
| ✅ **Over-generalisation** | Turns "some/many" into "all/always/only/solely". |
| ✅ **Scope creep** | Adds a topic the passage did not discuss. |
| ✅ **Distortion** | Reverses cause-effect, or swaps a qualifier. |
| ✅ **Half-right** | One clause is supported, another is not - still wrong. |

## 5. Original solved examples

### 📝 Example A (inference)

> **Passage.** "When a city widens its roads to cut congestion, the extra capacity often fills with
> new car trips that people previously avoided. Within a few years travel times return to their old
> levels, while the city has spent heavily and lost green space. Planners increasingly argue that
> managing demand - through public transport and pricing - addresses congestion more durably than
> adding lanes."

**Q. Which inference is best supported?**
(a) Widening roads always makes congestion worse.
(b) Added road capacity can be offset by newly generated car trips.
(c) Cities should ban private cars.
(d) Public transport is cheaper than road building.

**Answer: (b).** The passage says extra capacity "often fills with new car trips", which is exactly
(b). (a) over-generalises ("always"); the passage says "often". (c) is never stated - a policy leap.
(d) is plausible in reality but the passage compares **durability**, not **cost** - out-of-passage.

### 📝 Example B (assumption)

> **Passage.** "The district should shift its midday-meal supply to nearby farmer groups. Local
> sourcing will cut transport delays and put money into the village economy."

**Q. The argument assumes that:**
(a) nearby farmer groups can supply food of adequate quantity and reliability.
(b) transport costs are the largest expense of the scheme.
(c) villagers currently oppose the midday-meal scheme.
(d) distant suppliers are dishonest.

**Answer: (a).** For "shift supply to nearby farmer groups" to work, the argument must silently rely
on those groups being **able to supply reliably** - remove (a) and the plan collapses, so it is the
assumption. (b) is an unstated ranking; (c) and (d) are not needed by the argument and are not stated.

### 📝 Example C (central idea)

> **Passage.** "Early weather warnings save lives only if the last mile works: the alert must reach a
> fisherman without a smartphone, in a language he reads, in time to act. Sophisticated forecasting
> is necessary but not sufficient; delivery decides the outcome."

**Q. The central idea is that:**
(a) forecasting technology is useless.
(b) fishermen should buy smartphones.
(c) an early warning helps only if it reaches people in a usable, timely form.
(d) India has the best forecasting systems.

**Answer: (c).** It captures the whole passage - forecasting **plus** last-mile delivery. (a)
contradicts "necessary"; (b) fixes on one detail; (d) is out-of-passage.

## 6. Must-Know habits

- ✅ Read the **stem before** the passage.
- ✅ "Best supported", not "true": choose what the passage **defends**.
- ✅ Watch quantifiers - **some/most/all/only** decide most inference items.
- ✅ For assumptions, use the **negation test**: if negating the option breaks the argument, it is the
  assumption.
- ✅ For central idea, reject options that are **only one sentence** of a multi-point passage.

## 7. Common traps

- ❌ Picking a **true** statement that the passage never makes. -> Ask "which line proves this?"
- ❌ Turning a qualified claim ("often", "many") into an **absolute** ("always", "all"). -> Reject.
- ❌ Importing **subject knowledge** (economics, science) into a passage-only item. -> Text only.
- ❌ Choosing the **longest / most sophisticated** option by reflex. -> Length is not correctness.
- ❌ Answering the **wrong question type** (giving a detail when the stem wants the main idea). ->
  Re-read the stem.

## 8. Quick checks

- ✅ Can you name the question type from the stem alone? (idea / inference / assumption / tone)
- ✅ For any option you keep, can you point to the **exact phrase** that supports it?
- ✅ Did you reject at least one option specifically for being **out-of-passage**?

## 9. Mini-drill (with answers and explanations)

> 📝 **Passage.** "Rooftop solar lets a household generate its own electricity and sell surplus power
> back to the grid. But adoption is slow: the upfront cost is high, and many tenants cannot install
> panels on a roof they do not own. Subsidies help with cost, yet they do nothing for the tenant who
> has no roof to use."

1. **The main point of the passage is that:**
   (a) rooftop solar has both a cost barrier and an ownership barrier, and subsidies address only the
   first.
   (b) rooftop solar is a failure.
   (c) all households should install solar panels.
   (d) subsidies are a waste of money.

2. **It can be inferred that:**
   (a) tenants are the main users of rooftop solar.
   (b) a subsidy alone will not make rooftop solar reach every household.
   (c) selling power to the grid is illegal.
   (d) rooftop solar produces no surplus power.

3. **The argument assumes that:**
   (a) installing panels normally requires control over the roof.
   (b) electricity from the grid is free.
   (c) landlords always refuse permission.
   (d) subsidies are unavailable in India.

**Answers.**

1. **(a).** It states both barriers and the limit of subsidies. (b)/(d) are over-negative and
   unstated; (c) is a policy leap.
2. **(b).** The passage explicitly says subsidies "do nothing for the tenant who has no roof", so a
   subsidy alone leaves some households out - a forced inference. (a) reverses the passage (tenants
   often **cannot** install); (c)/(d) contradict the text.
3. **(a).** "Cannot install panels on a roof they do not own" only bites if installation **needs**
   roof control - the unstated premise. (b)/(c)/(d) are not required by the argument.
