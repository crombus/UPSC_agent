## 1. BODIN — the first systematic theory ✅
- *Six Books of the Commonwealth* (1576). Sovereignty = *"the absolute and perpetual power of a commonwealth."*
- **Three marks:** **Absolute** (unlimited by any human superior), **Perpetual** (belongs to the office, not the transient ruler), **Undivided/Inalienable** (cannot be split or given away). ✅
- **Chief mark:** the power to **make law** — the sovereign gives law but is not bound by it (*legibus solutus*).
- **Crucial limit:** sovereign is still bound by **Divine law, Natural law, and the fundamental laws** of the realm (e.g. cannot arbitrarily seize property). → **NOT wholly unlimited** — the 2022 PYQ hinge. ⚠️

---

## 2. AUSTIN — legal/analytical (monist) ✅
- *The Province of Jurisprudence Determined* (1832). The classic **legal theory of sovereignty**.
- **Definition:** if a **determinate human superior**, not habitually obedient to anyone else, receives **habitual obedience** from the bulk of society, that superior is **sovereign**, and the society is politically independent. ✅
- **Law = command of the sovereign, backed by a sanction.** Sovereignty is: **determinate, absolute, indivisible, illimitable, permanent.**
- **Criticisms:** ignores popular/political sovereignty, custom & international law; "determinate superior" fits few real states; command theory too narrow (Hart's critique). ⚠️

---

## 3. KAUTILYA — Saptānga & dharmic realism ✅
- *Arthaśāstra*. Sovereignty embodied in the **Saptānga (7 limbs/prakṛtis)** of the state: **Swāmī** (king/sovereign), **Amātya** (ministers), **Janapada** (territory+people), **Durga** (fort), **Kośa** (treasury), **Daṇḍa** (army/force), **Mitra** (ally). ✅
- The **Swāmī (king)** is the head, but sovereignty is **organic** — the state = a body of interdependent elements (an early **systems** view, distinct from Bodin's abstract absolute).
- **Dharma-bound, not arbitrary:** the king's authority is legitimated by **rājadharma** and the welfare of subjects — *"in the happiness of his subjects lies the king's happiness."* Realist but ethically constrained. ✅
- **Mandala theory / foreign policy:** *"no permanent friend or enemy"* (2025 PYQ) — the raja-mandala of concentric friends/foes; the *ṣāḍguṇya* (six measures) show sovereignty exercised pragmatically. 📰 mirrors modern *realpolitik*.

---

## 4. LASKI — the pluralist demolition ✅
- *A Grammar of Politics*. **Rejects the monistic/absolute theory** (2023 PYQ). ✅
- **Arguments against absolute sovereignty:**
  1. The state is **only ONE among many associations** (church, trade union, family) each commanding real loyalty — society is **federal**, not pyramidal.
  2. Absolute sovereignty is **morally dangerous** — it breeds tyranny and ignores the individual's conscience.
  3. In practice, no state is truly unlimited (international law, functional/economic limits, moral limits). ✅
- **Positive view:** authority must be **distributed/pluralist**; obedience is *earned* by serving human needs, not commanded. Sovereignty is **limited, functional, and moral**.

---

## 5. THE CORE DEBATE — Monist vs Pluralist ⚠️
| Axis | Bodin | Austin | Kautilya | Laski |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nature | absolute, perpetual, undivided | legal command + sanction | organic (Saptānga) | divided, limited, functional |
| Limit | Divine/Natural law | none (illimitable) | dharma, subjects' welfare | many (moral, intl, associational) |
| Locus | the commonwealth | determinate superior | the Swāmī (+ 7 limbs) | plural associations |
| School | early absolutism | monist/legal | realist-dharmic | pluralist |

> 🔑 The whole item is **"is sovereignty absolute?"** — Bodin/Austin say yes (with Bodin's caveat), Kautilya makes it dharma-bound & organic, **Laski says NO**. Position the thinkers on this single axis.

## 8. PYQ-MAPPED MODEL-ANSWER SKELETONS ⚠️

### 8.1 — "Why did Laski reject absolute sovereignty?" (2023, 20m)
```
Intro : sovereignty = supreme authority; the monist claim (Bodin/Austin) vs Laski's pluralism.
B1    : state = ONE association among many (church, union) → society is federal.
B2    : absolute sovereignty morally dangerous (tyranny) + practically false (intl/functional limits).
Link  : contrast Austin's "determinate superior" — Laski distributes authority.
Assess: pluralism protects liberty but risks under-stating the state's coordinating role.
Concl : sovereignty must be limited, functional, moral — not absolute.
```

### 8.2 — "Insights of the Arthaśāstra on sovereignty; modern relevance." (2024, 15m)
```
Intro : Kautilyan sovereignty embodied in the Saptānga (7 limbs), headed by the Swāmī.
Body  : organic/systems view; dharma-bound (subjects' welfare); mandala + ṣāḍguṇya realism.
Relev : modern statecraft — national security, realpolitik ("no permanent enemy"), welfare state.
Crit  : monarchical/hierarchical assumptions; yet the welfare + realist balance endures.
Concl : an indigenous, ethically-anchored realism — sovereignty as responsibility, not mere power.
```
