## 1. THEORIES OF PUNISHMENT ✅
- **Retributive:** punishment is **deserved** for the wrong itself; *lex talionis* ("eye for an eye"). **Kant** — punishment is a **categorical imperative**, the criminal must be punished because he *deserves* it, never merely as a means/deterrent (treating a person as means violates dignity). **Hegel** — punishment "annuls" the crime & honours the criminal as a rational agent. ✅
- **Deterrent (Bentham, utilitarian):** punishment is justified by its **future good** — deterring crime; pain of punishment must exceed gain of crime. Forward-looking. ✅
- **Reformative/Rehabilitative:** aim to **reform/reintegrate** the offender; crime is a treatable social/psychological product; **restorative justice**. ✅
- **Preventive:** **incapacitate** (imprison/remove) to protect society. ✅
- **Proportionality (2023 PYQ):** severity of punishment ∝ seriousness of crime (Beccaria) — grounds objections to disproportionate/juvenile punishment. ⚠️

---

## 2. CAPITAL PUNISHMENT ✅ (the star sub-topic)
- **The question:** is the state ever justified in taking a life? **Retributivists** (grave crimes deserve death) & **deterrence** advocates say yes; **reformists & abolitionists** say no.
- **Arguments FOR:** retributive justice for heinous crimes; deterrence; closure for victims; incapacitation. ⚠️
- **Arguments AGAINST:** ✅
  - **Right to life is inviolable** (2025 PYQ — "can the right to life be absolute?"); the state lacks moral authority to kill.
  - **Irreversibility** — wrongful executions cannot be undone.
  - **No proven deterrent** (2024 PYQ) — empirical evidence weak; murderers rarely calculate.
  - **Discriminatory** — falls disproportionately on the poor/marginalised.
  - Precludes **reform**.
- **India 📰:** "**rarest of rare**" doctrine (*Bachan Singh*, 1980); Law Commission 262nd Report (2015) recommended abolition except terrorism. Balances retribution & reform.
- **"Can the right to life be absolute?" (2025):** even a fundamental right is bounded (self-defence, just war); but the **deliberate, premeditated** taking of a defenceless prisoner's life is the hardest case → abolitionist weight. ⚠️

---

## 3. CORRUPTION ✅
- **Definition:** abuse of entrusted/public power for private gain.
- **Systemic vs ethical (2022 PYQ):** ⚠️
  - **Systemic** — corruption is produced by **structures** (opaque processes, discretion, weak accountability, scarcity) → fix institutions.
  - **Ethical** — corruption is a **moral failure** of the individual (greed, weak integrity) → fix values/character.
  - **Best answer:** *both* — systemic incentives + ethical erosion interact; reform needs institutional design **and** integrity/ethics.
- **"Particularistic vs universalistic standards" (2025 PYQ):** corruption reveals the tension between **particular loyalties** (family, caste, favouritism) and **universalistic norms** (impartiality, rule of law, equal treatment). Corruption = letting the particular override the universal. ⚠️ 📰 (Links to GS-IV probity).

---

## 4. MASS VIOLENCE & GENOCIDE ✅
- **Genocide:** the deliberate destruction, in whole or part, of a national/ethnic/racial/religious group (Raphael Lemkin's term; UN Genocide Convention 1948). The **ultimate crime against humanity**. 📰
- **Hannah Arendt — "the banality of evil"** (*Eichmann in Jerusalem*): great evil is often done not by monsters but by **ordinary people who stop thinking**, who follow orders and bureaucratic routine. Evil as **thoughtlessness**. ✅
- **Philosophical issues:** collective vs individual responsibility; "just following orders" (no defence — Nuremberg); dehumanisation as the precondition of atrocity; the failure of conscience. ⚠️
- **Response:** international law (ICC), the duty to remember, human-rights education, R2P (Responsibility to Protect). 📰

## 7. PYQ-MAPPED MODEL-ANSWER SKELETONS ⚠️

### 7.1 — "Can the right to life be absolute? (capital punishment)" (2025, 20m)
```
Intro : the tension — inviolable right to life vs state's retributive/deterrent claim.
B1    : FOR CP — retribution (Kant), deterrence, incapacitation, victim justice.
B2    : AGAINST — irreversibility, no proven deterrence, discrimination, precludes reform,
        sanctity/right to life; "rarest of rare" as a compromise.
Assess: no right is wholly absolute, yet premeditated killing of a defenceless prisoner is the hardest case.
Concl : lean abolitionist/severely-restricted — the right to life is near-absolute; the state should not kill.
```

### 7.2 — "Is corruption a systemic or an ethical issue?" (2022, 10m)
```
Intro : corruption = abuse of public power for private gain.
Body  : systemic (structures, discretion, weak accountability) VS ethical (individual integrity);
        particularistic loyalties overriding universalistic norms.
Assess: it is BOTH — institutional design + ethics; neither alone suffices.
Concl : durable reform needs transparent systems AND a culture of probity.
```
