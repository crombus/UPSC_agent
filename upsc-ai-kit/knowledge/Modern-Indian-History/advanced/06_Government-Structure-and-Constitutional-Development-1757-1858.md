## 1. Snapshot & core idea

**Deeper — parliamentary control emerged through crisis, not design.**

- ✅ The Company's territorial authority after **1765** created a contradiction: a profit-seeking commercial corporation was collecting revenue, maintaining armies and dispensing justice in India.
- ✅ The **Regulating Act, 1773** was Parliament's first serious attempt to control this contradiction by giving Bengal primacy, establishing a Governor-General-in-Council and creating the Supreme Court at Calcutta.
- ✅ The Act's defects were immediate: conflict between the Governor-General and Council, tension between the Supreme Court and Company courts, and unclear limits of Bombay and Madras in war and diplomacy.
- ✅ The **Amending Act, 1781** attempted to remove these practical defects by clarifying jurisdiction and protecting official actions from excessive judicial interference.
- ⚠️ The constitutional story is therefore not a smooth liberal evolution; it is a sequence of emergency repairs to keep imperial extraction administratively stable.

**Deeper — centralisation created the Company-state.**

- ✅ **Pitt's India Act, 1784** produced dual government in Britain: the **Court of Directors** managed Company affairs while the **Board of Control** supervised political matters in the name of the Crown.
- ✅ **Charter Act, 1833** marked the high point of centralisation: one Governor-General of India, one central law-making council, a Law Member, and a Law Commission for codification.
- ✅ The Company's commercial functions ended in **1833**, making it a purely administrative and political instrument of British imperial rule.
- ✅ **Charter Act, 1853** separated legislative from executive functions and opened civil service recruitment through competitive examination, though practical access for Indians remained limited.
- ⚠️ The pre-1858 structure anticipated Crown rule: centralised executive, codified law, bureaucratic recruitment and parliamentary oversight were already in place before the formal transfer to the Crown.

## 4. Must-Know Facts (Prelims)

- ✅ **Regulating Act, 1773**: Governor-General of Bengal, Council of four, Supreme Court at Calcutta, parliamentary oversight.
- ✅ **Warren Hastings** was the first Governor-General of Bengal; **William Bentinck** was the first Governor-General of India.
- ✅ **Supreme Court at Calcutta** began in **1774**.
- ✅ **Pitt's India Act, 1784**: Board of Control + Court of Directors = dual government in Britain.
- ✅ **Act of 1786**: Cornwallis could override the council and combine Governor-General with Commander-in-Chief.
- ✅ **Charter Act, 1813**: monopoly ended except tea and China trade; ₹1 lakh education grant; missionaries allowed; Crown sovereignty asserted.
- ✅ **Charter Act, 1833**: Law Member, Law Commission, centralisation, end of Company commercial functions.
- ✅ **Charter Act, 1853**: separate legislative and executive councils; competitive examination for civil service.
- ⚠️ "Open recruitment" as an idea belongs to **1833**, but practical competitive examination belongs to **1853**.

## 5. UPSC Traps

> 🔑 Trap: The phrase **Company-state** means a hybrid body — legally a Company, practically a sovereign territorial state.

- ❌ The Crown ruled India directly after Pitt's India Act. → ✅ The Crown supervised through the Board of Control; direct Crown rule came only in **1858**.
- ❌ The Charter Act 1833 decentralised administration. → ✅ It strongly **centralised** legislation and executive authority.
- ❌ The Law Commission was created by the 1853 Act. → ✅ The first Law Commission followed the **1833 Act**.
- ❌ Competitive civil service immediately enabled equal Indian entry. → ✅ The exam was in London and later age limits restricted Indian access in practice.
- ❌ The 1773 Act solved all administrative defects. → ✅ Its defects required the **1781 Amending Act** and later reforms.

## ➕ Historiography note (PYQ-gap)

⚠️ Historians use the term **Company-state** to capture the East India Company's unusual nature: a commercial corporation exercising sovereign powers of revenue, war, justice and legislation. Nationalist readings stress exploitation and parliamentary control as devices for imperial extraction; administrative histories stress the growth of rule-bound institutions; recent scholarship highlights the hybrid, improvised and violent nature of Company sovereignty. For UPSC, the safe synthesis is: constitutional development before 1858 was not democratic self-government, but the creation of a centralised colonial state.
