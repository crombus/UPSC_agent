## 1. Snapshot & core idea

**Deeper — thesis: Rajiv modernised the economy's aspirations but destabilised the polity's secular-federal balance.**

- ✅ Rajiv's **technocratic modernisation** (telecom, computers, science missions, panchayati raj, literacy) began the shift away from the closed Nehruvian model, prefiguring 1991.
- ⚠️ Politically, however, his **over-centralisation, dependence on advisers and weak party control** replicated his mother's de-institutionalisation without her mass authority.
- ✅ The **Shah Bano reversal (1986)** and the **Ayodhya shrine opening** were a fatal attempt to **balance communal constituencies**, which instead energised both Muslim conservatism and Hindu mobilisation.
- ✅ **Bofors (1987)** converted an anti-corruption plank against him, and **V.P. Singh's Jan Morcha/National Front** turned Congress's own "clean government" idiom into its nemesis.

**Deeper — 1989–99: the simultaneous rise of caste, communal and coalition politics.**

- ✅ The **Mandal Commission (1990)** brought **OBC assertion** to the centre of national politics; the **rath yatra (1990)** and **Babri demolition (1992)** marked the ascent of **Hindutva mobilisation**.
- ✅ **1991 economic reforms** (Rao–Manmohan) responded to a **balance-of-payments crisis** and permanently altered the state–market relationship (developed in topic 38).
- ✅ **Congress dominance ended**: the decade's unstable coalitions — a 13-day BJP government (1996), the **United Front** (Deve Gowda, Gujral), and the **NDA under Vajpayee (1998–99)** — normalised **multi-party coalition governance**.
- ⚠️ Chandra's synthesis: "**Mandal, Mandir and Market**" — caste justice, communal mobilisation and liberalisation — jointly reconfigured Indian politics as the Nehruvian consensus dissolved.

## 4. Must-Know Facts (Prelims)

- ✅ Congress fell from a record majority (1984) to about **197 seats (1989)**; the **BJP rose from 2 (1984) to 86 (1989)**.
- ✅ The **National Front (1989)** was supported from outside by both the **BJP and the Left**.
- ✅ **Mandal** meant **27% OBC reservation**, taking total reservation toward **~49.5%** with SC/ST.
- ✅ The **1991 reforms** followed a **balance-of-payments crisis**; **Rajiv Gandhi was assassinated in 1991**.
- ✅ The **Babri Masjid** was demolished on **6 December 1992**.
- ✅ The **NDA under Vajpayee** formed in **1998**; the **1999** election consolidated it (BJP+allies ~296).

## 5. UPSC Traps

> 🔑 Trap: Chandra's "**Mandal, Mandir and Market**" captures the three simultaneous forces (1989–92) that dismantled the Nehruvian consensus.

- ❌ Rajiv was anti-reform. → He **began the modernising shift** that reforms later completed.
- ❌ Shah Bano and Ayodhya were unrelated to his decline. → The **twin concessions** damaged him with all sides.
- ❌ The National Front relied only on the Left. → It had **outside support from both the BJP and the Left**.
- ❌ Liberalisation was a gradual 1980s process. → It was a **decisive 1991 rupture** driven by crisis.
- ❌ Coalition governments were an aberration of the 1990s. → They became the **new normal** of national governance.

## ➕ Historiography note (PYQ-gap)

⚠️ Chandra reads 1989–99 as the unravelling of the Nehruvian consensus under three converging pressures — OBC assertion (Mandal), Hindutva mobilisation (Mandir), and liberalisation (Market). Critics of the "decline" narrative argue the era instead **deepened democracy**: it brought subordinated castes, regional forces and market energies into the mainstream, replacing elite consensus with genuine competition. A strong answer holds the tension: the period was both a **crisis of the old order** and a **democratic deepening**, and its coalition-plus-reform settlement defined 21st-century India. PYQ application: this is the anchor for "changing nature of Indian politics since the late 1980s" prompts.
