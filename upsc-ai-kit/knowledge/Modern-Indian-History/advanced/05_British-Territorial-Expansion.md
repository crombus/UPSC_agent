## 1. Snapshot & core idea

**Deeper — expansion moved from ring-fence to paramountcy.**

- ✅ Early Company policy often sought buffers, but after Bengal the Company increasingly used war, diplomacy, Residents and subsidies to dominate Indian states.
- ✅ Wellesley's **Subsidiary Alliance** transformed rulers into dependent allies: they hosted British troops, paid subsidies, accepted a Resident and surrendered independent external relations.
- ⚠️ This produced **indirect rule**: Indian rulers survived formally, but sovereignty shifted to the Company.
- ✅ Dalhousie's **Doctrine of Lapse** moved from indirect control to annexation by denying adoption rights in selected states.
- ✅ Awadh's annexation in **1856** used the separate justification of alleged misgovernance, showing that British expansion used multiple legal pretexts.

**Deeper — Indian resistance differed by region.**

- ✅ Mysore under Haidar Ali and Tipu Sultan resisted through military reform, diplomacy and attempts to balance British power.
- ✅ Maratha weakness lay in confederate rivalries; British diplomacy exploited divisions among the Peshwa, Scindia, Holkar, Bhonsle and Gaekwad.
- ✅ Sikh power was consolidated under Ranjit Singh, but after his death court factionalism and military tensions enabled British victory in the Anglo-Sikh wars.
- ⚠️ Bengal revenues, sepoy armies and superior logistics allowed the Company to fight repeated wars far beyond its original coastal bases.

## 4. Must-Know Facts (Prelims)

- ✅ **Treaty of Madras (1769)** ended the First Anglo-Mysore War.
- ✅ **Treaty of Mangalore (1784)** ended the Second Anglo-Mysore War; Haidar Ali died in **1782**.
- ✅ **Treaty of Seringapatam (1792)** ended the Third Anglo-Mysore War; Tipu ceded half his territory.
- ✅ **Fourth Anglo-Mysore War (1799)**: Tipu died; Wodeyars restored under Subsidiary Alliance.
- ✅ **Treaty of Salbai (1782)** ended the First Anglo-Maratha War.
- ✅ **Second Anglo-Maratha War (1803–05)**: Wellesley; Battle of Assaye; Scindia and Bhonsle submitted.
- ✅ **Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817–18)**: Peshwa Baji Rao II deposed; pensioned at Bithur.
- ✅ **Subsidiary Alliance**: Wellesley; Hyderabad first in **1798**; Resident and no independent diplomacy.
- ✅ **Doctrine of Lapse**: Satara 1848, Jaitpur, Sambalpur, Baghat, Udaipur, Jhansi 1853, Nagpur 1854.
- ✅ **Sindh** annexed in **1843** by Charles Napier; **Punjab** annexed in **1849** after the Second Anglo-Sikh War.
- ✅ **Awadh** annexed in **1856** on alleged misgovernance.

## 5. UPSC Traps

> 🔑 Trap: Expansion was not only battlefield conquest; it was a spectrum — buffer policy, Subsidiary Alliance, Resident control, Lapse, misgovernance and direct annexation.

- ❌ Subsidiary Alliance and Doctrine of Lapse were the same policy. → Alliance created dependency; Lapse annexed states by succession doctrine.
- ❌ Dalhousie introduced Subsidiary Alliance. → **Wellesley** systematised it from 1798.
- ❌ Wellesley annexed Jhansi under Lapse. → **Dalhousie** annexed Jhansi in 1853 under Lapse.
- ❌ British defeated united Maratha nationalism. → They defeated a **confederacy** weakened by internal rivalries.
- ❌ Anglo-Sikh wars were fought against Ranjit Singh. → They occurred after his death, when Punjab politics was unstable.
- ❌ Awadh's annexation was legally a lapse case. → It was justified by alleged **misgovernance**.

## ➕ Historiography note (PYQ-gap)

⚠️ Advanced answers should frame expansion as the growth of **paramountcy**. The Company did not simply conquer territory; it built graded sovereignty: protected allies, subsidiary states, annexed provinces and frontier buffers. Historians of indirect rule stress that subsidies transferred Indian revenues to support British armies, while Residents reshaped court politics. The trajectory runs from **ring-fence** and buffer logic to Wellesley's **Subsidiary Alliance**, then to Dalhousie's annexationist legalism.
