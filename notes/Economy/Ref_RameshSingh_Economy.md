## 1. Introduction

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Economics**: study of how individuals, society and government make prioritised choices while using scarce resources to satisfy wants.
- **Economy**: economics in practice in a defined region/country; the real picture arising from application of economic principles.
- **Organising an economy**: allocation decisions may be market-led, state-led or mixed.
- **Primary sector**: agriculture and allied extraction from nature; **secondary**: manufacturing/industry; **tertiary**: services.
- **Agrarian / industrial / service economy**: classification by dominant contribution to total output; India is service-led in output but still has high livelihood dependence on primary sector.
- **GDP**: value of all final goods and services produced within national boundaries in one year. Formula use: `C + I + G + (X-M)`.
- **NDP**: `GDP - depreciation`; depreciation is wear and tear/capital consumption.
- **GNP**: `GDP + net income from abroad`; includes net private remittances, interest on external loans and grants.
- **NNP**: `GNP - depreciation`; used as the purest form of national income; per-capita income = NNP / population.
- **Factor cost vs market price**: factor cost is producer/input cost; market price includes net product taxes. Book notes India shifted to market-price-based national accounts from 2015.
- **Current vs constant prices**: current price includes current inflation; constant price uses a base year to remove inflation effect.
- **Nominal, real, disposable income**: nominal is money income; real adjusts for inflation; disposable is income available after direct taxes.
- **GVA**: value of output minus intermediate consumption; it measures value added
  by producers/sectors. `GDP at market prices = GVA at basic prices + product
  taxes - product subsidies`.
- **NFIA**: net factor income from abroad; `GNP = GDP + NFIA`.
- **Three measurement routes**: production/value-added, income and expenditure
  methods should theoretically yield the same GDP after statistical adjustment.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
| Concept | Exam-use distinction |
|---|---|
| GDP vs GNP | GDP is territorial; GNP is national/resident-income based. |
| Gross vs Net | Net deducts depreciation. |
| Domestic vs National | National adds net income from abroad. |
| Market price vs factor cost | Market price = factor cost + product taxes - subsidies. |
| Constant vs current price | Constant price is better for real growth comparison. |

- **National income revision**: 9th ed. discusses base year shift to 2011-12 and CSO switch to market price.
- **Subsidies and taxes** affect conversion between factor cost and market price; direct taxes do not change factor/market valuation in the same way as indirect taxes.
- Avoid double counting by including final output or the value added at each stage,
  not both. Transfer payments and transactions in existing assets are not current
  production, although related service charges may enter GDP.

### Prelims traps
- GDP may rise while welfare, equality or quality of output does not.
- NNP is lower than GNP; NDP is lower than GDP because depreciation is deducted.
- GNP can be lower than GDP if net income from abroad is negative.
- Constant-price GDP is used for real growth; current-price GDP overstates growth under inflation.

### Mains angle
Use GDP/GNP/NNP distinctions to argue why India needs growth plus quality-of-life indicators, not output numbers alone.

## 2. Growth, Development and Happiness

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Progress**: broad, general betterment; not a precise economic measure.
- **Economic growth**: quantitative increase in economic variables over time, usually real GDP/GNP.
- **Economic development**: growth plus qualitative changes such as poverty reduction, education, health, equality and institutional transformation.
- **Human Development Index (HDI)**: UNDP composite of life expectancy, educational attainment and per-capita income.
- **Happiness / life satisfaction**: book links post-2010 policy thinking to well-being beyond income, including social norms, trust and behaviour.
- **Economic man/rational man**: conventional model of self-interested rational choice; challenged by behavioural economics.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Growth is **necessary but not sufficient** for development.
- Development measurement evolved from income to human development and then to broader well-being/happiness concerns.
- HDI is not a complete measure but corrects GDP-only bias by including health and education.
- Social norms, culture, trust, values and governance can influence development outcomes.

| Term | Nature | UPSC use |
|---|---|---|
| Growth | Quantitative | GDP, output, income rise |
| Development | Quantitative + qualitative | inclusive welfare, structural change |
| Human development | People-centric | health, education, income |
| Happiness | Subjective well-being | policy legitimacy, quality of life |

### Prelims traps
- Growth and development are not synonyms.
- Higher per-capita income does not automatically mean high human development.
- HDI includes GNI/per-capita income but is not an income-only index.
- Happiness indices are policy signals, not substitutes for hard socio-economic indicators.

### Mains angle
Frame development as expansion of human capabilities: growth must translate into health, education, dignity, trust and sustainable welfare.

## 3. Evolution of the Indian Economy

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Colonial economy**: economy structured to serve the metropolitan power through raw material supply, market for manufactured goods and drain of wealth.
- **Prime moving force debate**: whether agriculture or industry should lead development; India chose industry after Independence.
- **Mixed economy**: coexistence of public and private sectors, with the state taking a commanding role in strategic sectors.
- **Public sector emphasis**: state-led industrialisation, heavy industries and infrastructure through PSUs.
- **Planned economy**: coordinated allocation of resources through plans and targets.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Book identifies colonial inheritance: low literacy, low life expectancy, stagnant output, weak industry, poor infrastructure and neglected social sector.
- Post-Independence consensus favoured:
  - direct state responsibility for development;
  - ambitious public sector role;
  - heavy industry;
  - caution toward foreign investment;
  - economic planning.
- **Industry-first logic**: heavy industry and infrastructure were expected to create long-run self-reliance.
- **Agriculture-first critique**: India had surplus labour in agriculture and needed food security, rural demand and productivity rise.

### Prelims traps
- Mixed economy is not socialism; private sector operates but within planned/state-led priorities.
- Public sector was chosen for strategic development, not merely ownership preference.
- Colonial railways/infrastructure served extraction and administration, not balanced industrialisation.

### Mains angle
India's development path reflects a trade-off between self-reliant industrialisation and neglected agricultural/rural transformation.

## 4. Economic Planning

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Economic planning**: conscious, coordinated decision-making by a determinate authority on production, allocation, investment, trade and distribution, based on national objectives.
- **Indicative planning**: state sets broad targets/signals; market and private sector retain operational freedom.
- **Imperative/command planning**: state directly decides allocation and production targets.
- **Centralised planning**: plan formulation and control concentrated at the national level.
- **Decentralised planning**: lower levels participate in planning and implementation.
- **Rolling plan**: plan with annual revision and forward extension.
- **Perspective plan**: long-term plan frame within which medium-term plans operate.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- First national planning experiment: Soviet Union's Five-Year Plans beginning 1928.
- Planning gained appeal in colonial and newly independent countries as an instrument of rapid development.
- India used planning to pursue growth, self-reliance, social justice, industrialisation and balanced regional development.

| Planning type | Main feature | Trap |
|---|---|---|
| Imperative | Binding commands | associated with command economies |
| Indicative | Broad guidance | compatible with markets |
| Centralised | top-down | faster control, weaker local fit |
| Decentralised | bottom-up | better local needs, coordination challenge |

### Prelims traps
- Planning does not always mean abolition of markets.
- Democratic planning can be indicative and consultative.
- A plan may be physical, financial, perspective, annual, rolling or fixed-period.

### Mains angle
Planning remains relevant when markets underprovide public goods, infrastructure, regional balance and social justice.

<a id="ch5"></a>
## 5. Planning in India

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Planning Commission**: non-constitutional, advisory planning body set up in 1950; prepared plans, assessed resources and allocated plan assistance.
- **National Development Council (NDC)**: forum linking Centre, states and Planning Commission for approval and review of plans.
- **Five-Year Plans**: medium-term development strategy documents.
- **MPLADS**: scheme for MPs to recommend local works.
- **Multi-level planning**: Centre, state, district, block and local-level planning.
- **Inclusive growth**: growth with broad-based benefits, especially for marginalised groups.
- **NITI Aayog**: successor policy think tank to Planning Commission, focused on cooperative federalism, strategy and monitoring rather than plan fund allocation.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Pre-Independence planning proposals: Visvesvaraya Plan, FICCI proposal, Congress/National Planning Committee, Bombay Plan, Gandhian Plan, People's Plan, Sarvodaya Plan.
- Major planning objectives: growth, modernisation, self-reliance, poverty removal, employment, equity and regional balance.
- Book's critique of planning includes centralisation, weak perspective, employment failures, PSU overemphasis, industry bias, faulty location policy and politicisation.
- Plan financing phases shifted with public savings, deficit financing, external assistance and post-1991 reforms.
- **Central Sector schemes** are fully funded and implemented by the Union
  government; **Centrally Sponsored Schemes** are implemented by states with
  Centre-state cost sharing.
- Investment models include public investment, private investment and PPPs; the
  choice depends on public-good character, commercial viability, risk allocation
  and state capacity.

### ⚠️ Updated
- Planning Commission has been replaced by **NITI Aayog**; there are no Five-Year Plans after the Twelfth Plan. Policy now uses vision/strategy/action agenda style documents and scheme-based budgeting.

### Prelims traps
- Planning Commission was not constitutional/statutory.
- NITI Aayog does not allocate plan funds like the Planning Commission did.
- NDC and Finance Commission had different roles: plan coordination vs constitutional tax devolution/grants.

### Mains angle
Discuss shift from centralised planning to cooperative federalism and outcome-based governance.

## 6. Economic Reforms

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Economic reforms**: policy changes to alter the structure and functioning of the economy, especially after the 1991 crisis.
- **Liberalisation**: reducing state controls/licensing and freeing economic decision-making.
- **Privatisation**: increasing private sector role; may include disinvestment, strategic sale or reduced public monopoly.
- **Globalisation**: integrating domestic economy with global trade, investment, technology and finance.
- **Washington Consensus**: package of market-oriented reforms including fiscal discipline, trade liberalisation, privatisation and deregulation.
- **Obligatory reform**: reform undertaken under crisis/pressure, e.g., BoP crisis and IMF conditionalities.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- 1991 reforms were rooted in BoP crisis, fiscal stress, inefficient controls and global economic changes.
- LPG reform measures included industrial delicensing, trade liberalisation, exchange-rate reforms, financial-sector reforms, tax reforms and disinvestment.
- Book classifies reform generations:
  - **First generation**: product market, trade, exchange rate, industrial policy, financial-sector opening.
  - **Second generation**: factor markets, agriculture, labour, infrastructure, governance.
  - **Third generation**: local governance and decentralisation.
  - **Fourth generation**: knowledge economy, IT-enabled governance and human capital.

### Prelims traps
- Liberalisation, privatisation and globalisation are related but not identical.
- Reforms began in crisis but did not mean withdrawal of the state from welfare or regulation.
- Disinvestment is not always privatisation; government may retain ownership/control.

### Mains angle
Evaluate reforms as a shift from command-and-control to market-compatible state intervention, while noting unresolved employment, agriculture, inequality and capacity gaps.

## 7. Inflation and Business Cycle

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Inflation**: sustained rise in the general level of prices; not a rise in the price of one commodity.
- **Deflation/disinflation**: fall in the general price level or reduction in inflation rate; policy usage may imply demand/output compression.
- **Demand-pull inflation**: excess demand over supply; "too much money chasing too little output".
- **Cost-push inflation**: rise in input/factor costs pushing prices up.
- **Low/creeping inflation**: slow, predictable, single-digit inflation.
- **Galloping inflation**: very high double/triple digit inflation.
- **Hyperinflation**: extremely large and accelerating inflation with loss of currency confidence.
- **Bottleneck/structural inflation**: supply falls while demand remains stable.
- **Core inflation**: inflation excluding food and energy.
- **Inflationary gap**: excess government spending over national income/fiscal deficit pressure.
- **Deflationary/output gap**: spending shortfall leading to slowdown.
- **Inflation tax/seigniorage**: erosion of money value due to money creation.
- **Phillips Curve**: short-run inverse relation between inflation and unemployment; long-run trade-off disputed.
- **GDP deflator**: broad price index implicit in GDP calculation.
- **Base effect**: an unusually high or low comparison-period price level can
  mechanically depress or raise the measured year-on-year inflation rate.
- **Skewflation**: sharp inflation concentrated in a narrow set of commodities
  rather than a broad rise in the price level.
- **Reflation**: deliberate policy stimulus intended to lift prices and output
  from deflation or a severe slowdown.
- **Business cycle phases**: depression, recovery, boom, recession; growth recession is slowdown with positive but below-trend growth.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Inflation rate = `(Price index current - Price index previous) / previous × 100`.
- India historically used **WPI** prominently; CPI became central for monetary policy targeting.
- Anti-inflation tools:
  - supply side: imports, buffer stocks, distribution, anti-hoarding;
  - cost side: duty cuts, productivity and technology;
  - monetary: CRR/repo/bank rate/liquidity tightening.
- **Stagflation**: stagnation + inflation; important because standard demand management becomes difficult.
- **Double-dip recession**: a short recovery followed by another contraction.
- WPI largely tracks goods at the wholesale/producer stage and excludes services;
  CPI tracks the retail cost faced by consumers. PPI, housing-price and
  service-price indices answer different price-measurement questions and are not
  interchangeable with CPI.

### ⚠️ Updated
- CPI inflation targeting remains centred on **4% with a tolerance band of ±2%**. Since 2022, **SDF** is the lower bound of RBI's liquidity corridor, with repo as policy anchor and MSF as upper bound.

### Prelims traps
- Deflation is falling prices; disinflation is fall in the rate of inflation.
- Core inflation excludes volatile food/energy; this may be less socially representative in India.
- WPI excludes services and reflects wholesale prices; CPI reflects consumer cost of living.
- A recession is output contraction; growth recession is positive but inadequate growth.

### Mains angle
Inflation management in India needs monetary credibility plus food supply, logistics, storage and fiscal coordination.

## 8. Agriculture and Food Management

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Kharif**: monsoon-sown crops; **Rabi**: winter-sown crops.
- **Land reforms**: institutional changes in land ownership/tenancy/ceilings/consolidation.
- **Green Revolution**: HYV seeds, irrigation, fertilisers, pesticides, credit, extension and procurement support.
- **Cropping pattern**: allocation of land among crops.
- **MSP**: pre-announced minimum support price to protect farmers from price fall.
- **Procurement price**: price at which government procures foodgrains.
- **Issue price**: price at which foodgrains are issued through PDS.
- **Buffer stock**: foodgrain stock maintained for food security and price stabilisation.
- **Economic cost of foodgrains**: procurement cost + acquisition/handling/storage/distribution costs.
- **PDS**: public distribution system for subsidised food distribution.
- **Market Intervention Scheme**: support for perishable/non-MSP commodities during distress.
- **WTO Boxes**: Amber (trade-distorting support), Blue (production-limiting support), Green (minimal distortion), S&D (special treatment for developing countries).
- **SPS measures**: sanitary and phytosanitary standards for health safety in trade.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Food policy phases: scarcity/import dependence → Green Revolution/self-sufficiency → food management, diversification and sustainability.
- Land reform failures: poor records, legal loopholes, political resistance, weak implementation.
- Green Revolution impacts: food security and productivity gains but regional/crop skew, ecological stress and inequality.
- Food management chain: MSP → procurement → buffer stock/storage → PDS/open market sale → price stabilisation.
- Agriculture constraints: small holdings, low irrigation efficiency, seed/fertiliser/pesticide issues, weak extension, credit stress, marketing bottlenecks, farm waste and value-chain gaps.
- Food processing improves value addition, shelf-life, farmer income and employment.

### Source coverage additions
- **Cropping systems** include mixed cropping, inter-cropping, crop rotation,
  multiple cropping and diversification. They affect soil health, risk,
  irrigation demand and income stability.
- **Animal husbandry and allied activities** diversify farm income and can grow
  faster than crop output; they require feed, veterinary, breeding, processing
  and cold-chain support.
- **Decentralised Procurement Scheme** allows designated states to procure,
  store and distribute foodgrains for TPDS and other welfare schemes.
- **Open Market Sale Scheme** releases FCI grain into the market to moderate
  prices and manage excess stocks; the **Price Stabilisation Fund** supports
  market intervention in selected commodities.
- **Agricultural marketing chain:** farm gate -> aggregation/FPO -> mandi or
  direct market -> storage/cold chain -> processing -> wholesale/retail/export.
  APMC reform, e-NAM, transparent assaying and warehouse receipts target
  fragmentation and information asymmetry.
- **Commodity futures** assist price discovery and hedging but can face liquidity,
  manipulation and regulatory concerns; futures trading does not itself create
  physical supply.
- **Upstream requirements** include seed, fertiliser, machinery, irrigation,
  credit and extension; **downstream requirements** include storage, logistics,
  processing, marketing and retail.
- **Irrigation reform** requires expansion plus efficiency: drip/sprinkler,
  watershed management, command-area maintenance, crop alignment and "more crop
  per drop".
- **Input reform:** quality seed, balanced and soil-responsive fertiliser use,
  safe pesticide use, mechanisation suited to small holdings, institutional
  credit and effective extension services.
- **Risk management:** PMFBY covers specified crop risks; NMSA promotes
  climate-resilient and sustainable agriculture. Insurance cannot substitute for
  irrigation, extension or functioning markets.
- **WTO agriculture:** Aggregate Measurement of Support and Amber/Blue/Green/S&D
  boxes concern domestic support; export competition, market access and SPS
  rules are separate pillars/issues. **NAMA** concerns non-agricultural market
  access; the Swiss formula cuts higher tariffs proportionately more.
- **NFSA** creates statutory food-security entitlements and requires
  identification, delivery, grievance redress and transparency; it is broader
  than maintaining buffer stocks.
- **Food processing ecosystem:** cold chains, value addition, quality assurance,
  testing, R&D, skills and modern logistics reduce wastage and create non-farm
  employment. Mega Food Parks and related infrastructure schemes are enabling
  mechanisms, not substitutes for viable supply chains.

### Prelims traps
- MSP announcement is not the same as universal procurement.
- Issue price differs from MSP/procurement price.
- Green Box subsidies are permitted because they are minimally trade-distorting.
- Food subsidy burden includes difference between economic cost and issue price.

### Mains angle
Agriculture policy must move from production-centric food security to income security, diversification, climate resilience and market reform.

## 9. Industry and Infrastructure

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Industrial Policy Resolution 1948**: mixed economy with state role in strategic sectors.
- **Industrial Policy Resolution 1956**: commanding heights/public sector emphasis and industrial licensing.
- **New Industrial Policy 1991**: delicensing, reduced public-sector reservation, MRTP liberalisation, FDI/technology opening.
- **Disinvestment**: sale of government equity in PSUs; may be minority, majority, strategic or listing.
- **MSME**: micro, small and medium enterprises; key for employment, exports and regional dispersion.
- **Infrastructure**: enabling facilities such as power, roads, railways, ports, aviation, urban services and energy.
- **PPP**: public-private partnership for financing/constructing/operating infrastructure.
- **Viability Gap Funding**: public grant support to make socially useful PPP projects commercially viable.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Industrial policy moved from state-led import substitution to liberalised competitiveness.
- Industrial bottlenecks: licensing, technology gaps, infrastructure shortage, capital constraints and regulatory uncertainty.
- Infrastructure has high multiplier effects but needs long-term finance and regulatory credibility.
- PPP models may include BOT, BOOT, DBFOT, annuity and hybrid structures.
- Book highlights Make in India, Start-up India, ease of doing business, FDI policy, UDAY, smart cities, roads, railways, aviation, petroleum and renewable energy.
- Sector-specific constraints differ: steel/aluminium are energy- and
  infrastructure-intensive; apparel/footwear are employment-intensive and
  sensitive to logistics, scale and labour regulation.
- Infrastructure PPP success depends on realistic demand estimates, land and
  clearances, balanced risk allocation, contract enforcement and long-term
  finance. VGF addresses viability, not poor project design.

### Prelims traps
- Disinvestment ≠ always loss of government control.
- MSMEs are important for employment intensity, not only output.
- PPP does not mean full privatisation; risk allocation is central.
- Infrastructure industries and infrastructure sector are not identical categories.

### Mains angle
Industrial growth requires reliable infrastructure, regulatory predictability, MSME competitiveness, technology and export orientation.

## 10. Services Sector

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Services sector/tertiary sector**: activities not producing tangible goods but providing services—trade, transport, finance, education, health, IT, tourism, consultancy, R&D, legal/accountancy etc.
- **GVA in services**: value added by services activities.
- **Internal trade**: domestic distribution and sale of goods/services.
- **Trade in services**: cross-border services exchange governed by bilateral and WTO/GATS negotiations.
- **Construction development**: interface sector between industry and services with employment and infrastructure links.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- India shows a services-led growth pattern before full manufacturing maturity.
- Services contribute strongly to output growth, exports, urban employment and productivity spillovers.
- Major service concerns: regulation, skill gaps, standards, infrastructure, access, global mobility restrictions and domestic reform needs.
- Book contrasts manufacturing-led development with India's services-led trajectory.
- Under GATS, services may be supplied through cross-border delivery,
  consumption abroad, commercial presence or movement of natural persons.
  India's interest is especially strong in digitally deliverable services and
  professional mobility.

### Prelims traps
- High services share in GDP does not mean majority employment is in services.
- Services include high-productivity IT/finance and low-productivity informal trade/personal services.
- WTO services negotiations differ from goods trade negotiations.

### Mains angle
India must leverage services exports while creating manufacturing and formal service jobs to absorb labour.

## 11. Indian Financial Market

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Financial market**: market where funds move between fund-surplus and fund-scarce entities on interest/dividend basis.
- **Money market**: short-term funds market up to 364 days.
- **Capital market**: long-term funds market beyond 364 days.
- **Discount rate**: market rate for short-term money-market transactions, guided by liquidity and repo rate.
- **Unorganised money market**: indigenous bankers, moneylenders, chit funds/nidhis/loan companies.
- **Organised money market instruments**: Treasury Bills, Certificates of Deposit, Commercial Paper, Commercial Bills, Call Money, Money Market Mutual Funds, Repo/Reverse Repo, Cash Management Bills.
- **Mutual fund**: pooled investment managed by professionals through an AMC and trustees.
- **Project financing**: long-term finance for infrastructure/industrial projects.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
| Instrument | User | Maturity/use |
|---|---|---|
| Treasury Bill | Government | up to 364 days; issued at discount |
| Certificate of Deposit | Banks/FIs | negotiable deposit instrument |
| Commercial Paper | Corporates | unsecured short-term borrowing |
| Call Money | Banks/FIs | overnight to short notice |
| Repo | Banks/FIs with RBI | short-term borrowing against securities |
| CMB | Government | temporary cash mismatch, less than 91 days |

- Financial regulation in India is multi-agency: RBI, SEBI, IRDAI, PFRDA and government ministries.
- FSDC was created for financial stability and inter-regulatory coordination.
- **DFHI/primary dealers and market infrastructure** support liquidity in
  government securities and money markets. Regulation also includes
  quasi-regulators, self-regulatory arrangements and special statutes for
  particular intermediaries.

### Prelims traps
- Money market is maturity-based, not "market for currency only".
- Treasury Bills are money-market instruments; dated G-Secs are capital-market/debt instruments.
- Repo is both a money-market instrument and a monetary policy tool.
- Mutual funds invest across asset classes; not all mutual funds are equity funds.

### Mains angle
A deep financial market improves savings mobilisation, monetary transmission, infrastructure finance and risk diversification.

<a id="ch12"></a>
## 12. Banking in India

### Core concepts & definitions
- **NBFC**: company engaged in financial activity but not a bank; cannot perform all banking functions like demand deposits/cheque-based payments unless specifically allowed.
- **RBI**: central bank set up in 1935, nationalised in 1949; currency issuer, banker to government, bankers' bank, lender of last resort, forex reserve manager and monetary authority.
- **CRR**: share of bank deposits kept with RBI as cash.
- **SLR**: share of deposits maintained by banks in prescribed liquid assets.
- **Bank Rate**: RBI's long-term lending rate; aligned with MSF in book's period.
- **Repo rate**: rate at which RBI lends short-term funds against securities.
- **Reverse repo**: rate at which RBI absorbs short-term surplus from banks.
- **MSF**: overnight borrowing facility at penal/upper corridor rate.
- **LAF**: daily RBI facility to inject/absorb liquidity via repo/reverse repo.
- **OMO**: RBI sale/purchase of government securities to manage liquidity.
- **MCLR**: marginal cost of funds based lending rate; introduced from 2016 for better transmission.
- **Priority Sector Lending**: mandated bank credit to sectors such as agriculture, MSME, weaker sections etc.
- **NPA**: loan asset that stops generating income as per RBI norms.
- **SARFAESI**: law enabling secured creditors to enforce security without court intervention, subject to process.
- **CAR/CRAR**: capital adequacy ratio; bank capital as proportion of risk-weighted assets.
- **Basel Accords**: global prudential norms on capital, risk and liquidity.
- **Money supply**: RBI measures M1/M2/M3/M4; narrow money is more liquid, broad money includes time deposits.
- **Money multiplier**: ratio of money supply to reserve/base money.
- **Payments banks**: small-value payments/deposit banks with lending restrictions.
- **Small finance banks**: banks focused on underserved segments with lending function.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Banking nationalisation aimed at wider reach, social banking, priority lending and development.
- Financial-sector reforms followed Narasimham Committee recommendations: lower pre-emptions, prudential norms, competition and autonomy.
- NPA resolution toolkit in book: ARCs, SDR, S4A, AQR, SARFAESI, bad bank/PARA idea.
- Financial inclusion: PMJDY, credit counselling, small/payment banks, MUDRA, gold monetisation/sovereign gold bonds.
- India's banking structure also includes RRBs and cooperative banks, which are
  central to rural credit but face governance, capital and asset-quality
  constraints.
- **High-powered/reserve money** is the monetary base; broader money expands
  through bank deposits and credit. The money multiplier is not mechanically
  fixed because cash preference, excess reserves, credit demand and regulation
  matter.
- **ALM** manages maturity and interest-rate mismatches between bank assets and
  liabilities. Poor ALM can create liquidity stress even when a bank appears
  solvent.
- Wilful default is a conduct classification; it is not synonymous with every
  NPA. NPA resolution may use recovery law, insolvency, restructuring, ARCs or
  negotiated sale depending on the asset and borrower.

### ⚠️ Updated
- India now has **12 public sector banks**: SBI plus 11 nationalised banks after mergers.
- RBI's operating corridor now uses **SDF as floor**, repo as policy rate and MSF as ceiling; reverse repo is not the active floor.
- India's bad-bank idea materialised as **NARCL**, with IDRCL for resolution support.

### Prelims traps
- CRR is kept with RBI in cash; SLR is maintained by banks in liquid assets.
- Repo is borrowing by banks from RBI; reverse repo is RBI borrowing/absorbing liquidity.
- NPA is income-recognition/asset-classification based, not simply "wilful default".
- Payments banks cannot function like universal commercial banks.

### Mains angle
Banking reforms must balance financial stability, credit growth, inclusion and swift NPA resolution.

## 13. Insurance in India

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Insurance**: risk transfer mechanism where insured pays premium and insurer compensates specified loss.
- **Life insurance**: risk cover linked to life/death/survival.
- **General insurance**: non-life risk cover such as health, motor, property, crop, marine etc.
- **LIC**: public-sector life insurer.
- **GIC**: general insurance/reinsurance institution in book's institutional discussion.
- **IRDAI**: insurance regulator for licensing, solvency, product and market conduct.
- **Reinsurance**: insurer transfers part of risk to another insurer.
- **DICGC**: deposit insurance corporation for bank deposits.
- **ECGC**: export credit insurance support.
- **Insurance penetration**: premium as percentage of GDP.
- **Insurance density**: premium per capita.
- **Solvency margin**: excess of insurer's assets over liabilities required for claim-paying ability.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Insurance reforms opened the sector to private participation and regulation.
- Insurance supports savings, risk protection, long-term funds and social security.
- Crop/health/life accident schemes improve inclusion but need awareness, claim settlement and affordability.

### ⚠️ Updated
- DICGC deposit insurance cover is now **₹5 lakh per depositor per bank**.
- Insurance FDI cap has been materially liberalised since the book; current search indicates **100% FDI under automatic route** for insurance companies, while LIC remains under a separate cap.

### Prelims traps
- Insurance penetration and density are different indicators.
- Reinsurance insures the insurer; it is not direct retail insurance.
- Deposit insurance is per depositor per bank in the same right/capacity, not per account.

### Mains angle
Insurance is central to social protection, disaster resilience, health security and long-term capital formation.

## 14. Security Market in India

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Securities market**: market for financial securities such as shares, bonds, debentures and derivatives.
- **Primary market**: new issue market where issuers raise capital.
- **Secondary market**: trading of existing securities.
- **Stock exchange**: organised platform for securities trading.
- **SEBI**: securities market regulator.
- **Demutualisation**: separation of ownership, management and trading rights in an exchange.
- **IPO**: first public issue of shares.
- **Book building**: price discovery process through bids within a price band.
- **Paid-up/subscribed/issued/authorised capital**: stages of share capital permission and actual payment.
- **Greenshoe option**: over-allotment option to stabilise post-issue price.
- **Derivatives**: contracts deriving value from underlying assets; include futures/options.
- **Participatory Notes**: offshore derivative instruments used by foreign investors to gain exposure to Indian securities.
- **Hedge fund**: pooled fund using complex/high-risk strategies.
- **Short selling**: selling borrowed securities expecting price fall.
- **Bull/Bear**: rising-market/falling-market sentiment.
- **Securitisation**: pooling financial assets and issuing securities backed by cash flows.
- **REIT/InvIT**: trusts pooling investment into real estate/infrastructure assets.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- NSE/BSE are key exchanges; depositories enable dematerialised holding.
- SEBI regulates securities issuance, intermediaries, investor protection, insider trading and market conduct.
- Primary market raises funds; secondary market gives liquidity and price discovery.
- Commodity derivatives regulation shifted to SEBI after merger of FMC with SEBI (book discusses the transition context).

### Prelims traps
- Primary market proceeds go to issuer; secondary-market trades generally do not.
- Futures create obligation; options create right without obligation for buyer.
- Bullish means expectation of price rise; bearish means expectation of fall.
- Demat account holds securities; trading account executes transactions.

### Mains angle
A transparent securities market lowers capital cost, supports infrastructure/corporate finance and broadens financial inclusion.

<a id="ch15"></a>
## 15. External Sector in India

### Core concepts & definitions
- **External sector**: all foreign-currency economic activities—exports, imports, investment, debt, forex, BoP etc.
- **Forex reserves**: foreign currency assets + gold + SDRs + reserve tranche at IMF.
- **External debt**: debt owed to non-residents, classified by sovereign/non-sovereign, currency, maturity and concessionality.
- **Fixed exchange rate**: official/IMF-type fixed currency value.
- **Floating exchange rate**: value determined by market demand-supply.
- **Managed float**: market-based rate with central-bank intervention to reduce volatility.
- **Trade balance**: exports minus imports.
- **Current account**: goods, services, income, remittances/transfers; may be surplus/deficit.
- **Capital account (modern BPM6 use)**: capital transfers and acquisition or
  disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets.
- **Financial account**: FDI, portfolio investment, loans, banking capital,
  deposits and reserve-asset transactions. Older Indian texts often use
  "capital account" broadly for these financial flows; UPSC questions may test
  the distinction.
- **BoP**: current account + capital account + financial account, reconciled
  through errors/omissions and reserve changes.
- **Depreciation**: market-driven fall in domestic currency value.
- **Devaluation**: official reduction in currency value.
- **Appreciation**: market-driven rise in currency value.
- **Revaluation**: official increase in currency value.
- **Convertibility**: freedom to convert domestic currency into foreign currency for specified transactions.
- **NEER/REER**: nominal/real effective exchange rate against a basket of currencies; REER adjusts for inflation.
- **Hard/soft/hot currency**: strong widely accepted / weak / quickly moving speculative currency.
- **SEZ**: special zone with trade/investment facilitation.
- **GAAR**: anti-avoidance rule against tax-avoidance arrangements.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- India's 1991 crisis led to exchange-rate and external-sector reforms.
- LERMS/dual exchange rate was a step toward market-determined exchange rate.
- CAD sustainability depends on financing quality, reserves, external debt and growth.
- Forex reserve accumulation provides insurance but has sterilisation and opportunity costs.
- Current-account convertibility covers ordinary trade and current payments;
  capital/financial-account convertibility concerns asset transactions and is
  more restricted because volatile flows can amplify crises.
- Foreign-currency borrowing creates currency mismatch when revenue is in
  rupees; depreciation can sharply increase debt-service costs.
- Regional trade agreements can deepen market access but also create trade
  diversion, rules-of-origin complexity and adjustment pressure on domestic
  sectors.

### Prelims traps
- CAD is a current-account concept; modern BoP accounting separately records
  the current, capital and financial accounts.
- Depreciation/appreciation occur under market-determined rates; devaluation/revaluation are official.
- FDI is more stable than portfolio flows.
- A negative BoP is problematic only if financing/reserve position is weak.

### Mains angle
External-sector resilience needs export competitiveness, prudent external debt, stable capital flows and adequate reserves.

## 16. International Economic Organisations and India

### Core concepts & definitions
- **International monetary system**: rules/institutions for exchange rates, reserves, payments and adjustment.
- **Bretton Woods institutions**: IMF and World Bank system created after WWII.
- **IMF**: institution for monetary cooperation, BoP support, surveillance and financial stability.
- **Quota**: member's IMF subscription determining voting power, access to financing and SDR allocation.
- **World Bank Group**:
  - **IBRD**: loans to middle-income/creditworthy countries.
  - **IDA**: concessional support to poorer countries.
  - **IFC**: private-sector financing.
  - **MIGA**: political-risk insurance/guarantees.
  - **ICSID**: investment dispute settlement.
- **ADB**: regional development finance institution.
- **OECD**: club/forum of advanced economies and policy standards.
- **WTO**: rules-based multilateral trading system for goods, services and IP.
- **BRICS Bank/NDB**: development bank created by BRICS.
- **AIIB**: infrastructure-focused multilateral bank led by Asian economies.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- IMF system concerns: adjustment, liquidity and confidence.
- WTO agriculture issues: subsidy boxes, market access, export subsidies, SPS and special treatment.
- India uses multilateral institutions for finance, trade negotiation, development policy and global economic governance.

### Prelims traps
- World Bank is not one institution only; it is a group with distinct arms.
- IMF provides BoP/macroeconomic support; World Bank focuses on development/project/programme finance.
- WTO is rules/negotiation/dispute body, not a lending institution.
- IDA is concessional; IBRD lending is relatively harder.

### Mains angle
India's global economic diplomacy balances development space, trade interests, infrastructure finance and reform of global governance.

<a id="ch17"></a>
## 17. Tax Structure in India

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Tax**: compulsory levy; book frames it as a mode of income redistribution and public finance.
- **Incidence of tax**: point/person where tax is imposed.
- **Impact of tax**: point/person where tax burden is finally felt.
- **Direct tax**: incidence and impact on same person/entity, e.g., income tax.
- **Indirect tax**: incidence and impact differ; burden shifted to consumers, e.g., GST/excise/customs.
- **Progressive tax**: higher tax rate as income/value rises.
- **Regressive tax**: lower rate as income/value rises, burdening lower incomes relatively more.
- **Proportional tax**: same rate across income/value levels.
- **Good tax system**: fairness, efficiency, administrative simplicity, flexibility and transparency.
- **VAT**: tax on value added at each production/distribution stage with input credit.
- **GST**: destination-based indirect tax subsuming many central/state indirect taxes.
- **Securities Transaction Tax / Commodities Transaction Tax**: transaction-based levies.
- **Capital gains tax**: tax on gains from capital asset transfer.
- **MAT**: minimum alternate tax on companies with book profits but low taxable income.
- **Tax expenditure**: revenue foregone through exemptions/deductions/concessions.
- **Divisible pool**: shareable central tax pool for devolution to states.
- **Grants-in-aid**: constitutionally recommended transfers to states.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Taxes redistribute income first through collection, then through public expenditure.
- VAT/GST logic: avoid cascading by taxing value addition and allowing input tax credit.
- Finance Commission concepts in book: tax devolution, fiscal capacity, fiscal discipline and grants.
- Tax incidence is an economic outcome, not merely a legal label: market
  structure and elasticities determine how much burden can be shifted.
- The GST Council is a constitutional federal body under Article 279A; Parliament
  and state legislatures retain their respective law-making powers within the
  GST framework.

### ⚠️ Updated
- 14th Finance Commission's 42% devolution has been replaced by **15th Finance Commission's 41%** states' share for 2021-22 to 2025-26.
- GST rates are materially different from 2017-era discussion. Current search indicates GST 2.0 structure with broad slabs **0%, 5%, 18%, 40%** plus special rates such as 3%/0.25%; verify latest CBIC notification for item-level rates.

### Prelims traps
- Incidence and impact determine direct/indirect nature, not the tax name alone.
- Progressive taxation refers to rate structure, not total revenue collected.
- GST is destination-based; exports are generally zero-rated, imports taxed like domestic consumption.
- Tax expenditure is not direct spending but revenue foregone.

### Mains angle
Tax reform must raise buoyancy while protecting equity, federal balance and compliance simplicity.

<a id="ch18"></a>
## 18. Public Finance in India

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Public finance**: government income, expenditure, borrowing, lending, printing and fiscal management.
- **Budget**: Annual Financial Statement under Article 112 for Union; shows receipts and expenditure.
- **BE/RE/AE/QE/Actuals**: Budget Estimate, Revised Estimate, Advance Estimate, Quick Estimate and final actual data.
- **Revenue receipts**: receipts not creating liability or reducing assets; tax and non-tax.
- **Revenue expenditure**: expenditure not creating assets or reducing liabilities; salaries, interest, subsidies etc.
- **Revenue deficit**: revenue expenditure minus revenue receipts.
- **Effective revenue deficit**: revenue deficit minus grants for creation of capital assets.
- **Capital receipts**: receipts creating liability or reducing assets—borrowings, loan recoveries, disinvestment etc.
- **Capital expenditure**: creates assets or reduces liabilities.
- **Fiscal deficit**: total expenditure minus total receipts excluding borrowings; indicates borrowing requirement.
- **Primary deficit**: fiscal deficit minus interest payments.
- **Monetised deficit**: part of fiscal deficit financed by RBI support.
- **Deficit financing**: financing expenditure above receipts.
- **Fiscal policy**: government policy on taxation, expenditure and borrowing.
- **FRBM Act**: law for fiscal responsibility and deficit reduction.
- **Zero-based budgeting**: every expenditure justified from zero rather than incremental base.
- **Charged expenditure**: expenditure not voted by Parliament but discussed.
- **Cut motion**: parliamentary motion to reduce demand for grants.
- **Gender/outcome/performance budgeting**: budget classification by gender impact, outcomes and performance.
- **Golden rule**: borrowing should finance investment, not current consumption.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
| Deficit | Formula/meaning |
|---|---|
| Revenue deficit | Revenue expenditure - revenue receipts |
| Effective revenue deficit | Revenue deficit - grants for capital asset creation |
| Fiscal deficit | Total expenditure - non-borrowing receipts |
| Primary deficit | Fiscal deficit - interest payments |

- Deficit financing means may include external aid, external borrowing, internal borrowing and money creation; each has costs.
- Crowding out: excessive government borrowing can reduce funds for private investment.
- Fiscal consolidation aims to reduce deficit and improve composition of expenditure.

### Prelims traps
- Fiscal deficit is not simply total expenditure minus total receipts including borrowings.
- Revenue expenditure can include grants that create assets outside Union books, hence ERD.
- Primary deficit can be low even when fiscal deficit is high due to interest burden.
- Charged expenditure is not subject to vote, but can be discussed.

### Mains angle
Sound public finance is about deficit level and quality: borrowing for productive capital formation is different from borrowing for current consumption.

## 19. Sustainability and Climate Change: India and the World

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Sustainable development**: development balancing economic, social and environmental dimensions without compromising future generations.
- **Climate change**: long-term climate alteration driven significantly by anthropogenic greenhouse gases.
- **SDGs**: 17 goals and 169 targets adopted in 2015 for 2016-2030.
- **Paris Agreement/COP21**: global agreement to keep temperature rise well below 2°C and pursue 1.5°C efforts.
- **INDC/NDC**: nationally determined climate action commitments.
- **Climate finance**: financial resources for mitigation/adaptation in climate action.
- **Green finance**: finance directed toward environmentally sustainable projects.
- **GCF/GEF**: global climate/environment finance mechanisms.
- **SAPCC**: State Action Plan on Climate Change.
- **NAFCC**: National Adaptation Fund for Climate Change.
- **PAT**: Perform, Achieve and Trade mechanism for energy efficiency.
- **Carbon credit**: tradable unit representing emission reduction/avoidance.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Book stresses inequity in historical and per-capita emissions; India's per-capita emissions were much lower than developed countries in book data.
- SDGs integrate poverty, inequality, gender, health, education, sustainable cities, climate, oceans, forests and partnerships.
- India's climate policy combines development needs, renewable energy, energy efficiency, adaptation and global equity.
- Coal cess/National Clean Energy Fund, renewable energy and International Solar Alliance appear as policy tools.

### Prelims traps
- SDGs replaced MDGs; SDGs are universal, not only for developing countries.
- Climate finance and green finance overlap but are not identical.
- Mitigation reduces emissions; adaptation reduces vulnerability.
- Historical emissions and per-capita emissions can change responsibility debates.

### Mains angle
India's climate position is equity-based: pursue low-carbon growth without sacrificing poverty reduction and development space.

## 20. Human Development in India

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Human development**: expansion of people's capabilities and quality of life.
- **Inclusive growth**: growth whose benefits reach disadvantaged and marginalised sections.
- **Demographic dividend**: growth potential from rising working-age population share, conditional on health, education and jobs.
- **Poverty estimates**: measurement of population below defined poverty threshold.
- **Gender Development Index / gender issues**: development gap between women and men.
- **Social sector**: health, education, shelter, welfare, social security and related public services.
- **Skill gap**: mismatch between labour force skills and labour-market needs.
- **Labour reforms**: changes in labour laws and institutions for flexibility, security and formalisation.
- **Worker Population Ratio**: proportion of population employed/working.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Book's HDR 2015 data placed India in medium human development and flagged low schooling, gender gaps and BRICS comparison.
- Human development requires investment in social infrastructure, governance, decentralisation and accountability.
- India faces interconnected challenges: poverty, education quality, health access, employment, women's unpaid work, child labour and demographic pressure.
- Poverty measurement can be monetary or multidimensional. Headcount ratios do
  not capture depth/severity; MPI combines deprivations across health, education
  and living standards.
- Labour indicators must be distinguished: LFPR measures labour-force
  participation, WPR measures employment relative to population, and the
  unemployment rate measures unemployment as a share of the labour force.
- Open, disguised, seasonal, structural and frictional unemployment describe
  different problems and require different policy responses.

### ⚠️ Updated
- Latest searched UNDP/HDR update places India at **rank 130 of 193** with HDI value around **0.685** in the 2025 report; use latest UNDP release for exam-year numbers.

### Prelims traps
- Demographic dividend is not automatic; it requires employability and jobs.
- HDI is a composite index, not a poverty index.
- Poverty ratio and absolute number of poor may move differently.
- Social sector expenditure is an input; human development outcomes require delivery quality.

### Mains angle
India's growth story depends on converting demographic potential into human capital through health, education, skills, gender equality and jobs.

## 21. Burning Socio-Economic Issues

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Bad bank**: institution/vehicle that takes over bad loans from banks, separating stressed assets from good banking operations.
- **PARA**: proposed Public Sector Asset Rehabilitation Agency for large stressed assets/twin balance sheet problem.
- **Twin Balance Sheet crisis**: stressed corporate balance sheets plus stressed bank balance sheets, causing credit slowdown.
- **Universal healthcare**: assured access to quality healthcare, with public financing and primary-care emphasis.
- **Demonetisation aftereffects**: effects on cash, informal sector, digital payments, tax base and formalisation.
- **Inequality**: unequal distribution of income, wealth, opportunity and access.
- **Universal Basic Income (UBI)**: periodic unconditional income support to individuals/households.
- **State legitimacy**: citizens' trust in state's capacity and fairness, relevant for taxation, compliance and reform.
- **Farm indebtedness**: debt burden from institutional and non-institutional sources, linked to income volatility.
- **Deglobalisation**: retreat from open trade/global integration through protectionism, regionalism and anti-globalisation politics.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- Bad bank logic: isolate bad loans → clean bank balance sheets → restart lending; implementation challenges include valuation, governance and incentives.
- Twin balance sheet solutions include recapitalisation, restructuring, IBC-style resolution and asset rehabilitation.
- Universal healthcare requires hospitals, personnel, medicines, diagnostics, insurance, finance and preventive/promotive care.
- UBI debate: targets poverty and welfare leakage but raises fiscal, targeting, inflation and work-incentive questions.
- Farm distress remedies: credit access, income support, risk insurance, market reform, irrigation, diversification and non-farm jobs.

### ⚠️ Updated
- The bad-bank proposal has evolved into **NARCL** with IDRCL support for stressed asset resolution.

### Prelims traps
- Bad bank is not a normal commercial bank for deposits/lending.
- UBI is unconditional; targeted subsidies are conditional/means-tested.
- Demographic dividend can become demographic burden without jobs and skills.
- Deglobalisation does not mean the end of trade; it means reversal/fragmentation of liberal globalisation.

### Mains angle
These issues connect macro stability with welfare legitimacy: banking stress, inequality, health, jobs and farm distress can all weaken growth quality.

## 22. Economic Concepts and Terminologies

### Core concepts & definitions
- **Abuse of dominance**: dominant firm's anti-competitive practices such as predatory pricing or price squeezing.
- **Activity/participation rate**: labour force as percentage of population.
- **ADR/GDR**: depository receipts enabling foreign investors to invest in non-domestic company shares.
- **Adverse selection**: hidden-information problem where riskier participants are more likely to enter a transaction.
- **Amortisation**: gradual repayment/write-off of debt or intangible asset cost.
- **Arbitrage**: profit from price differences across markets.
- **Asset Reconstruction Company (ARC)**: entity acquiring and resolving stressed financial assets.
- **Autarky**: economic self-sufficiency/closed economy.
- **Badla**: carry-forward trading system historically used in Indian stock markets.
- **Balanced budget**: receipts equal expenditure.
- **Balance of payments**: record of all economic transactions with the world.
- **Bond/debenture**: debt instrument; issuer borrows and pays interest/principal.
- **Book building**: price discovery for securities issue.
- **Bracket creep**: inflation pushing taxpayers into higher tax brackets without real income gain.
- **Bubble**: asset price far above fundamentals due to speculation.
- **Capital adequacy ratio**: capital relative to risk-weighted assets.
- **Carbon credit**: tradable emission-reduction unit.
- **Ceteris paribus**: other things remaining constant.
- **Collateral**: asset pledged to secure a loan.
- **Comparative advantage**: ability to produce at lower opportunity cost.
- **Crowding-out effect**: government borrowing reducing private investment availability.
- **CSR**: corporate social responsibility.
- **Debenture**: long-term debt instrument of a company.
- **Demerger**: splitting a company/business unit.
- **Derivative**: instrument deriving value from underlying asset.
- **Dumping**: export at unfairly low price, often below normal value.
- **Dutch disease**: resource/export boom causing currency appreciation and weakening other tradable sectors.
- **Economies of scale/scope**: cost advantage from larger output / variety of products.
- **Effective revenue deficit**: revenue deficit net of grants for capital asset creation.
- **Externality**: uncompensated effect of one agent's action on another.
- **FCCB**: foreign currency convertible bond.
- **Fisher effect**: nominal interest rate reflects real rate plus expected inflation.
- **Free rider**: beneficiary who does not pay for a public good.
- **Giffen good**: inferior good whose demand rises when price rises due to strong income effect.
- **Gini coefficient**: inequality measure from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality).
- **Greenshoe option**: over-allotment price-stabilisation option.
- **Gresham's law**: bad money drives out good money when both are legal tender at fixed rate.
- **Hedge fund**: pooled fund using sophisticated strategies.
- **Inflation**: sustained general price rise.
- **Insider trading**: trading using unpublished price-sensitive information.
- **IPO**: first sale of shares to public.
- **J-curve effect**: trade balance initially worsens after depreciation before improving.
- **Junk bond**: high-risk, low-rated bond.
- **Laffer curve**: relation suggesting tax revenue may fall beyond very high tax rates.
- **Liquidity trap**: monetary policy becomes ineffective at very low interest rates.
- **Lorenz curve**: graphical representation of income/wealth distribution.
- **Moral hazard**: hidden-action problem where protection from risk encourages riskier behaviour.
- **Most Favoured Nation**: WTO non-discrimination principle.
- **Narrow banking**: bank model investing deposits only in safe/liquid assets.
- **Opportunity cost**: value of next best alternative foregone.
- **Pareto efficiency**: no one can be made better off without making someone worse off.
- **Penny stock**: low-priced, speculative share.
- **Phillips curve**: inflation-unemployment trade-off concept.
- **Predatory pricing**: pricing below cost to eliminate competitors.
- **PPP**: purchasing power parity or public-private partnership depending on context.
- **Prisoner's dilemma**: game where individual rationality leads to collectively worse outcome.
- **QIP**: qualified institutional placement.
- **Rent seeking**: gaining income through privilege/regulation rather than productive activity.
- **Reverse mortgage**: loan against house property, often for elderly income support.
- **Ricardian equivalence**: idea that debt-financed spending may not stimulate demand if people expect future taxes.
- **Seigniorage**: revenue from money creation.
- **Shadow banking**: credit intermediation outside regular banking.
- **Short selling**: sale of borrowed asset expecting price fall.
- **Solvency margin**: excess assets over liabilities for insurers.
- **Sunk cost**: cost already incurred and irrecoverable.
- **Systemic risk**: risk of breakdown of entire financial system.
- **Tobin tax**: proposed tax on financial/currency transactions.
- **Tragedy of the commons**: overuse of common resource due to individual incentives.
- **Transfer payments**: payments without current goods/services in return.
- **Veblen effect**: demand rises with price due to status appeal.
- **Velocity of circulation**: rate at which money changes hands.
- **Venture capital**: risk capital for early-stage/high-growth firms.
- **Vostro account**: domestic bank account held on behalf of foreign bank.
- **Welfare economics**: study of allocation and social welfare.
- **Zero-coupon bond**: bond issued at discount and redeemed at face value without periodic coupon.

### Key facts / classifications / mechanisms
- This chapter is a glossary: use it for precise meanings in prelims options and mains keywords.
- Many terms overlap with banking, securities, external sector, taxation and public finance chapters.

### Prelims traps
- Same abbreviation can differ by context: PPP = purchasing power parity or public-private partnership.
- Liquidity and solvency are different: liquidity is cash-flow ability; solvency is asset-liability soundness.
- Depreciation in national income differs from currency depreciation in external sector.
- Moral hazard and adverse selection are both information problems but differ by hidden action vs hidden information.

### Mains angle
Using precise economic terminology improves analytical clarity in answers on finance, markets, welfare and governance.
